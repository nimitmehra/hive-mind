## I. What Happened

### The United States announced a naval blockade of all Iranian ports — the most significant military escalation since the ceasefire began — converting Saturday's failed diplomacy into Monday's operational confrontation within hours.

CENTCOM confirmed in an official press release that the blockade takes effect today, April 13, at 10:00 AM ET (7:30 PM IST). It applies to "all maritime traffic entering and exiting Iranian ports" across the Arabian Gulf and Gulf of Oman, "enforced impartially against vessels of all nations" (source: CENTCOM press release, confirmed independently by CNBC, Bloomberg, NPR, Al Jazeera, Newsweek, Stars and Stripes). The blockade preserves "freedom of navigation for vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz to and from non-Iranian ports" — a legal distinction Iran rejects. The operation evolved directly from Saturday's mine clearing transit (Phase 1, USS Peterson and Murphy) to full port blockade (Phase 2) — the speed of the pivot from Islamabad talks to blockade suggests this was a pre-planned contingency, not an impulsive reaction. Iran's IRGC called the blockade a ceasefire violation and warned that approaching military vessels "will be dealt with severely." Iran's Zolfaghari "strongly denied" the US completed Saturday's mine clearing transit — but a WSJ-obtained IRGC radio recording of "this is the last warning" during the transit undermines Iran's denial, since a warning implies ongoing transit, not reversal (source: WSJ, Fortune). The ceasefire technically runs to ~April 22, but its survival now depends on whether the blockade triggers a kinetic incident at sea.

### Brent surged 7.4% to $102.20 in a single session — the largest move since the ceasefire announcement — destroying the five-day sub-$100 count and every deployment signal built on ceasefire optimism.

The oil complex moved in lockstep: WTI +8.4% to $104.73, heating oil +8.7% to $4.09/gal (source: yfinance, confirmed). Heating oil's outperformance over crude is a diagnostic signal — refined products are under tighter supply stress than crude itself, meaning refining capacity is constrained beyond just crude supply. The physical-futures gap persists: dated Brent (physical market) remains at $120+ while futures are at $102. OPEC+'s planned 206K bpd May increase is structurally irrelevant — most Gulf barrels are stranded behind Hormuz regardless of production decisions. Goldman Sachs' ceasefire-optimistic $90 Q2 Brent forecast, published April 9, is already obsolete four days later. JP Morgan's pre-blockade assessment: Brent hits $120 if the stalemate drags to July, with a worst case of $150 if Hormuz stays shut through mid-May (source: Bloomberg). Bob McNally of Rapidan Energy projected commercial escort operations by late April — the blockade appears to be a step on that escalation path.
