## I. What Happened

### Israel struck Iran's South Pars and Mahshahr petrochemical complexes — halting half the country's petrochemical output — and killed IRGC Intelligence Chief Khademi in the most significant decapitation since Tangsiri.

Israel's first sustained campaign against Iran's energy infrastructure hit South Pars — the country's largest petrochemical plant, responsible for half of output and linked to the gas field shared with Qatar — and Mahshahr, where destruction of utility plants cut off 50+ downstream facilities (source: Euronews, i24News, confirmed by Defense Minister Katz). A separate strike killed Brigadier General Majid Khademi, the IRGC intelligence chief, along with Quds Force commander Bagheri and two senior officers — cumulative leadership losses now span the Navy chief, intelligence chief, and multiple Quds Force commanders (source: Xinhua, Fox News; confirmed by Tasnim). The IRGC responded with a "New Persian Gulf Order" declaration that Hormuz will "never return to its former state," and designated 18 Western technology companies — Apple, Nvidia, Tesla, JPMorgan among them — as "legitimate targets," a horizontal escalation into civilian technology infrastructure (source: CNBC, PressTV). The South Pars strike sets a precedent: hitting infrastructure linked to a gas field shared with Qatar puts every Gulf energy asset within the expanding target matrix.

### WTI crude inverted above Brent for the first time in this war — $114.98 versus $111.15 — while 32 supertankers stack off Yanbu and Saudi Arabia cuts April allocations.

The WTI premium over Brent is structurally abnormal — Brent historically trades above WTI as the international benchmark — and signals that Middle Eastern crude trapped behind Hormuz is being discounted as semi-stranded by the physical market (source: OilPriceAPI, Fortune, Financial Times). At Yanbu, Saudi Arabia's Red Sea bypass port, 32 supertankers are now queued (up from 27); March handled 47 VLCC loadings against a pre-war average of 11-12, but effective throughput of 4 million bpd cannot keep pace with the pipeline's 7 million bpd capacity, and Saudi Arabia has notified customers that April allocations will be reduced (source: maritime data, ENR, New Arab). OPEC+'s 206,000 bpd May increase is paper only — the Yanbu bottleneck prevents physical delivery. ENR's assessment: "Hormuz bypass infrastructure was sized for a short disruption. This is not that."

### IDF Northern Command admitted it overestimated damage to Hezbollah — calling the gap "significant" — and shifted its Lebanon objective from disarming the group to a 2-3 km security zone.

Maj. Gen. Rafi Milo acknowledged that Hezbollah retains hundreds of launchers and tens of thousands of rockets despite the 2024 ground offensive — a revelation that undermines the narrative of decisive degradation (source: Times of Israel, Haaretz). Haaretz reported the IDF is "stretched to breaking point" and faces a "familiar deadly stalemate," with the strategic reframe conceding that disarming Hezbollah would require occupying all of Lebanon and is "unrealistic" (source: Haaretz, FDD Long War Journal). Since March 2, Hezbollah has fired 210+ missiles into Israel — roughly 100 during Passover alone — and four Nahal Brigade soldiers were recently killed in southern Lebanon. The two-front war is grinding: tactical gains against Iran continue but the Lebanon front consumes troops and ammunition without producing the strategic outcomes claimed.

### Trump's Tuesday deadline arrives tonight with Iran's ceasefire rejection in hand and Hegseth pre-announcing "the most intense day of strikes since Day One."

The deadline flagged in yesterday's brief — 8 PM ET Tuesday (5:30 AM IST Wednesday) — arrives with both sides hardened. Trump escalated to the war's most specific threat: "Every bridge in Iran will be decimated by 12 o'clock tomorrow night, every power plant... complete demolition... over four hours" (source: NBC News, PBS). Iran submitted a 10-clause counter-proposal through Pakistan demanding permanent war end, sanctions relief, reconstruction reparations, and a Hormuz protocol — conditions Washington considers maximalist; Trump called it "not good enough, but a very significant step," hedging language that keeps the door cracked (source: Axios, IRNA, Fox News). This is the fourth extension cycle — 48 hours to 5 days to 10 days to 20 hours — and the first where both sides have publicly committed to structurally incompatible positions. Separately, Fars News reported 6 children under 10 killed in overnight airstrikes on a Tehran residential area (REPORTED — not independently confirmed); cumulative Iranian casualties stand at 3,540+ killed per Al Jazeera's tracker.

### Urea hit $700/mt in Egypt and $800/mt in the US Gulf — with 30% of globally traded supply offline, no strategic reserve, and the Northern Hemisphere planting window narrowing.

Qatar halted one of the world's largest urea plants after Hormuz closed its export route; combined with disruptions to Saudi, Bahraini, and Iranian exports, roughly 30% of globally traded supply is offline (source: QatarEnergy, WEF, Anadolu Agency). Unlike oil, which has the SPR and OPEC+ spare capacity, fertilizer has no strategic reserve and no quick substitute — if this crisis extends through May, the 2026 planting season takes permanent yield damage, feeding a food inflation cascade through to consumer prices (source: Carnegie, AgWeb). CF Industries, the largest US nitrogen producer, is up 69.9% in three months to $130.98; the market is pricing the cascade but may still be underpriced if Hormuz stays shut through planting.

### UAE fuel prices surged 31% (petrol) and 72% (diesel) for April — the first consumer-facing war inflation in a Gulf oil-producing state — while Indian markets rallied into the war's densest binary week.

The UAE paradox: one of the world's largest oil exporters now paying Dh3.39/litre for petrol (from Dh2.59), with the government announcing Dh900 inflation allowances. Presidential adviser Gargash declared Hormuz freedom "non-negotiable" — the strongest Gulf positioning of the war (source: Khaleej Times, The National). In India, markets reopened after a 3-day closure and rallied on ceasefire hopes: Nifty +1.12% to 22,968, Bank Nifty +2.1% — reversing the 350-point GIFT Nifty drop flagged yesterday — but face three sequential binaries: Trump deadline (tonight), RBI MPC decision (Wednesday, consensus hold at 5.25%), and NOP cap deadline (Friday April 10, $11-15B still unwinding) (source: Yahoo Finance, BusinessToday, Reuters poll 69/71 expect hold). FPI outflows hit Rs 23,801 crore in early April; cumulative 2026 total of Rs 1.5 trillion is the worst since 1992.
