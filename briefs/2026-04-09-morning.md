## I. What Happened

### Pakistan's 14-day ceasefire fractured within 18 hours — Israel launched the deadliest day of the Lebanon war, Iran re-closed Hormuz, and five Gulf states came under missile attack during the truce.

Three simultaneous crises erupted on what was supposed to be Day 1 of peace. Israel launched "Operation Eternal Darkness" — 50 IAF jets striking 100 Hezbollah targets across Lebanon in 10 minutes, killing at least 254 and wounding 1,165, including strikes on central Beirut WITHOUT prior warning — the single deadliest day of the 2026 Lebanon war (source: Al Jazeera, Times of Israel, Middle East Eye, UN News). Netanyahu stated the ceasefire "does not include Lebanon"; Trump called it a "separate skirmish" (source: PBS, Fox News, NBC). Iran re-closed the Strait of Hormuz, citing Israeli strikes as a ceasefire violation — Bloomberg ship-tracking data shows only 3-4 ships transited on April 8-9, down from 11 on Tuesday, with 479 vessels stranded including 426 tankers; White House Press Secretary Leavitt called the re-closure "false," directly contradicted by Bloomberg's operational data (source: Bloomberg, CNBC, CBS News). Iran's Lavan Island oil refinery was also struck after the ceasefire took effect — UAE Mirage 2000-9 jets suspected, though Abu Dhabi has not confirmed or denied (source: Al Jazeera, Press TV, Turkiye Today). Within approximately one hour, Iran launched retaliatory missile and drone attacks on five Gulf states: UAE intercepted 17 ballistic missiles and 35 drones, Saudi Arabia 5 missiles and 9 drones with its East-West Pipeline pumping station struck, Bahrain 6 missiles and 31 drones, Qatar 7 missiles/drones, and Kuwait 28 drones (source: Al Jazeera, Gulf News, The National, CNBC). Pakistan PM Sharif contradicted both Trump and Netanyahu, insisting the ceasefire covers "everywhere including Lebanon" (source: NBC, CBS) — creating a three-way contradiction on the deal's scope that is Friday Islamabad's first fault line.

### Brent futures at $97 say "peace" — physical crude at $120+ says "prove it," creating the widest futures-physical disconnect since 2008.

Brent settled at $96.75 on ceasefire day (-11.5%, the biggest single-session drop since the 1991 Gulf War), then edged to $96.61 on Day 2 as initial euphoria faded (source: Trading Economics, CNBC, Fortune). But the physical market violently disagrees: dated Brent spot trades above $120, Dubai physical at $126-140 per EBC Financial Group — a $24-44 gap that didn't exist pre-war and is the widest since 2008 (source: CNBC, S&P Global). Amrita Sen of Energy Aspects calls futures a "false sense of security... masking the true tightness." The disconnect reflects Hormuz reality: only 3-4 daily transits versus 100-120 pre-war, 40+ VLCCs waiting at Yanbu with 5-day delays, and war risk insurance still 7-10x pre-war even after dipping post-ceasefire to 0.35-0.45% of vessel value (source: Bloomberg, Lloyd's List, TT News). CBS News estimates oil exports return to previous levels by July 2026 at the earliest — the physical market's timeline, not the paper market's. Jason Schenker of Prestige Economics: "Almost anything going wrong in these talks could very quickly put us back above $100."

### Governor Malhotra resolved the brief's three unknowns — CPI below consensus, GDP above consensus, NOP cap standing — then delivered the most dovish forward guidance of his tenure, opening a June rate cut pathway.

The 12 noon press conference closed every gap flagged in yesterday's brief: FY27 CPI at 4.6% came below the 4.7-5.1% consensus (Brent at $94 versus the RBI's $85 baseline narrowed the inflation overshoot); FY27 GDP at 6.9% beat the 6.5-6.8% range; and the NOP cap April 10 deadline was confirmed standing, though Malhotra called curbs "temporary" and "reactive" — banks have already unwound ~75% ($30B of ~$40B) (source: Upstox, BusinessToday, Bloomberg, The Print, MarketScreener). The key line: "interest rates are likely to remain low in the medium to long term." The vote was 6-0 unanimous on hold at 5.25%, with Ram Singh dissenting only on stance (pushing for accommodative over neutral). If Brent stays below $100, a June 25bps cut is now on the table — the ceasefire's most durable Indian market legacy.

### Sensex surged 3.95% for its biggest single-day gain in six years — but foreign institutional investors sold Rs 8,692 crore INTO the rally, exposing the move as short-covering, not conviction.

Nifty 50 closed at 24,012.95 (+3.85%), touching the 200-day moving average at ~24,051 intraday; Sensex gained 2,946 points to 77,563; Bank Nifty led at +5.0%; all 50 Nifty constituents closed green; and India VIX crashed 20% to 19.70 — pre-war levels (source: Upstox, Moolaah, GoodReturns, Bloomberg). But FIIs sold Rs 8,692 crore on ceasefire day, following -Rs 8,336 crore on April 7, bringing April sales to Rs 37,944 crore; DIIs absorbed Rs 7,979 crore (source: Trendlyne, NSE/NSDL). The rally was driven by massive short-covering and domestic institutional buying, not FII re-entry. India 10Y GOI collapsed 12 basis points to 6.91% — the biggest single-session move in months, back below 7% for the first time in weeks (source: MarketScreener, TradingEconomics). The FII flow flip remains the next structural tell: until foreign money buys INTO India rather than sells into the rally, the 24,000 level is vulnerable to a single bad headline from Islamabad.

### Defence stocks rose 4.0% on a ceasefire day — the non-reversal that proves the market now sees a permanent geopolitical regime change, not a temporary war trade.

The ITA defence ETF gained +4.0% on the day markets were supposed to sell war and buy peace — an anomaly explained by the $1.5 trillion FY2027 defence budget conversation in Congress, which has decoupled defence spending from any single conflict's outcome (source: 24/7 Wall St, CNBC). Defence is now 4.2% of S&P 500 market capitalisation, up from 2.8% pre-war. Lockheed Martin barely moved (+0.1%), suggesting defence primes are fully priced; the sector rally was driven by second-tier names catching up. For the investor: even if Friday Islamabad succeeds and the ceasefire extends, defence remains a structural trade — the floor is the budget, not the war.

### Iran's state media alignment broke within 18 hours — IRNA preserves the ceasefire while Tasnim builds the IRGC's case to exit it, a divergence that reveals the civilian-military split.

Yesterday's brief flagged IRNA-Tasnim alignment as evidence the IRGC accepted the deal. That alignment fractured overnight: IRNA (civilian government-linked) pivoted to violation framing that preserves the ceasefire — "we won the deal, and they are breaking it" — while Tasnim (IRGC-linked) cited anonymous sources "assessing the possibility of exiting the deal," and the IRGC's own Sepah News channel issued a "regret-inducing response" threat (source: IRNA, Tasnim, MEMRI monitoring). Reports suggest President Pezeshkian confronted IRGC commander Hossein Taeb on April 4, accusing the IRGC of "acting unilaterally" and having "destroyed any remaining chance of a ceasefire" — though this comes from a single source, Iran International, a diaspora outlet with credible IRGC sourcing (source: Iran International, Ynet News). The IRGC's Aerospace Force separately issued what MEMRI described as "emergency, exceptional instructions for unprecedented retaliation against Israel." The divergence pattern is itself intelligence: IRNA's framing gives the civilian government space to negotiate at Friday Islamabad; Tasnim's framing gives the IRGC a pre-built justification to exit if the talks fail.
