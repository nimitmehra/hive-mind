## I. What Happened

### The Houthis compressed their escalation timeline to hours, not weeks — launching two missile attacks on Israel within 24 hours and invalidating analyst predictions of gradual spacing.

Yemen's Houthis launched their first attack on Israel on Saturday morning — a ballistic missile at Beersheba, intercepted by IDF air defenses, no casualties — and then launched a second missile-and-drone salvo less than 24 hours later, targeting what spokesman Yahya Saree described as "sensitive Israeli military sites" (sources: IDF confirmed both interceptions; Saree claimed responsibility via Al-Masirah TV, declaring "operations will continue in coming days"; AP, Al Jazeera, Bloomberg, SBS News independently confirmed). Michael Knights' prediction of 5-10 day spacing between escalation steps was invalidated within the first day. CENTCOM struck Houthi positions in Yemen in response. The critical distinction remains: both attacks targeted Israel, NOT Red Sea shipping — the double-chokepoint scenario (Hormuz + Red Sea both closed) has not materialized. But the Houthis have demonstrated a tempo that suggests Red Sea shipping attacks could come faster than previously assessed, potentially within days.

### Iran struck a Saudi air base injuring 15+ US troops, hit a Tel Aviv commercial street killing one civilian, and fired sirens across three countries — demonstrating sustained multi-front capability a month into the war.

Iran fired 6 ballistic missiles and 29 drones at Prince Sultan Air Base outside Riyadh on Friday, injuring at least 15 US service members including 5 seriously, and damaging aircraft (sources: NPR citing US official, Fortune, Al Jazeera, PBS, Washington Post, Stars and Stripes independently confirmed). Total US wounded in the war has now surpassed 300. Separately, an Iranian missile salvo struck a busy commercial street in Tel Aviv, killing Vyacheslav Vidmant, 52, and injuring at least 11 others, with 8 impact sites across the city including a university (sources: Times of Israel, Al Jazeera, CNN). Early Sunday, air raid sirens activated simultaneously across Kuwait, Bahrain, and Israel — the broadest regional bombardment pattern of the war (sources: Middle East Eye, Bahrain Interior Ministry, Kuwait Defense Ministry). One month into the conflict, CENTCOM's 10,000+ strikes and claimed destruction of two-thirds of Iran's arms manufacturing have not eliminated Tehran's ability to project force across multiple countries simultaneously.

### India's rupee breached ₹95/USD — activating the graph's trigger point — as the RBI imposed emergency position caps and forex reserves fell below $700 billion for the first time since the war began.

The RBI reference rate closed at ₹95.09/USD on Friday March 27, confirmed by Business Standard, Bloomberg, and The Week — breaching the ₹95 trigger that Goldman Sachs forecast just 9 days earlier on March 18 (source: Goldman Sachs research note). The RBI responded within 24 hours by imposing a Net Open Position (NOP-INR) hard cap of $100 million on ALL authorized dealer banks regardless of balance sheet size, with an April 10 compliance deadline — preventing banks from building large short-rupee positions (source: Business Standard, Bloomberg). This is explicitly an anti-speculation measure and represents an escalation from conventional intervention (selling dollars) to regulatory intervention. Forex reserves dropped $30.14 billion in three weeks of March to $698.35 billion, falling below the $700 billion mark from a pre-war high of $728.49 billion on February 27 (source: RBI data via Business Standard). FPI outflows crossed $11.5 billion in March alone, with 2026 total past ₹1 trillion. The escalation ladder — conventional intervention → regulatory caps → full capital controls — has moved to step two. Emergency fuel duty cuts (petrol from ₹13 to ₹3/liter, diesel duty eliminated entirely) represent $15-20 billion in annual fiscal cost. Nifty closed at 22,820 (-2.1%), Sensex at 73,583 (-2.2%) on Friday; Indian markets were closed Saturday.

### Gold bounced 2.7% after its worst weekly rout in 43 years — a potential regime shift signal, but one session within a violent correction does not yet confirm it.

Gold surged 2.7% to $4,492 on Friday — its strongest single-session gain since the war began — but this must be read against the week's context: gold suffered its worst weekly decline since 1983, falling ~11% in five sessions and now down ~20% from January's all-time high of $5,599 (sources: yfinance, Global Times, CBS News). The paradox that defined March was gold FALLING during a war: oil-driven inflation pushed real yields higher (US 10Y rose to 4.44%), and the dollar strengthened, both of which compete with gold. That correlation broke Friday. Gold rose alongside oil while equities fell — the classic "systemic crisis" pattern where fear overwhelms real-yield math. But whether Friday was a genuine regime shift or a dead-cat bounce within the worst selloff in four decades remains unresolved. Turkey's sale of 60 tonnes of gold ($8 billion) to defend the lira is paradoxically itself a signal — central banks liquidating gold under stress confirms its role as the ultimate reserve asset. The test: if gold sustains above $4,600 for 3+ sessions, the regime shift from "inflation event" (temporary, reversible) to "systemic crisis" (extended, cascading) is confirmed. One session is not enough. JP Morgan targets $6,300 by year-end.

### European TTF gas surged 70% in a single month as Spain, Italy, Slovakia, and Slovenia enacted emergency energy measures — the worst monthly gain since September 2021 and the beginning of what the Atlantic Council is calling a potential 2022-level disruption.

The Dutch TTF benchmark hit €54.52/MWh, up from €38 at the start of March, driven by the destruction of Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility (17% of Qatar's capacity, 5-year repair timeline) and the Hormuz blockade preventing remaining LNG transits (sources: OilPriceAPI, Euronews, S&P Global). European gas storage sits at 30% capacity — Dutch storage at a critical 6.0% — the lowest at this season since 2022, with less buffer than when TTF peaked above €300/MWh. Spain slashed energy VAT from 21% to 10%; Italy allocated €100 million for industrial support; Slovakia and Slovenia introduced fuel rationing at the pump; France removed winter diesel requirements (sources: Euronews, IRU). The ECB postponed its planned rate cut on March 19, joining the Fed and Bank of England in an unprecedented simultaneous monetary freeze — three major central banks all trapped by a single geopolitical event. The Atlantic Council published "How the Iran war could trigger a European energy crisis," framing this as potentially 2022-level (source: Atlantic Council, Bloomberg). Goldman base case: TTF at €72/MWh in Q2.

### Iran's IRGC-linked Tasnim agency reported that Tehran sent a formal ceasefire response through intermediaries — contradicting Iran's public "no negotiations" stance and revealing a dual-track posture as the April 6 deadline approaches.

The semi-official Tasnim News Agency — linked to the IRGC, not the civilian government — reported that Iran's response to the US 15-point ceasefire proposal was "officially sent last night through intermediaries" and that Iran is "awaiting the other side's reply" (sources: Tasnim, carried verbatim by CGTN; Al Arabiya independently confirmed an Iranian official called the proposal "one-sided and unfair"). This directly contradicts Iran's public posture of zero engagement, and the fact that Tasnim — not a foreign outlet — reported it means the IRGC apparatus acknowledges the channel exists. The gap remains enormous: the US demands nuclear rollback and Hormuz reopening; Iran demands sovereignty over Hormuz, reparations, and no-recurrence guarantees. Pakistan's FM Dar continues to publicly confirm his relay role. VP Vance stated the US has "accomplished the vast majority of military projects" and the war will continue "a little while longer" to ensure Iran is "neutered" — language that simultaneously signals near-completion and indefinite extension (source: Times of Israel, Benny Show podcast). The US also acknowledged the war "may extend past initial 4 to 6 week timeline" (source: Times of Israel).

### The IRGC threatened US university campuses across the Gulf by Monday noon, while 2,500 Marines arrived aboard the USS Tripoli and Trump declared the US "no longer needs NATO" — a single day that encapsulated both escalation and isolation.

The IRGC demanded the US condemn strikes on two Iranian universities by 12:00 noon Monday Tehran time, or face retaliation against US university campuses in the Gulf — specifically naming Texas A&M in Qatar and NYU Abu Dhabi, and warning personnel to stay one kilometer away (sources: Times of Israel liveblog, Manila Times, Iran International). This creates a testable deadline: follow-through or bluff becomes intelligence about IRGC's escalation posture within hours. Simultaneously, the USS Tripoli, carrying 2,500 Marines from the Japan-based 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit, arrived in the Middle East — an amphibious assault ship being precisely the asset you'd pre-position for an island seizure, and Axios previously reported Kharg Island invasion is under "serious consideration" (sources: CENTCOM, Time, Military.com, Stars and Stripes). At a Saudi-sponsored investment conference in Miami, Trump called NATO "cowards" and a "paper tiger" for refusing to escort ships through Hormuz, stating the US "no longer needs NATO" (sources: Bloomberg, Washington Post, NPR). The implication: any US attempt to force open Hormuz proceeds without European naval support.

### Qatar's helium shutdown has put South Korean chip fabs on a countdown to early April — with inventories exhausting in roughly 8 days, KOSPI has already suffered a triple cascade losing 10.6% this month.

Qatar produces 30% of global helium, and South Korea imported 64.7% of its helium from Qatar in 2025 (source: Korea International Trade Association). When strikes destroyed Ras Laffan's facilities on March 18-19, helium exports stopped. Chip fabs use helium for wafer cooling and purging — there is no strategic stockpile, and TrendForce estimates existing inventories sustain Asian fab operations only through early April (source: TrendForce, Tom's Hardware). KOSPI's -10.6% monthly decline reflects a triple cascade: helium supply disruption (~40%), Google's TurboQuant AI memory compression paper reducing capex expectations (~25%), and foreign capital flight of 51.4 trillion won ($37.8 billion) since February — the largest since the Global Financial Crisis (source: TrendForce, Nikkei Asia, CNBC). Samsung's foreign ownership hit 48.9%, a 12.5-year low, while the won fell to 1,508/USD, a 17-year low. This is a multi-hop cascade most coverage misses: war → Qatar → helium → semiconductors → KOSPI → global tech supply chains.

### Hezbollah intensified to 45+ military operations in a single day as Israel expanded its Lebanon ground invasion — the most active ground combat theater of the war, with 1,189 killed and nearly one million displaced.

Hezbollah announced more than 45 operations against Israel on Thursday alone, including rockets, drones, and targeting of Israeli troops in southern Lebanon (sources: Al Jazeera, Defense News, Times of Israel). Israel sent additional troops in and struck Beirut, killing two top members of Hezbollah's communications unit overnight March 28 (source: IDF confirmed). At least 1,189 people have been killed in Lebanon since March 2, including 124 children, with nearly one million displaced — 20% of the country's population (sources: Al Jazeera citing Lebanese health authorities). Israel now faces sustained bombardment on three fronts: Iranian missiles directly, Houthi missiles from Yemen, and Hezbollah rockets and drones from Lebanon.

### VLCC tanker rates hit 50-year highs while war risk insurance costs created a de facto insurance blockade — splitting global shipping into two tiers and stranding Western tanker fleets.

VLCC day rates on the Middle East Gulf-to-China route reached $401,000-$445,000/day, with confirmed spot fixtures at $538,000 and even $770,000/day — the highest in at least 50 years (sources: Baltic Exchange, Seatrade Maritime, Lloyd's List). Simultaneously, marine war risk insurance surged from 0.25% to 1-7.5% of hull value, meaning a $200-300 million VLCC faces $2-15 million in extra insurance per transit — if insurers write the policy at all (sources: Lloyd's List, S&P Global). The result is a bifurcated market: Western operators locked out by insurance costs, while IRGC-tolled passages at $2 million per transit in yuan serve Chinese and non-Western vessels. Frontline, the bellwether tanker stock, barely moved (+0.2%) despite record rates — its fleet is stranded on the wrong side of the insurance barrier. The Hormuz toll system continues operating at 142 transits March 1-25 versus a 2,652 baseline (95% reduction), with CNN framing it as a potential permanent revenue stream aligned with Iran's ceasefire demand for "sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz" (sources: USNI News, CNN, Lloyd's List).
