## What Changed Since Morning

### The IDF launched the "most intense day of strikes since Day One" — three Tehran airports, Shiraz's last nitric-acid plant, bridges near Qom, two South Pars power units — while SABIC's Jubail complex caught fire from ballistic missile debris, marking the most significant Saudi energy infrastructure damage of the war.

This morning's brief noted IDF strike tempo was accelerating. Since then, Israel executed what Hegseth called the heaviest single-day bombardment of the 39-day war: 150+ aircraft hitting dozens of infrastructure targets, three Tehran airports struck with aircraft and helicopters on the tarmac, the Shiraz Marvdasht petrochemical complex destroyed — one of Iran's few remaining nitric-acid facilities for ballistic missiles — and a Persian-language railway warning issued to all Iranians signalling imminent rail infrastructure strikes (source: ABC News, Al Jazeera, PBS NewsHour, NBC News, Times of Israel). CENTCOM's running total: 13,000+ Iranian targets struck since February 28. Netanyahu claimed 70% of Iran's steel production destroyed. Meanwhile at Jubail, debris from seven Saudi-intercepted ballistic missiles ignited fires at SABIC plants in Saudi Arabia's largest industrial city — 150+ plants, 7-12% of Saudi GDP, roughly half of FDI in the kingdom. Evacuations were ordered and operations suspended to prevent chain reactions (source: Arab News, SPA/GlobalSecurity, Upstream, Insurance Journal). Rystad Energy estimated Gulf-wide repair costs at minimum $25 billion. No confirmed Aramco crude-processing damage — petrochemical is not crude — but the psychological barrier between "successful interception" and "industrial damage" has been crossed. King Fahd Causeway, the 25km bridge linking Bahrain to Saudi Arabia, was closed indefinitely on the same day (source: Haaretz). Combined with Bapco's total loss, Bahrain is now effectively isolated. Separately, IDF strikes destroyed the Rafi-Nia synagogue in Tehran — Iran's remaining Jewish community's primary worship site, with Hebrew books scattered in rubble — which Iran will leverage for propaganda value internationally (source: Al Jazeera, Middle East Eye, Iranian state media).

### Indian markets erased Sunday's ceasefire rally — Nifty -0.51%, Bank Nifty -2.0% — as Goldman raised US recession probability to 30%, Moody's to 48.6%, and heating oil surged 5.3% on a distillate supply crisis distinct from crude.

The Axios-driven rebound this morning's brief flagged as "fragile and easily reversible" reversed exactly as expected. Nifty fell to an intraday low of 22,719 before recovering to close at approximately 22,851 (-0.51%); Bank Nifty was the worst sector at roughly 51,549 (-2.0% from 52,609). Only 14 of 50 Nifty stocks closed green. FIIs sold Rs 8,167 crore on April 6; DIIs bought Rs 8,000 crore, keeping the tug-of-war alive (source: Upstox, IBTimes India, 5Paisa). India VIX rose to 25.47 (+5.2%). The RBI MPC Day 2 deliberations proceeded normally — decision at 10 AM IST tomorrow — with hold consensus at 69/71, but BusinessToday raised the framing question of a rate HIKE in FY27, reflecting the squeeze of a $120.84/bbl crude basket sitting 46% above the RBI's $85 assumption. Globally, Goldman raised US recession probability to 30% (third upward revision in a month), Moody's Mark Zandi put it at 48.6% with a specific trigger — Brent $125 Q2 average equals recession, currently $14 away — and EY-Parthenon at 40% (source: Goldman Sachs, Fortune, Moody's). The single most important market signal was NOT crude: heating oil surged +5.3% to $4.56/gallon while Brent rose only +0.9%. The distillate supply crisis — Hormuz blocks 500,000 barrels per day of Middle Eastern diesel exports, EIA inventories drew 3.5x expectations — is a distinct cascade from crude pricing, with lower reversibility even under ceasefire because refinery capacity cannot restart quickly (source: EIA, Lloyd's List). VIX jumped 6% to 25.63 but remains massively mispriced for tonight's binary: it implies 8-10% probability of a 5%+ S&P move in 10 days when the actual probability is 25-35%.

### ISW assessed that an IRGC military council has assumed "core decision-making power" in Iran — corroborated by a Times of Israel report that Mojtaba Khamenei is unconscious in Qom — while NATO allies moved to their sharpest break with Washington yet.

ISW/Critical Threats, among the most credible Western analytical sources on Iranian military structures, assessed that a military council of senior IRGC officers has assumed core decision-making in Iran. This corroborates — and is corroborated by — a separate Times of Israel report citing a diplomatic memo (US/Israeli intelligence basis) claiming Mojtaba Khamenei is "unconscious and receiving treatment in Qom in severe condition, unable to govern" (source: ISW/Critical Threats, Times of Israel, War on the Rocks). HOWEVER: the incapacitation report is single-source with adversarial intelligence basis — both the US and Israel have incentive to portray Iranian leadership as collapsing. What IS confirmed: Day 39 with zero audio or video appearances, and today's Khademi martyrdom statement was again text-only. If the IRGC is governing by military council, the implication for the deadline is significant: military councils are less likely to make political concessions than civilian leaders, meaning the "deal by tonight" scenario is even less probable. In Iranian state media, IRNA and Tasnim are aligned today — both defiant — unlike the IRNA-softens/Tasnim-aggressive divergence that preceded past de-escalation signals. Alignment is escalatory intelligence (source: IRNA, Tasnim, Press TV). Meanwhile, NATO fractures widened to their most visible point: Spain closed airspace entirely, Italy denied Sicily base access, France blocked Israel-bound overflight, Germany declared "not our war," and Macron called April 6 strikes "outside international law" — the sharpest allied criticism of the war (source: WSJ, The Hill, Al Jazeera, CNN). The IRGC also threatened the $500 billion Stargate AI data center in Abu Dhabi and broadened the threat to Amazon, Oracle, Nvidia, Apple, Google, Microsoft, and Tesla facilities across the Gulf — a new escalation category targeting civilian tech infrastructure, though markets ignored earlier IRGC corporate threats on April 1 (source: NewsX, Defence Security Asia, Tom's Hardware).
