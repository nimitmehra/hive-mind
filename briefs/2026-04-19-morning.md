## I. What Happened

### Iran's Supreme National Security Council formally ratified the IRGC's Hormuz closure, collapsing the three-way factional split for the first time since March.
The SNSC — the civilian apex body that by statute outranks both the IRGC and the Foreign Ministry, chaired by President Pezeshkian — issued a late-Saturday statement, read on state broadcaster IRIB and carried as the lead item on Tasnim, Press TV, and WANA, declaring Iran is "determined to enforce monitoring and control over transit through the Strait of Hormuz until the definitive end of the war and the establishment of lasting peace" (sources: Press TV, Tasnim, Washington Examiner, Al Jazeera — CONFIRMED via four independent chains). The statement operationalises what Khatam al-Anbiya declared Saturday morning: transit certificates, $1/bbl fees on loaded tankers, and explicit denial of passage to US-flagged and Israeli-linked vessels. Araghchi's Friday "completely open" framework is suspended until the US lifts its blockade. CENTCOM has issued no specific response 16–18 hours after the UKMTO reports of Saturday's gunboat fire, and Araghchi himself — per Al Jazeera — is "currently reviewing" new US proposals: the Foreign Ministry has not been institutionally removed, but its bargaining authority is visibly circumscribed. For the crisis calendar, the significance is structural: the civilian-IRGC-Supreme-Leader divergence that the graph's `iran` node tracked through early April is operationally closed, with the civilian wing endorsing the hardline direction rather than resisting it.

### India summoned Iran's ambassador on Saturday evening — the first formal India-Iran diplomatic friction in the 52 days of this crisis.
India's Ministry of External Affairs called in Iranian Ambassador Mohammad Fathali for a meeting with Foreign Secretary Vikram Misri, who conveyed "deep concern at the shooting incident involving two Indian-flagged ships in the Strait of Hormuz" and named the VLCC *Sanmar Herald* (carrying roughly 2M bbl of Iraqi crude, bridge window damaged under direct fire) and the tanker *Jag Arnav*, forced to retreat westward (sources: The Wire, Tribune India, NewsX, The Federal — CONFIRMED via four Indian outlets). Misri reminded Fathali that Tehran had earlier facilitated safe passage of India-bound ships and urged it to resume that practice; Fathali "undertook to convey these views to Tehran." India had preserved strategic-autonomy silence through the March bombing campaign, the Khamenei elevation, the US blockade announcement, and every prior Hormuz escalation; the summons is the first explicit negative-diplomatic signal of the crisis. The framing is deliberately narrow — "deep concern," not "condemns"; urging resumption of facilitation rather than cessation of harassment — preserving India's lane to restart. But coming twenty-four hours before the Bessent waiver expires, and with Reliance having already rejected the Iranian cargoes *Derya* and *Lenore* on compliance grounds, India's physical and diplomatic posture toward Tehran has simultaneously tightened along every available axis.

### The Hormuz kinetic broadened from tanker-only to container ship and a cruise liner within twenty-four hours — REPORTED, single-chain, but widening the target set.
Beyond Saturday morning's IRGC fast-boat fire on *Jag Arnav* and *Sanmar Herald*, UKMTO and the UK Ministry of Defence separately reported an unidentified container ship damaged by an "unknown projectile" in the same Strait approach, with visible container damage (sources: Military Times, Al Jazeera citing UKMTO — REPORTED). A projectile is operationally distinct from warning-shot small arms: rocket, missile, or drone widens the escalation ladder from nuisance to standoff munition. Separately, Malta-flagged cruise liner *Mein Schiff 4* logged a "nearby splash" off Oman and its master reported an IRGC VHF threat to "fire at and destroy the ship" (Wikipedia 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis timeline citing UKMTO advisory traffic — REPORTED, master-report only, no photographic corroboration). Cruise-vessel targeting, if confirmed, would trigger an IMO-level maritime emergency rather than a purely commercial insurance response.
