## What Changed Since Morning

### This morning's two lead stories — the Brent pullback and the rupee rally — were both FALSE DAWNS that reversed completely by afternoon, driven by Trump contradicting his own Sunday diplomacy claims within 18 hours.

This morning's brief led with Brent crude's -4.3% pullback to $107.71, built on Trump's Sunday claim that Iran "agreed to most of" the 15-point demands. That pullback lasted less than 12 hours. Trump's Financial Times interview, published during European morning trading, declared "my favourite thing is to take the oil in Iran" and discussed seizing Kharg Island — directly contradicting his diplomatic framing (sources: FT, Bloomberg, CNN Business, NBC News, Al Jazeera, CNBC — all independently reported). Brent reversed from an early $104 low to touch $116 before settling at approximately $115.27, up 2.4% from Friday's close. CNN headlined "Oil prices top $116 after Trump says he wants to 'take the oil.'" The 18-hour arc — "agreed to most" to "take the oil" — caused a $12/bbl swing driven entirely by one person's contradictory statements. The IEA's characterization of the Hormuz closure as "the biggest oil shock in history" now sits alongside a record: Brent +51% in March, on track for its largest monthly surge ever recorded (source: CNBC).

### The RBI's NOP cap — this morning's second story — failed within four hours, and the rupee hit a new record intraday low of 95.22 before closing at approximately 95.07.

The morning brief correctly flagged the rupee's 93.88 rally as "mechanical, NOT fundamental." That assessment was vindicated faster than expected. The rupee opened at 93.56 as banks sold dollars to comply with the NOP cap, then reversed as oil surged back above $115. By afternoon it hit a record intraday low of 95.22 — worse than Friday's 95.09 close — before settling at approximately 95.07 (sources: FXStreet, Bloomberg, Business Standard, Freepressjournal, MUFG Research). MUFG Research titled their note: "India — RBI limits on INR onshore positions: Can't stop this train." The RBI's escalation ladder — conventional spot intervention ($30B+ burned in March), then the NOP regulatory cap — has been exhausted. The next steps would be an emergency rate hike or outright capital controls, both politically radioactive.

### The Nifty crashed 2.14% to 22,331 on FY26's last trading day — deepening from this morning's -0.9% — as record FPI outflows of ₹1.14 lakh crore in March marked the worst monthly selloff by foreign investors ever recorded.

This morning's 22,612 reading was early-session. By close, the Nifty settled at 22,331.40 (-488 points), Sensex at 71,947.55 (-1,635 points). Broader markets were worse: MidCap -2.68%, SmallCap -2.66%. PSU Banks, Bank Nifty, and Financial Services were the worst-performing sectors (source: Business Standard, NSE data). FPIs dumped a record ₹1.13-1.14 lakh crore in the March cash market alone (source: NSDL, Daily Pioneer). India entered FY26 as the world's fastest-growing major economy. It ended the fiscal year with record FPI outflows, a record-low rupee, and the Nifty down 13.9% from peak.

### Iran struck a power and water desalination plant in Kuwait, killing an Indian worker — expanding its targeting from military and energy infrastructure to civilian water systems that 90% of Kuwait's population depends on.

An Iranian drone/missile attack hit a desalination plant, killing one Indian worker and causing significant material damage. Kuwait's National Guard separately downed five drones (sources: Al Jazeera, The National Abu Dhabi, Anadolu Agency, India TV, Outlook India, The Week India — 7 independent sources). Kuwait relies on seawater desalination for approximately 90% of its drinking water. This represents a fourth category in Iran's targeting expansion: military (Feb 28) → energy (Mar 18) → industrial/ALBA/EGA (Mar 29) → civilian water/power (today). The Indian worker's death brings the conflict's Indian death toll to at least 7, across Oman, Saudi Arabia, maritime vessels, and now Kuwait. Saudi Arabia separately intercepted five Iranian ballistic missiles targeting its Eastern Province (source: Al Jazeera liveblog, Saudi MoD).

### The IRGC's noon university deadline passed without a confirmed attack — diminishing the credibility of future IRGC ultimatums.

The IRGC had demanded the US condemn strikes on Iranian universities by noon Tehran time (~1:30 PM IST). The US issued no condemnation. As of this evening — five hours past the deadline — no credible source reports an IRGC attack on any Gulf university campus (sources: Al Jazeera liveblog, Times of Israel liveblog — neither reports an attack). The American University of Iraq Baghdad pre-emptively moved to remote learning, and other US-affiliated campuses in Qatar, UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Bahrain took precautionary measures. The non-event is intelligence: a specific deadline set and not met weakens the IRGC's signaling power on future threats.

### Gold surged 2.4% to $4,598.70 — its strongest session in a week — as the market shifted from pricing de-escalation (morning) to systemic risk (evening), now within $1.30 of the $4,600 regime-shift confirmation threshold.

This morning, gold was up just 0.6%, consistent with the de-escalation narrative. The afternoon reversal — Trump's "take the oil" rhetoric, the Kuwait strike, Saudi missile interceptions — triggered a genuine safe-haven bid. Three consecutive up sessions (+2.7%, +0.6%, +2.4%) after the worst weekly rout since 2011 suggests the market may be shifting from an "inflation event" regime (where gold falls because oil-driven inflation pushes real yields higher) to a "systemic crisis" regime (where gold and oil rise together while equities fall). Not yet confirmed — the graph requires a sustained close above $4,600 for three sessions (source: yfinance confirmed, MUFG analysis, CNBC).
