## I. What Happened

### Brent crude pulled back 4.3% to $107.71 — the first significant decline since the war began — after Trump claimed Iran "agreed to most of" the US demands and Pakistan offered to host direct talks, but the pullback rests entirely on claims, not confirmed de-escalation.

Brent crude dropped to $107.71 on Monday morning, down from Friday's $112.57 — the most significant single-session pullback in 30 days of war. The catalyst: Trump told reporters on Air Force One Sunday that Iran has agreed to "most of" the 15-point demands, saying "They gave us most of the points. Why wouldn't they?" — and the Islamabad quadrilateral meeting (Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, Egypt) concluded with Pakistan offering to host direct US-Iran talks "in coming days" (sources: CNN, Bloomberg, NPR, Al Jazeera, Time, Political Wire all independently confirmed Trump's statement and Pakistan's offer). Iran has NOT confirmed any agreement — FM Araghchi called the plan "extremely maximalist and unreasonable" on March 25, and Iran has not publicly responded to Trump's latest claim. The market is pricing de-escalation HOPE, not de-escalation REALITY. The pullback is fragile: Pentagon is simultaneously preparing "weeks of limited ground operations" including potential Kharg Island raids (Al Jazeera, Axios), and Trump told the Financial Times "Maybe we take Kharg Island, maybe we don't." If Iran denies Trump's characterization or ground operations proceed, the $107 floor disappears.

### The RBI's NOP cap is working mechanically — the rupee rallied to ₹93.88 on Monday — but the underlying positions are 2-3x larger than estimated and banks are pushing back hard on the April 10 compliance deadline.

The rupee strengthened to ₹93.88 (yfinance, Monday Asian session) from a Friday RBI reference close of ₹95.09, as banks began selling dollars to unwind long-dollar onshore positions forced by the NOP cap (sources: Bloomberg, Business Standard, MarketScreener/Reuters, TradingView/Reuters). But the position size has been revised dramatically upward: Bloomberg reports "$30 billion unwinding looms"; Business Standard estimates "$25 billion to over $50 billion" — far exceeding our previous $10-18 billion estimate. Banks met the RBI on Saturday and asked for three months to comply, citing mark-to-market losses and the risk of "one-sided panic" if the April 10 deadline holds (source: Business Standard). Most positions are in the 1-3 month maturity bucket — an extension would let them roll off naturally rather than forcing a fire sale. The rupee rally is MECHANICAL: banks selling dollars creates one-directional buying pressure. Once unwinding completes, structural pressures (oil at $107, FPI outflows, reserves below $700B) reassert. The Nifty confirmed this by falling 0.9% to 22,612 DESPITE the rupee rally — the market sees through the technical effect to the underlying crisis.

### Pakistan's Islamabad quad elevated from bilateral message relay to multilateral mediation — the first institutional framework attempt, with talks continuing Monday and a hosting offer for direct US-Iran negotiations.

The four foreign ministers — Pakistan (host), Saudi Arabia (Prince Faisal bin Farhan), Turkey (Hakan Fidan), and Egypt (Badr Abdelatti) — met in Islamabad Saturday, with talks continuing Monday (sources: Bloomberg, NPR, Al Jazeera, Business Standard, Egyptian Streets). No joint statement was issued — significant because it means they couldn't agree on language, suggesting the gap between the US and Iranian positions was too wide for a consensus text. But the PROCESS matters more than the statement: Pakistan offering to host direct US-Iran talks "in coming days" is qualitatively different from relaying messages. The quad exchanged "assessments on dire economic repercussions" including supply chain disruption, food security, and energy security (Bloomberg) — the economic framing suggests these nations are feeling domestic pressure from the crisis. Turkey's FM Fidan sought to "establish a mechanism aimed at de-escalation" — institutional language, not ad hoc diplomacy.

### The IRGC expanded its targeting from military and energy infrastructure to INDUSTRIAL facilities — striking Bahrain's ALBA and UAE's EGA aluminum plants, claiming ties to US defense supply chains.

The IRGC confirmed strikes on Aluminium Bahrain (ALBA) — the world's largest aluminum smelter — and Emirates Global Aluminium (EGA) in the UAE, injuring two at ALBA and causing assessed damage at both (sources: CNBC, JPost, The National Abu Dhabi, Al Arabiya, Al Jazeera, Arab News — 6 independent sources). Bahrain separately reported intercepting 20 missiles and 23 UAVs on March 28, with additional damage to Bapco energy facilities. The IRGC framing: retaliation for US-Israeli strikes on two Iranian steel plants — "the companies have ties to US military and aeronautics firms." This represents a new targeting doctrine: any Gulf industrial facility with alleged US defense supply chain links is now a potential target. ALBA had already shut 3 of 6 smelting lines (19% of capacity) due to Hormuz disruption; this strike damages what was still operating. The precedent is dangerous — Gulf states' economic diversification pillars (aluminum, petrochemicals, aviation services) are now in the crosshairs.

### The IRGC university deadline is TODAY at noon Tehran time — the most immediately testable trigger of the war, with no US condemnation issued and the IRGC declaring all US and Israeli universities "legitimate targets."

The IRGC set a deadline of 12:00 noon Monday March 30, Tehran time (~1:30 PM IST), for Washington to condemn strikes on Iranian universities. The US has NOT issued any condemnation. The IRGC escalated Saturday to declaring "all universities of the occupying regime and American universities in the West Asia region are legitimate targets" and specifically said they will strike two universities in retaliation (sources: Asian Mirror, Press TV, Sputnik, JPost, The Week India, Republic World). The IDF confirmed it targeted Malek Ashtar University and Imam Hossein University, alleging both are IRGC military research facilities. By 2:00 PM IST today, we will know if the IRGC follows through — making this the most immediately falsifiable threat of the war. Follow-through = unprecedented targeting of civilian educational institutions across sovereign Gulf states (Texas A&M Qatar, NYU Abu Dhabi). Non-follow-through = IRGC credibility on future deadlines diminishes.

### The Pentagon is preparing "weeks of limited ground operations" in Iran — deploying the 82nd Airborne alongside Marine units as Trump simultaneously claims diplomacy is working.

Al Jazeera reported the Pentagon is "readying for weeks of US ground operations in Iran," including raids on Kharg Island and coastal sites near the Strait of Hormuz, using special operations and conventional infantry troops — falling "short of a full invasion" (sources: Al Jazeera citing Pentagon sources, Axios March 26 corroborates, CNN/CNBC on 82nd Airborne). Approximately 3,000 troops from the 82nd Airborne Division are deploying alongside two Marine Expeditionary Units, in addition to the 2,500 Marines on USS Tripoli that arrived March 28. Trump told the Financial Times: "Maybe we take Kharg Island, maybe we don't. We have a lot of options. It would also mean we had to be there for a while." A senior Iranian official told CNN: "Tehran will determine when the war ends." The defining contradiction of Day 30: the US is simultaneously claiming Iran "agreed to most" demands AND preparing to invade. Maximum pressure negotiation — or a genuine dual-track?

### KOSPI fell another 2.9% as the helium data shows Samsung may be insulated but the industry is not — the semiconductor cascade is differentiating between winners and losers.

KOSPI dropped to 5,281.76 on Monday (-2.9%, -16.3% monthly), as the helium cascade deepened with a critical data reconciliation. Samsung reportedly maintains "roughly six months" of helium stock (Korea Plus, Carra Globe), while TrendForce's March 24 "two-week clock" (now ~6 days) appears to apply to the INDUSTRY average — especially SK hynix, which lacks Samsung's strategic reserves and HeRS recycling system. Foreign investors have dumped ₩51.4 trillion ($37.8 billion) since February — the largest capital flight since the Global Financial Crisis. Samsung foreign ownership fell to 48.9% (12.5-year low). The won hit ₩1,512 (17-year low). Samsung is also exploring Russian helium supply. The cascade is no longer a binary "fabs stop or don't" — it's differentiating: Samsung buffered, SK hynix exposed, smaller fabs at risk.
