## I. What Happened

### The deal holds, but the strait is quietly re-closing by caution — transits fell to 12, and vessels are going dark.
Iran's claim to have shut the Strait of Hormuz remains rhetoric — CENTCOM reports no enforcement and "traffic continues to flow," and Iran's own foreign ministry says shipping is "operating normally," contradicting its military. But the operational picture has deteriorated regardless: **transits fell to 12 on Sunday from ~55 on Saturday, and 5 of 8 inbound vessels disabled their transponders**, a "dark, sanctioned" profile that Windward says "resembles the late-blockade baseline more than a functioning open strait" (source: Al Jazeera, CNBC, Windward). So the reopening is faltering not through an Iranian blockade but through shipowner caution in response to the closure rhetoric and the Lebanon fighting. The distinction still holds — declaration, not enforcement — but the chilling effect is real, and it is now the channel through which the oil premium could re-arm. (This corrects the impression of a cleanly "called bluff": the bluff moved the water even without a blockade.)

### The nuclear talks restarted in Geneva — and immediately hit the inspections wall.
Contrary to the weekend's "postponed indefinitely" read, **technical US-Iran talks began Sunday in Geneva**, and negotiators established a "communication line" to avoid incidents and ensure safe passage during the 60-day window — a concrete de-escalation mechanism (source: Al Jazeera, PBS). But the substance is already stuck: NPR reports that **a US-Iran dispute over nuclear inspections is clouding efforts to finalise the war-ending deal** (source: NPR, 23 June) — the same enrichment-and-verification core that stalled every prior round. The interim MoU holds and the Supreme Leader has endorsed it, but the hard part has started exactly where everyone expected it to jam. The 60-day clock is running on the inspections fight.

### Trump threatened to "hit Iran very hard again" — escalatory rhetoric in the middle of the negotiation.
Even with the Geneva talks underway, Trump threatened to "hit Iran very hard again" over the Hezbollah fighting (source: CBS). It is rhetoric — no new strike, and the talks continued — but it is the negotiate-while-threatening posture in plain view, and it signals US frustration that Iran is using Israel's Lebanon operations to justify its Hormuz pressure. The question is whether it is leverage to force Iran to de-link Hormuz from Lebanon, or the first sign of genuine re-escalation. So far the former: talks did not break.

### The Lebanon ceasefire is fraying — a fresh flare-up killed two after two days of calm.
The truce that took effect Friday is holding only in fits: after roughly two days of calm, **violence flared again in southern Lebanon, with Israeli fire killing two** (source: search-surfaced reporting). Israel maintains the ceasefire requires Hezbollah's "complete cessation," which it says is unmet; the IDF holds its buffer to the Litani. This is the connective tissue of the whole week — Iran cites the Lebanon breaches as the "violations" behind its Hormuz rhetoric, and a renewal of heavy fighting is the most likely route to derailing the Geneva talks.

### The India signal reversed: foreign investors went back to selling, and the "turn" did not hold.
A sober correction for the deployment thesis. After a single net-positive week, **FIIs resumed net selling — ₹636 crore on Monday** (domestic institutions absorbing with +₹1,036 crore), and on Tuesday bought only a token ₹18 crore in cash while selling Nifty futures; June's FII outflow stands near ₹43,000 crore (source: NSE provisional, Business Standard). The Nifty is holding the 24,000 level (~24,103) — but on domestic flows, not foreign conviction. The importer dividend from sub-$77 crude persists, yet the binding trigger the thesis requires — a sustained FII turn — has not fired and, if anything, reverted within a week. The catalyst is still pending; the hawkish Fed and a firm dollar remain the reason foreign money is staying away.
