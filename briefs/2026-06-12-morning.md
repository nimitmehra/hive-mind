## I. What Happened

### Trump cancelled the threatened third strike round and announced a "great settlement" with Iran — but the deal is unsigned, Iran says no final conclusion has been reached, and the enrichment red line that sank every prior round is unresolved.
Hours before the "bigger, more powerful" strike round he had promised, President Trump called the strikes off and announced the US and Iran had reached a "great settlement" to be "signed quickly," saying terms were approved by eleven states and that he believes Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei is ready to sign (source: RFE/RL, CNN, CBS). Pakistan's PM Sharif went further, declaring a "final, agreed-upon text" has been reached — but Iran's own messaging is split: FM Araghchi told state TV the deal has "never been closer" and an MoU could come "within 1 or 2 days," while spokesman Baqaei stressed Iran "has not reached a final conclusion," citing "US contradictions" (source: IRNA, Al Jazeera, CBS). A senior US official put the odds of signing within days at "80-85%, not 100%." This is the eighth "days away" claim of the war — but the first paired with a concrete de-escalatory action (the cancelled strikes) and on-record Iranian optimism, which is why markets moved. The cancellation is confirmed; the deal is not.

### Oil fell ~4% — Brent to $86.71, WTI below $85 — as the war premium that refused to bid up through the entire kinetic escalation finally deflated down on the first credible de-escalation signal.
Brent dropped 4.1% to $86.71 and WTI 3.9% to $84.29, with WTI falling ~4% in fifteen minutes at the moment Trump cancelled the strikes (source: CNBC, Bloomberg, TradingEconomics). This is the asymmetry the briefs have tracked for weeks, resolving exactly as predicted: a market positioned for choreography — one that wouldn't pay up for state-on-state strikes or a formal Hormuz closure — sold off hard the instant de-escalation looked real. Oil is now down ~6% on the week and −18% on the month; WTI is below $85 and Brent is approaching the "sub-$85 sustained" watch, though neither has crossed. The move is high-reversibility and two-way: an unsigned deal that breaks on enrichment snaps the premium back. And one structural caveat limits the downside even in a deal — Araghchi said Iran intends to retain "control" of Hormuz even under a settlement, so a reopening restores flow but may not restore the pre-war freedom-of-navigation regime.

### India staged its first genuinely war-positive session — Nifty +2.0%, Sensex +2.3%, the rupee firming to ₹95.10 — as cheaper oil and deal hopes flipped the import-cost story from headwind to tailwind.
The Nifty rose 2.0% to 23,622.90, the Sensex 2.3% (+1,695 points) to 75,527.95, and Bank Nifty led at +3.0%, with the rupee firming to ₹95.10 — away from the ₹96 line it had been pressing two days earlier (source: Business Standard, market-data.py). The mechanism is clean and, for once, runs in India's favour: India imports ~85% of its oil, so a ~$6 drop in Brent eases the import bill, the current-account deficit, and inflation simultaneously — the first time in this war the crisis has helped rather than hurt Indian assets. The deployment scorecard is improving for the first time (Brent now $86.71, rupee firming toward the ₹93-94 organic zone) but remains incomplete: this was a one-day rally on an 80-85% deal *hope*, not a signed agreement, and not yet the sustained FII-flow turn the deployment thesis requires.

### Gold rose 3.7% — but read it carefully: this is not a haven bid, it is the inflation-regime headwind unwinding.
Gold jumped 3.7% to $4,239.90, with silver +6.6% and copper +3.4%, bouncing off the ~23-March low (source: market-data.py). On a day when equities rose and the VIX collapsed, a safe-haven flight makes no sense — and the tell is copper, up 3.4%: industrial metals do not rally in a risk-off. What actually happened is the mirror image of the mechanism that crushed gold all cycle: oil down → inflation and December-hike fears ease → real yields and the dollar soften → the headwind on zero-yield gold releases. This is the *same* inflation regime relaxing, not the mutation to "financial-system risk" that would signal a true regime change. The $4,600 regime trigger stays watching, and the reader should resist the temptation to read this bounce as a flight to safety.

### The naval blockade stays "in full force until finalised" — so the enforcement campaign that killed three Indian sailors continues, even as the macro arc turns.
Trump was explicit that the US blockade "will remain in full force and effect until this Transaction is finalised" (source: RFE/RL). India, meanwhile, advanced its Settebello protest procedurally — summoning US Chargé d'Affaires Jason Meeks, handing him a formal demarche, and conveying "deepest concern over the ongoing incidents of attacks," now counting three Indian-crewed ships hit by US fire off Oman in four days (source: Business Standard, SCMP). The deal track caps the escalation risk — a signed agreement ends the blockade and the strikes — but until the ink dries, the irritant in the India-US lane persists, and the reported 30-day Hormuz-reopening clock has not started.

### Continuing threads.
**The other fronts are folding into the deal:** Araghchi said the settlement includes "a ceasefire on all fronts, including Lebanon," subordinating the Lebanon theatre and the Houthi axis to the wider talks; no new major Lebanon strike or Houthi attack on neutral shipping was confirmed, and the Red Sea wildcard trigger stays unfired on Day ~105. **Israel, benched from the negotiations, is cautiously supportive** — Netanyahu "expressed appreciation" for Trump's commitment to remove Iran's nuclear material and end its proxy support, his concern being enforcement, not the framework (source: CBS). **The crisis trades gave back:** defense fell (ITA −1.0%, Lockheed −1.5%) as the kinetic-continuation bid faded, and aluminium dropped 5.8% as the one real Gulf supply premium de-priced on de-escalation — copper's +3.4% on the same day confirming that was a premium unwind, not a demand collapse.

## II. What Analysts Say

### The Hill / Washington news desks — deal-skeptic consensus
Trump's announcement was "met with skepticism" because talks remain publicly deadlocked over uranium enrichment, sanctions relief and Iran's stockpile, and he has claimed a deal was close before without delivering. **Falsifiable:** if a signed MoU with explicit enrichment terms Iran's atomic agency endorses appears within ~5 days, the skeptics are wrong; if it slips again on enrichment, this is soft-deadline #8. The skeptic camp has been right on all ~7 prior "days away" claims.

### Council on Foreign Relations — "deferral is not resolution"
A durable ceasefire hinges on six unresolved issues — enrichment, sanctions sequencing, the uranium stockpile, Hormuz administration, proxy support, verification. A two-stage structure that defers these to "stage two" (as Araghchi describes) is a pause, not a settlement. **Falsifiable:** if the MoU signs but stage-two talks collapse within the reported 60-day window over enrichment or verification, the "deferral ≠ resolution" read is confirmed.

### Prediction markets — probabilistic discipline
A senior US official's "80-85% within days" and prediction-market pricing (~83% the S&P opens higher on the news) put a high-but-not-certain probability on a near-term deal. Market-implied odds have been a better real-time gauge through this crisis than headline rhetoric — and they are not pricing certainty.

### Macro / commodity desks — the "deal helps importers, not the Fed" split
A credible deal collapses the residual oil premium (Brent toward/below $85) and is unambiguously positive for oil-importing India, but does not reverse the US CPI/Fed problem: May's 4.2% print is locked and the 10Y barely moved (4.49%) even as oil fell 4%. **Falsifiable:** if Brent sustains below $85 and Indian assets firm while US December-hike odds stay elevated, the split holds — a deal is more bullish for Indian equities than American ones.

### Source Tone Assessment
**Iranian** media struck its most de-escalatory note of the war (Araghchi's "never closer") while keeping the hedge alive (Baqaei's "no final conclusion," and the insistence on retaining Hormuz control) — a coordinated negotiating posture, not internal division. **US** messaging flipped from "maximum force on all channels" to "deal-making on all channels" within 24 hours, Fox amplifying the administration's confidence while CNN and The Hill foreground the unsigned reality. **Indian** media swung from the "India vs America" sovereignty grievance straight to the relief rally within a day. **Gulf** states (Pakistan most forward) are co-sponsoring; **Israel** is cautiously supportive from outside the room; **European** coverage is energy-first relief.

The `marine-war-risk-insurance` node is the cleanest tell in the graph that the market is trading a probability, not a fact. One of the two — collapsing paper or stubborn physical — is wrong about the next 30 days, and the physical side only resolves when an actual signature reopens flow.
