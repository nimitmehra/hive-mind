## I. What Happened

### The US struck inside Iran while declaring the deal "largely negotiated"; Iran vowed a "graveyard" of revenge but has not fired a shot. [NEW — CONFIRMED ACTION; the dominant event]
On Monday May 25 (US time), American forces conducted what CENTCOM called "self-defense strikes" inside southern Iran — hitting two IRGC fast boats the US said were *laying mines* in the Strait of Hormuz and a surface-to-air missile site at Bandar Abbas the Pentagon said was targeting US aircraft (Iran's account is the inverse: that the US was conducting "naval harassment against Iranian commercial vessels"); spokesman Timothy Hawkins stressed the action was limited and taken "with restraint" under the April 8 ceasefire (CONFIRMED — CENTCOM via CNBC/NBC/WaPo, CNN, Al Jazeera, NPR). Iran's Foreign Ministry called it "bad faith" and a ceasefire violation; the IRGC declared its "reciprocal response" "legitimate and certain," and on Wednesday Navy deputy Mohammad Akbarzadeh warned Iran would turn its coast "from Chabahar to Mahshahr into a graveyard for aggressors" — yet **Iran has carried out no retaliatory strike as of May 27** (Euronews confirms only threats; the tell is that Akbarzadeh himself called full war "remote"). The signal is the simultaneity: the same 48 hours saw Trump call a Hormuz-reopening deal "largely negotiated" while warning he would go "back to the battlefront… bigger and stronger" without one, and at a rare Camp David cabinet meeting say he is "willing to resume fighting" and "doesn't care about the midterms" (CONFIRMED — NPR, CNN, PBS). This is not a contradiction to resolve but a single coercive strategy — strikes and signatures as two hands of the same bargain — and it is the precise kinetic event the May 25 brief warned the rally was mispricing. Note the limit: these were *targeted* self-defense strikes, not a resumption of the full air campaign — the Kharg/ground-invasion trigger stays dormant.

### Israel launched its expanded Lebanon offensive across the Litani, and Washington approved it — the deal's binding constraint is now a live ground war. [UPDATED — major escalation; trigger FIRES on both limbs]
The May 25 brief had the US merely "weighing a greenlight"; within 48 hours the operation is underway. Netanyahu said the IDF is "operating with large forces on the ground and seizing strategic areas" beyond the security zone and across the Litani River; overnight strikes hit 100-plus Hezbollah sites and Lebanon's health ministry reported at least 31 killed, including children, with evacuation warnings for Nabatiyeh province (CONFIRMED — Euronews, Algemeiner, Times of Israel, Lebanon Health Ministry). Washington approved the expansion but warned Israel against striking *in Beirut* to protect the Iran talks — though a senior Israeli official said targeted assassinations there remain permitted "if an operational opportunity presents itself." This collides head-on with Iran's precondition that any deal "end the war on all fronts including Lebanon," a track in which Hezbollah signs nothing. The next test is the US-brokered Israel-Lebanon ceasefire talks scheduled June 2-3: a monitored Lebanon clause folded into the MOU clears the path; continued expansion stalls or breaks the Iran deal.

Two regimes are running side by side: a crisis complex de-pricing the Hormuz tail, and an AI-semiconductor super-cycle that is fully decoupled from the war.

### The deal is "closer than at any point since the ceasefire" yet unresolved on its three hardest clauses — and Tehran is hardening, not softening. [UPDATED — Lebanon now a named blocker]
US reporting describes a draft MOU: a 60-day ceasefire extension, Hormuz reopened "with no tolls" and Iran clearing its own mines, the US lifting its port blockade and issuing sanctions waivers, a mechanism to *dispose* of Iran's enriched-uranium stockpile, and "strong inspections" (REPORTED — Axios, CNN). But three sticking points are now explicit: Hormuz control and tolls, the nuclear file, and whether the ceasefire covers Israel's Hezbollah campaign — which Iran insists it must. And Tehran's principals are cooling: Deputy FM Ali Bagheri Kani said "the two sides had not yet agreed on lifting the blockade"; Khamenei adviser Ali Shamkhani dismissed US control over Iran's nuclear programme as a "fantasy"; FM Araghchi said he was unsure a deal is imminent (CONFIRMED — Euronews, NPR). So the framework is simultaneously closer on paper and structurally stuck on its three load-bearing clauses — and both the US strikes and the Lebanon escalation cut directly against it.
