## I. What Happened

### The "signed Sunday" deal became a real framework — but it is unsigned, its text is unseen, and the signing has slipped to Friday in Switzerland.
The lead of Friday's brief — Trump's claim the deal "will be signed Sunday" — half-came true. On 14 June the US and Iran announced an initial framework, a memorandum of understanding brokered with Pakistani and Qatari mediation: a 60-day ceasefire extension, a Hormuz reopening and US blockade lift each within ~30 days, an end to Lebanon hostilities, and the nuclear/enrichment core deferred to a later 60-day track (source: NPR, CNN, Al Jazeera, Axios — multiple independent). That is genuine de-escalation, and the strongest diplomatic milestone of the war. But it is not the *signed text* Friday's brief was watching for: neither side has released the MoU ("the deal that remains unseen," per The Hill), US officials told CBS it was "signed" electronically while Pakistan's PM and NPR describe a formal ceremony scheduled for **Friday 19 June in Switzerland**, with preparatory meetings in Doha this week. The single trigger that matters — a published, agreed text — has not fired. Watch Friday.

### Israel openly rejected the deal's Lebanon clause and struck Beirut — the fracture line Friday's brief flagged is now live.
Friday's brief named Lebanon "the clearest near-term obstacle to the deal holding." Within hours of the framework, Israel struck Beirut's southern suburbs (at least three killed) and issued forced-displacement orders for some 29 towns in southern Lebanon, citing three Hezbollah projectiles into the north (source: Al Jazeera — confirmed action). More consequentially, Netanyahu told Trump that Israel will not withdraw from Lebanon and does not consider itself bound by the deal's Lebanon provisions, and Defence Minister Katz said troops will stay "indefinitely" (source: NPR, Times of Israel). Since ending the Lebanon fighting is an Iranian condition for a lasting peace, Israel — outside the negotiation and unbound by it — is the deal's most concrete spoiler. The US-Iran ceasefire itself is holding; the danger is on the Israel-Hezbollah axis the deal does not control.

### Iran's hardliners revolted against the framework, and its parliament moved to cut off the IAEA — the enrichment red line is now an open domestic fight.
The framework's weakest seam is the same one Friday's brief identified: enrichment. The MoU only contains a pledge to *negotiate* suspension later — and Iran is visibly moving the other way. Reports indicate that the day after the ceasefire, Iran's parliament voted for the Atomic Energy Organisation to end all cooperation with the IAEA, while Iran circulated a 10-point counter-proposal demanding recognition of its sovereign right to enrich (source: Fox, Iran International, MSNBC — reported, not independently confirmed as binding). Hardliners staged street protests in Tehran and Mashhad against Foreign Minister Araghchi and Speaker Ghalibaf, and Kayhan's editor attacked the deal as surrendering Iran's best bargaining chip. The civilian government is selling the framework; the IRGC-aligned bloc is trying to kill it. A REPORTED legislative act is not yet a confirmed enrichment resumption — but the direction of travel is toward less monitoring, not more.

### The relief rally fired hard on Monday — Wall Street records, oil at a cycle low — then began giving it back Tuesday on the Lebanon escalation.
Monday 15 June delivered the de-escalation repricing the market had positioned for: the S&P closed at a record 7,554.29 (+1.7%) and the Dow at a record 51,671.03, Brent fell 5.0% to $82.92 and WTI to ~$80 (lowest since mid-April), the VIX collapsed 8.4% to 16.20, and gold rose 3.0% to $4,340.90 (source: yfinance — confirmed). Crucially, the move did not overshoot: Brent held above its ~$73 pre-war baseline, because the structural floor is intact — the Pentagon assesses clearing 20-plus Hormuz mines at up to six months, and Iran keeps its ~$1/barrel "service fee." And the trade is already partly reversing: Tuesday saw oil tick back up and equities ease as the Israel-Lebanon strikes and the IAEA news re-armed the risk (source: PBS). The asymmetry has flipped — with the war premium largely discharged, oil's near-term risk is now to the *upside*, not the downside.

### India got its importer dividend — but it badly lagged a North-Asian market that is now an active competitor for capital.
The oil drop and a firmer rupee (₹94.55, up ~40 paise) handed India the cleanest macro tailwind of the war: Nifty rose 0.98% to 23,853.90 and the Sensex 0.97% to 76,264.33, led by rate-sensitives (Realty +2.75%, autos, banks) (source: Business Standard). But on the same risk-on day, North Asia ripped — KOSPI +6.8% to a record, Nikkei +4.9% to a record, SMH +4.4% — driven not by the war but by the AI-semiconductor supercycle (SK Hynix +11% on memory demand) and SpaceX's IPO clearing a liquidity overhang. India's +1% against North Asia's +3-7% is the problem in miniature: the global tech-and-EM allocation is flowing to Korea and Taiwan, not Mumbai. The importer dividend is real; the equity-flow turn that would convert it into conviction is not — FII flows were still net-selling at the last reading.

### The strait is still shut. Day 109, no transits, paper deal notwithstanding.
For all the framework's promise, the Strait of Hormuz remains physically closed — no vessel has transited a reopened strait, and the 30-day reopening clock is political, not operational (source: maritime trackers). The first real confirmation will not be a signature but a ship.

## II. What Analysts Say

### Council on Foreign Relations — a framework, not a settlement
A ceasefire-extension framework was achievable precisely because it defers the hardest issues — enrichment, sanctions sequencing, Lebanon, verification — to a later track. **Falsifiable:** if a single agreed text with explicit enrichment terms is published and signed Friday, it is more durable than skeptics claim; if Friday slips, splits, or the text stays unpublished, the "deferral, not resolution" read holds.

### Pentagon planners (via maritime/defence reporting) — demining is a six-month problem
Clearing 20-plus naval mines and restoring normal Hormuz throughput is a months-long operation, not a 30-day one; physical normalisation badly lags the political timeline. **Falsifiable:** if transit volumes exceed ~30 vessels/day for three consecutive days within 30 days (vs near-zero now, 138 the norm), too pessimistic; if transits stay depressed through July, it holds.

### India macro desks (Business Standard / Livemint) — importer dividend real, but conditional
Oil below $85 plus a firmer rupee is the best catalyst for Indian equities of the war, improving FY27 earnings visibility — but it does not reverse the US-CPI/Fed overhang, and the deal's Iran- and Lebanon-side risks could re-arm the oil premium. **Falsifiable:** the India "buy" converts from hope to conviction only on (1) a published signed text Friday and (2) an FII-flow turn; neither is confirmed.

### Oil desks — "buy-the-news is capped, and the risk has flipped up
The war premium is now largely out, and the structural floor (demining, Iranian service fee, idled-field restarts) means downside is capped above ~$73. With de-escalation priced, the near-term asymmetry favours an *upside* re-arm on Lebanon. **Falsifiable:** if Brent breaks and holds below $80 on a clean signing, the floor read is wrong; if it stalls in the low-to-mid $80s or rebounds on Lebanon, it holds.

### Source Tone Assessment
**Iranian** media is fractured — official optimism (deputy FM, Araghchi) over a visible hardliner revolt (Kayhan, street protests, the IAEA vote); watch IRNA-vs-Tasnim divergence as the internal-split tell. **Israeli** media is defiant and spoiler-mode — Netanyahu/Katz openly unbound by the Lebanon clause. **US** coverage splits the usual way: Trump's "done deal" framing versus the careful desks (NPR, CFR, The Hill) holding "initial, contested, unsigned." **Gulf** states are unambiguously pro-deal (Doha hosting prep talks). **Indian** coverage is relief-framed but measured — the importer dividend noted, the interim nature flagged. **European** outlets give a cautious welcome, energy-relief-focused (TTF easing) but wary on durability.

India's headwind right now is not the war — it is Korea and Taiwan.

### Risk Landscape
**Fading:** kinetic US-Iran escalation (ceasefire holding into the signing window); equity volatility (VIX 16.20). **Re-arming:** the oil premium (downside spent, Lebanon/IAEA risk now skews oil *up*); the Lebanon axis (Israel unbound, actively striking). **Stable-but-unresolved:** the enrichment red line (now an open Iranian domestic revolt — the deal's single point of failure); the legitimacy vacuum around an unseen Mojtaba Khamenei (who actually bound Tehran?). **Deployment question (India):** the macro setup is the best of the war — Brent <$85, rupee firmer, yields easing — but the two conditions that convert hope to conviction remain unmet: (1) a *published signed text* on Friday 19 June, and (2) an *FII-flow turn* (five net-positive sessions). Both are open, and North Asia is an active competitor for the same capital. Correct posture: staged and ready, not deployed — a clean Friday signing would be the strongest "lean in" signal of the war; a slip or a Lebanon-driven blow-up is the larger near-term risk.
