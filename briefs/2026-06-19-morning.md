## I. What Happened

### It is done: the US and Iran signed the 14-point deal, and — the test this brief set for weeks — the full text is published.
After nine "days away" claims across four months, the interim Memorandum of Understanding is signed and, decisively, **public**. Trump (with VP Vance) signed it electronically and formally at the G7/Versailles on 17 June; the in-person ceremony and start of implementation talks ran today at the **Bürgenstock resort in Switzerland**, with Qatar and Pakistan as guarantors (source: NBC, Swiss Federal DFA, i24NEWS). The single agreed text — which this brief repeatedly named as the real test, not the "signed" claim — was **published by Iranian President Pezeshkian on X and printed by NBC and i24NEWS**. Its 14 points: reopen Hormuz (free passage for 60 days), lift the US blockade immediately with US forces out within 30 days, end the war "on all fronts including Lebanon," release sanctioned Iranian funds, and a 60-day track for the nuclear file. The war-ending interim deal is now a fact; the hard nuclear core is explicitly deferred. NBC's framing captures it: "Trump finally sealed his Iran deal. Now talks on the 'final' deal begin."

### The Lebanon fracture — the deal's likeliest spoiler all week — went quiet on the same day, with a ceasefire taking effect at 4 p.m.
A new Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire took effect at **4 p.m. local time today**, brokered by the US and Qatar through parallel talks with Israel and Iran (source: Times of Israel, citing an Israeli source). That the guns fell silent on the Israel-Hezbollah axis on the very day of the signing removes the most acute near-term threat to the MoU holding. The caveat keeps it from being a full resolution: **IDF troops remain in the southern Lebanon buffer zone** — so Iran's stated condition (no continued occupation) is not fully met — Israel reserves the right to respond, and Lebanon the state was sidelined from the truce. It is a ceasefire, not a peace; its durability with Israeli troops still on the ground is the new watch-item. But for now, the clause most likely to break the deal is, instead, holding it together.

### Hormuz is physically reopening — the first foreign tankers actually transited — but flow is still ~95% below normal.
The reopening moved from paper to water: AIS data from Starboard Maritime Intelligence shows **at least 13 foreign tankers transited the strait** — the first confirmed foreign crossings since the blockade — following the signed MoU and lifted US blockade (source: CNBC, citing Starboard). This is the operational confirmation that was absent on Wednesday, when AIS showed no foreign movement and this brief walked the call back. But it is partial: most tankers reversed course, commercial flow remains **5-10% of pre-war levels (<10 vessels/day vs 100+)**, and mine-clearance, the tanker backlog, and insurance normalisation mean full flow is weeks to months away. The MoU's free-passage window runs 60 days, after which an Iran-Oman-administered fee structure resumes — a reminder that Hormuz reopens with a structural cost-floor, not a clean slate.

### The peace dividend rotated through markets: defense and gold down, tankers up.
Markets traded the end of the war, not the war. **Lockheed fell 4% and the defense ETF (ITA) 1.6%** as the war-beneficiary trade unwound; **gold cracked below $4,200 (−1.1% to $4,177), with silver −2% and platinum −2.7%** — the structural safe-haven bid that held for four months finally broke once the war actually ended and the Fed turned hawkish; and **Frontline rose 5.4%** as the Hormuz reopening turned bullish for tanker demand (source: yfinance). Brent held at $79.64 and WTI at $75.83 — the war premium is spent, and oil is a supply-and-demand story again. Energy equities de-rated with crude (Exxon −2.1%, XLE −1.6%). This is the cleanest "war is over" market signature of the entire crisis.

### The paradox of signing day: India fell. The Nifty snapped a five-day streak on an IT rout — because the Fed, not the Gulf, now drives it.
On the day the war-ending deal was signed, the Nifty **fell 0.6% to 24,013 and the Sensex 0.8%, snapping a five-day winning streak**, led down by an Infosys/TCS-led IT rout (source: HDFC Sky, yfinance). The driver was not the Gulf but yesterday's hawkish Fed: a hike-biased Fed and a dollar above 100 hit India's US-exposed IT exporters, and the importer dividend (oil sub-$80, rupee firm at ₹94.31) only cushioned the fall. India also conspicuously lagged a risk-on global tape (EM +3.3%, SMH +5.8%). The signal for the investor is sharp: the war risk that dominated India for four months is now resolved, but the binding constraint has fully shifted to US monetary policy and foreign flows — the deal helps the import bill, not the IT sector the index leans on.
