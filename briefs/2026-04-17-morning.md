## I. What Happened

### Brent crashed 6.5% to $92.92 — the largest single-day oil drop since the April 8 ceasefire — while the physical market barely moved at $132, blowing the futures-physical gap to $39 and the widest of the war.

Brent futures fell $6.13 to $92.92 on April 16-17, driven by the convergence of Trump's "very close to over" ABC News interview, the 10-day Israel-Lebanon ceasefire announcement, Goldman's institutional anchor at $90 Q2, and the market pricing a >50% probability of deal before the April 21 ceasefire expiry (sources: yfinance confirmed, CNBC, Goldman via Reuters). Against this, EIA reported US crude inventories fell approximately 913,000 barrels versus a 154,000 barrel draw expected — a notable bullish signal — and the physical market ignored the selloff entirely: Dated Brent held at approximately $132, widening the physical-futures gap from ~$35 to ~$39, the widest sustained divergence of the crisis (sources: EIA confirmed, TradingEconomics). The gap widening on a futures crash is the critical signal: futures are pricing the deal, the physical market is pricing the blockade, and they cannot both be right. If the deal materialises by April 21, physical converges down. If it doesn't, futures snap back above $100 within 1-2 sessions. CF Industries rose 3.2% counter-directionally — the purest equity-market confirmation that physical supply disruption persists regardless of deal rhetoric, since Hormuz chokes 50% of seaborne urea and even a ceasefire does not restore that logistics chain for months (sources: yfinance, UBS price target raised to $140 from $97).

### Trump announced a 10-day Israel-Lebanon ceasefire on April 16 — the first structured military pause since the war began — but Netanyahu said he was "shocked to learn of it from media."

The State Department confirmed a 10-day cessation of hostilities between Israel and Lebanon, effective midnight Beirut time April 16, described as "a gesture of goodwill by the Government of Israel, intended to enable good-faith negotiations toward a permanent security and peace agreement" — the first structured military pause since the war began February 28 (sources: State Department release, NPR, Axios — confirmed). Netanyahu stated Israel will maintain a 10-kilometre security zone inside Lebanon and characterised the pause as a "timeout" agreed "at Trump's request"; Times of Israel reported he told ministers he was "shocked to learn of truce from media" — a discoordination signal suggesting Trump announced without full Israeli buy-in (sources: Time, JPost, Times of Israel — confirmed). Hezbollah responded that "any ceasefire must be comprehensive across all Lebanese territory" and retains "the right to resist" Israeli troops — not an explicit accept or reject (sources: CNN, NBC — confirmed as statement). The ceasefire connects directly to the Iran track: Ghalibaf named a Lebanon ceasefire as one of Iran's Islamabad preconditions; the US had de-linked Lebanon from the Iran negotiations. This 10-day pause may serve as the face-saving partial delivery that unlocks Round 2.

### The JCS Chairman clarified the blockade covers Iran's ports, not the Strait of Hormuz itself — resolving the contradiction that defined the blockade's first week, while Saudi Arabia publicly broke with Washington and called for its end.

JCS Chairman Gen. Dan Caine stated on record that the US naval blockade covers "Iran's ports and coastline," not the Strait of Hormuz — the single most important definitional shift since the blockade began April 13 (sources: official JCS statement, CNBC — confirmed). This resolves the apparent contradiction between CENTCOM's claim that "no ships broke through" and Kpler/Al Jazeera shipping data showing 14 vessels transiting the Strait since Day 1: the Strait is technically open; the ports are blockaded. The Pentagon updated its deterrence count to 13 vessels turned around as of April 16. Iran released video of a "near-engagement" between IRGC Navy and a US vessel — no shots fired, but the first visual evidence of proximity confrontation (sources: Fortune — confirmed as video release). Separately, Saudi Arabia publicly urged the US to end the Hormuz blockade on April 14 — the first break from its "most hawkish Gulf ally" positioning — with Saudi officials confirming they are "talking to Iran on a daily basis" (sources: ACLED, Arab News — confirmed). MBS's absence from specific de-escalation signals while the Saudi foreign ministry engages Iran suggests the Crown Prince is preserving leverage for post-ceasefire negotiations, given Sadara's $20 billion JV at zero capacity with $3.7 billion in debt and a grace period expiring June 15.

### India's triple squeeze tightens to T-2 days as the RBI deploys administrative measures while the finance ministry warned of "considerable downside risk" to growth.

Treasury Secretary Bessent confirmed the Iran oil sanctions waiver will not be renewed when it expires April 19 — two days from now (sources: BusinessToday, Atlantic Council — confirmed). India received approximately 4 million barrels of Iranian crude via tankers Felicity and Jaya, with another 4-6 million in transit racing the deadline; India also ordered approximately 30 million barrels of Russian crude during the waiver window, building a buffer before both doors closed (sources: Bloomberg, BusinessToday — confirmed). The RBI made a new administrative intervention: asking state refiners to limit spot dollar demand and use forwards instead, with banks barred from offering non-deliverable forwards effective April 10 and net open positions capped at $100 million (sources: BusinessToday — confirmed). Nifty closed April 16 at 24,196 (-0.14%) after opening up 619 points on ceasefire optimism and erasing all gains on profit booking; today it recovered to 24,286 (+0.4%). FIIs turned net buyers on April 15 for the first time in weeks (INR 666 crore), though broader CY2026 outflows remain at Rs 1.79 lakh crore. The rupee strengthened to Rs92.79 — mechanical on the Brent crash — but the finance ministry warned its 7.0-7.4% growth forecast faces "considerable downside risk."

### The ECB meets today leaning toward a hold at 2.15%, while the UK-France Hormuz conference drew 70-80 nations but will not produce a near-term escort.

Bloomberg reported April 15 that ECB officials are "leaning toward keeping interest rates unchanged this month, postponing their verdict on whether the fallout of the Iran war warrants a response" — an ECB official described the situation as a "layer cake of shocks" (sources: Bloomberg, CNBC — reported). TTF gas fell further to EUR 41.62, down 24% over the past month, giving Lagarde cover to hold; markets price a 76% probability of a June hike (sources: TradingEconomics — confirmed). The Macron-Starmer Hormuz security conference proceeds today with attendance ballooning from 40 to 70-80 countries, including German Chancellor Merz and Italian PM Meloni in person and South Korea's President Lee via video — whose 26 vessels remain detained in the Strait (sources: GOV.UK, Seoul Economic Daily, Al-Monitor — confirmed). France maintains any escort would deploy "when security conditions allow" — a post-war framework, not a near-term commercial escort. The US is excluded as a "warring party." Only UAE and Bahrain from the Middle East are attending; Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Oman, and Pakistan are absent.

### Islamabad Round 2 has no confirmed date despite active preparations — Pakistan's shuttle diplomacy has expanded to Doha and Antalya while Iran insists talks happen "in Pakistan and nowhere else."

Pakistan's Foreign Ministry spokesperson stated explicitly on April 16 that "no date had been set for that next round of negotiations" — directly contradicting the implied imminence from White House press secretary Leavitt's "very likely" and AP's "Friday-Sunday window" reporting (sources: Al Jazeera — confirmed). However, Iran's ambassador to Pakistan stated "we will do talks in Pakistan and nowhere else, because we trust Pakistan," confirming both attendance and venue (sources: Al Jazeera — confirmed). Field Marshal Munir's Tehran shuttle was broader than reported: he met both FM Araghchi and Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf, and Al Jazeera reported Araghchi gave Munir "a warm hug" (sources: Gulf News, Al Jazeera — confirmed). PM Sharif expanded his shuttle to Doha on April 16, meeting Emir Tamim and stressing "de-escalation, dialogue and close international coordination," with an Antalya regional security forum reported for today (sources: Arab News, Al Jazeera — confirmed). The ceasefire expires April 21 with 4-5 days remaining and no signed paper.

### The Signal You Might Miss

The JCS clarification — blockade covers ports, not the Strait — is buried in technical military reporting but is the single most important structural fact of the blockade's first week. It means the blockade is a SANCTIONS ENFORCEMENT mechanism at Iranian ports, not a TRADE ROUTE CLOSURE of the Strait itself. The shadow fleet transits the Strait freely; legitimate commercial shipping avoids it because of insurance and compliance risk, not military interdiction. This distinction matters enormously for the investor: a "port blockade" can be lifted by executive order overnight; a "Strait closure" requires naval operations and mine clearing to reverse. The market is pricing the drama of "Hormuz blockade" while the operational reality is "Iranian port embargo" — a fundamentally less dangerous and more reversible configuration. War risk insurance at 0.8-1.0% of hull value (4-6x pre-crisis) is pricing the port-blockade reality, not the Strait-closure headline. When the insurance market and the headline market diverge, the insurance market is usually right — because it has real money at stake.
