## I. What Happened

### Iran's foreign minister declared the Strait of Hormuz "completely open" — and the IRGC-linked Tasnim rebuked him publicly within hours. The opening is real but fenced.
Iranian FM Abbas Araghchi posted on X late Friday afternoon US time: *"In line with the ceasefire in Lebanon, the passage for all commercial vessels through Strait of Hormuz is declared completely open for the remaining period of ceasefire"* (sources: Al Jazeera, Bloomberg, Argus — CONFIRMED; Kpler/MarineTraffic show eight commercial vessels transited by 2pm EST, still only 10-15% of the pre-war ~120/day baseline). Within hours, three operational fences went up. Tasnim News Agency (IRGC-linked) called Araghchi's post a *"flawed and incomplete tweet"* that *"created unnecessary ambiguity"* — the sharpest public IRNA-Tasnim divergence since the April 9 ceasefire breakdown. FM spokesperson Baghaei added that *"if the maritime blockade of Iran's ports continues, Iran will take reciprocal measures"* — an explicit conditional. And the IRGC Navy published a formal permission regime: all vessels require IRGC authorisation, civilian ships only, military vessels barred, transit only via Iran-designated routes (sources: Tribune India/ANI, Bloomberg — CONFIRMED). Trump's response (CNN): the *"US naval blockade of ship movements to and from Iranian ports remains in full force"* until a deal; CENTCOM says 21 vessels have now been turned back since April 13. Two "openings" are now in play — Iran's for commercial vessels at its grace, Washington's refusal to lift the port blockade. Iranian oil tankers still cannot move.

### Friday's oil crash and US equity record close were the largest single-day Hormuz-repricing event of the crisis; the fertilizer counter-directional thesis was refuted and the tanker counter-directional thesis held.
The Araghchi declaration printed mid-afternoon US time and moved every correlated asset. Brent settled at $91.87 (−7.6% on yfinance; wire-ICE print $90.38 at −9.1%); WTI at $84.00 (−11.3%); Heating Oil at $3.34 (−12.8%, the largest single-asset move on the board); Brent-WTI spread compressed to $7.87 from the evening brief's stale $8.39 mark. The S&P 500 closed at a record 7,126.06 (+1.2%), NASDAQ extended its 12-day win streak — longest since 1992 — and the VIX collapsed to 17.48, a war-era low (source: yfinance direct pull + Washington Times, NBC, PBS — CONFIRMED). The surprise came in the commodity equities. CF Industries crashed −9.65% to $112.68, round-tripping Thursday's +3.2% that had been framed in the evening brief as "the purest equity-market confirmation that physical supply disruption persists regardless of deal rhetoric"; Cheniere fell −4.4%, Nutrien −5.2%. Urea NOLA FOB fell 18% on the week to ~$640/st. But Frontline, the VLCC tanker operator, rallied +5.6% to $37.13 — counter-directional on a day everything else in the energy complex crashed, because dual Russian-plus-Iranian sanctions waivers remove ~90 vessels from compliant trade regardless of any ceasefire. The read: the fertilizer supply-disruption trade co-moves with deal rhetoric after all; the shipping disruption trade does not.

### Lebanon ceasefire Day 2 — IDF drone strike killed one in Kounine; Hezbollah compliance held; Haaretz's military desk says Netanyahu has been "boxed in" by Trump.
An Israeli drone strike on the southern village of Kounine on Saturday killed one person, per Lebanon's Health Ministry, on Day 2 of the 10-day Israel-Lebanon pause (sources: CBC, Lebanon NNA, Times of Israel — CONFIRMED). Lebanon's army reports continued artillery shelling and machine-gun fire from Day 1 into Day 2; the IDF frames its strikes under the ceasefire text's "self-defense" clause, which it reads as licence to strike *"planned, imminent, or ongoing attacks"* inside the 10km buffer zone Netanyahu announced. Hezbollah's compliance posture is unchanged: caution and vigilance, no rockets fired, any direct targeting to be treated as a breach (Ali Fayyad via Al Jazeera Arabic). The signal layer comes from Haaretz, whose military-desk analysis Friday was titled *"Trump Dictates the Terms in Lebanon and Iran, Leaving Netanyahu Boxed In"* — the sharpest Israeli establishment critique of the cabinet's post-ceasefire position. Haaretz sourcing tracks senior IDF thinking; the same paper's April 5 piece admitted the IDF *"can't disarm Hezbollah."* Smotrich and Ben-Gvir have not walked out in Day 2, which means the hardline coalition is accepting the pause tactically — but the 10-day window's transition to anything permanent would force that calculation.
