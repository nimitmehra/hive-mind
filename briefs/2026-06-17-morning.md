## I. What Happened

### The deal crossed from paper to physical: Trump ordered the naval blockade removed, and the first Iranian tankers are reportedly transiting Hormuz.
The single most important development of the war: President Trump declared "the Deal with the Islamic Republic of Iran is now complete" and **authorised the immediate removal of the US naval blockade** of Iranian ports (source: Truth Social, confirmed by PBS, CBS, Times of Israel). Iran confirmed the blockade is lifting, and specialised maritime reporting says Iranian oil tankers have begun transiting the strait — the first operational reopening signal of the 110-day standoff (source: investinglive, single specialised source; independent AIS confirmation still pending). Roughly 118 tankers stranded in the Gulf could exit within ~15 days, with transits rebuilding toward half of pre-war levels in a month — though the Pentagon's ~6-month demining estimate still gates full normalisation. The important caveat: Trump's "complete" runs ahead of the paperwork — the **MoU text remains unseen and the formal signing is still Friday 19 June in Switzerland.** But the blockade removal is the first concrete deliverable, not a promise, and the graph moved the Hormuz partial-reopening trigger to active on it.

### The Lebanon fracture deepened into an open Trump–Netanyahu rift — the deal's survival now depends on Washington restraining its own ally.
Israel intensified its Lebanon campaign — the IDF struck a "Hezbollah command center" in Beirut and airstrikes in eastern Lebanon killed 8-10 Hezbollah members, reportedly including a senior official (source: Gulf News, AOL; strikes confirmed, casualty counts reported), after Hezbollah fired on Israeli forces in the south. The novel element is an **open US-Israel rift: Trump publicly told Netanyahu "Let's not blow it!"**, with Axios reporting far harsher private rebukes over the disproportionate strikes and civilian deaths threatening the deal (source: CNBC confirmed public; Axios private, reported). Netanyahu maintains Israel is not bound by the deal's Lebanon clause and is defending itself; Iran's parliament speaker Ghalibaf warned the strikes "threaten to upset the deal." With the US-Iran axis de-escalating on schedule, the binding risk has migrated to whether Trump can rein in Netanyahu before Friday — a tension inside the Western alliance, not between the war's principals.

### Oil fell further to near pre-war levels — and notably did NOT re-arm on the Lebanon strikes, correcting yesterday's expectation.
A correction to yesterday's brief, which anticipated a Tuesday "give-back" with oil ticking up on Lebanon: the opposite happened. Brent fell to **$78.65 and WTI to $74.98 — the lowest since early March, now only about $5 above the ~$73 pre-war baseline** (source: yfinance, TradingEconomics). The driver is supply recovery — the blockade lifting, ~118 tankers preparing to exit the Gulf, and higher expected OPEC+/UAE output — and it overwhelmed the Lebanon-escalation risk, which would normally bid crude. That the premium kept deflating despite Israeli strikes is itself the signal: the market is pricing actual barrels returning, not sentiment. The war premium that defined this crisis is now nearly fully unwound, and the graph moved the "Brent sub-$85 sustained" trigger to active.

### India reclaimed 24,000 on the importer dividend — but foreign investors are still selling into it.
The Nifty reclaimed the ~24,000 resistance level (24,064) and the Sensex closed near 76,960, with the rupee firming a second day to ~₹94.5 and the 10-year yield easing to 6.86% — the cleanest importer-dividend setup of the war, led by OMCs, banks, financials, paints and tyres (source: Business Standard). But the rally is domestically driven: **FII flows flickered positive by just ₹200 crore Monday, then reverted to ₹749 crore of net selling Tuesday** (net short Nifty futures), absorbed by ₹3,189 crore of DII buying. The conviction signal the investor is waiting for — a sustained FII turn — has not come, even with every macro variable breaking India's way. 24,000 is now the line: a hold on foreign buying would confirm; continued FII selling keeps it range-bound.

### The day's biggest market move was a 4.8% drop in semiconductors — and it has nothing to do with the war.
With the Fed's decision due today, US equities consolidated (S&P +0.13% to a near-record, Dow +0.78% to a record), but the SMH semiconductor ETF fell 4.8% — profit-taking after Monday's parabolic AI rip (SK Hynix +11%), not a risk-off or war signal (source: yfinance; oil fell and the VIX held at a calm 16.4). The reader should not misread the semis pullback as crisis-driven: the AI trade runs on its own cycle, and the de-escalation is intact. The **FOMC decision lands today** — the swing factor for whether oil's ~20% collapse loosens the Fed's hawkish hold and compounds the equity tailwind, or whether the rates channel stays sticky.

The `strait-of-hormuz → marine-war-risk-insurance` and `→ brent-crude` edges re-activated as the paper-physical convergence the 13 June brief flagged now completes from the physical side.

### The Signal You Might Miss
Everything broke India's way this week — Brent collapsed below $80, the rupee firmed, yields eased, the Nifty reclaimed 24,000 — and **foreign investors still sold** (+₹200cr Monday, −₹749cr Tuesday). That non-response is the signal. With FPI ownership of Indian equities at a multi-year low (~15-16%, now below domestic institutions for the first time), the marginal foreign buyer is not returning even on a textbook-perfect macro setup — because the same global capital is being absorbed by the North-Asian AI complex (KOSPI/Nikkei still +15-16% on the month). The importer dividend is necessary but not sufficient for the India "buy"; the missing ingredient is a foreign-flow turn that a cheaper oil import bill alone is not producing. That is the cleanest read in the graph of why India can have great news and a capped tape simultaneously.

### Risk Landscape
**Fading fast:** the oil/Hormuz risk (blockade lifting, war premium nearly gone, Brent ~$5 above pre-war) — the crisis's defining cascade is unwinding. **Migrating:** the deal risk, from the US-Iran axis (de-escalating) to the Israel-Lebanon axis (Trump-Netanyahu rift) — a single major Israeli strike before Friday is the live deal-breaker. **Stable-but-unresolved:** the enrichment red line (Iran reducing IAEA monitoring, deferred to the 60-day track). **Non-crisis swing factor:** the FOMC decision today. **Deployment question (India):** the macro conditions are now largely met — Brent sub-$85 (trigger active), rupee firm, yields easing — but the two confirmation triggers are not: (1) a *published signed text* on Friday 19 June, and (2) an *FII-flow turn* (five net-positive sessions), which Tuesday's selling moved further away. Posture: staged and ready, leaning constructive — a clean Friday signing would complete the macro case, but deploy on the flow turn, not on the news.
