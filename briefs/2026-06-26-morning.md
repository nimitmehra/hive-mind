## I. What Happened

### The hardest issue moved: a 60-day roadmap, and Iran agreed to let IAEA inspectors back in.
The nuclear track — stuck on inspections at midweek — produced real progress. The US and Iran **agreed a roadmap toward a final deal within 60 days**, and Iran **agreed to invite IAEA inspectors back into the country**, which VP Vance called "the first step in permanently ending" the weapons program, with IAEA contact possibly starting Monday (source: CNBC, NPR; "encouraging progress" per mediators Qatar and Pakistan). Working groups were set up for the nuclear file, sanctions, and monitoring. This is the most concrete movement on the core issue since the war began — inspectors-back is a genuine concession, not rhetoric. The caveats keep it honest: it is unresolved whether Iran can keep **enriching**, what happens to the **highly-enriched-uranium stockpile**, whether inspectors can view the **bombed** sites (a live US-Iran dispute), and the sanctions timeline. The 60-day clock now runs on those.

### Iran started actually turning ships back at Hormuz — rhetoric became (partial) enforcement.
The Strait recovery hit a wartime high — **49 transits on 24 June** — and then Iran did something it had only threatened before: **the IRGC turned tankers back. Windward independently corroborated five vessels reversing course and a sixth going dark on 25 June**, matching the IRGC's own claim (source: Windward, CNBC). The southern corridor, previously believed free of Iranian approval, is now actively policed, with a VHF broadcast warning that transit without IRGC permission is "at your own risk." This is the crucial distinction from 20 June, when Iran merely *declared* a closure that CENTCOM dismissed: this time ships were physically turned back, with operational corroboration — so the graph moved the enforcement trigger to active. It is not a blockade (43 transits still went through on the 25th), and a cargo ship was separately attacked off the Omani coast, but the message is clear: Iran is policing the strait as leverage while the talks proceed.

### The Lebanon ceasefire is a ceasefire mostly in name — Israel kept striking.
The truce is holding on paper only. **At least 12 Israeli air raids and continual artillery struck southern Lebanon after the deadline, and an Israeli drone killed two people on a motorcycle** (source: Al Jazeera, Lebanon NNA). Hezbollah says the ceasefire "will hold if Israel abides by it," but Israel's strikes "continued almost immediately." Lebanese-Israeli talks ran in Washington through 25 June. This matters beyond Lebanon: Israel's continued strikes are precisely what Iran cites to justify its Hormuz turn-backs, so the fraying ceasefire is the engine of the strait's instability.

### Oil hit the pre-war baseline — the war premium is fully gone, even as Iran policed the strait.
**Brent fell to $73.57 and WTI to $70.24** — back to where they sat before the 28 February war began (source: yfinance). The striking part: oil fell *on the same day the IRGC turned ships back*, because the market judges the enforcement limited and the reopening durable (43 transits still occurred). The premium that defined four months of this crisis is fully unwound. For the investor this means the oil-driven importer dividend for India is now maximally banked — crude can't fall much further on de-escalation alone — and Brent's risk has flipped asymmetric to the upside, with the IRGC enforcement and the Oman-coast attack as the re-arm channel.

### India held 24,000 — but on domestic money; foreign investors kept selling.
The Nifty rose 0.83% to ~24,021 and the Sensex 1.04% Friday (both ~+1.7% on the week) as cheap oil and fading war risk lifted sentiment — but **FIIs were net sellers of ₹1,843 crore**, absorbed by ₹3,637 crore of domestic (DII) buying. The pattern is now unmistakable and twice-confirmed: India is rising on domestic flows, and the foreign-flow turn the deployment thesis requires has not come. The importer dividend is fully in hand; the FII catalyst is not, and a hawkish Fed plus a firm dollar remain the reason foreign money is staying away.
