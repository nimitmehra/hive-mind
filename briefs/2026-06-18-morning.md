## I. What Happened

### The signing is confirmed for tomorrow at Bürgenstock — Switzerland is now on record, with Qatar and Pakistan as guarantors.
The interim deal moved from "Trump says Friday" to institutionally locked: the **Swiss Foreign Ministry has officially confirmed the date and venue** — Friday 19 June at the Bürgenstock resort — and the ceremony will be attended by **Qatar and Pakistan** as co-mediators/guarantors (source: Bloomberg, Swiss FM via India TV). Mediators describe the interim MoU text as "finalized," with separate Doha preparatory meetings this week. A multilateral signing with a neutral host and named guarantor states is structurally harder to cancel than a bilateral handshake — it raises the diplomatic cost of a last-minute walk. The caveat that matters for durability: "finalized" applies to the *interim* MoU (ceasefire, Hormuz, blockade, Lebanon), not the nuclear core, which is deferred to the 60-day track and remains openly contested. Tomorrow's test is simple — does a single agreed text actually get signed and published.

### Correction: the Hormuz reopening has not happened on the water — tanker traffic is unchanged, and we have walked back yesterday's read.
Yesterday's brief carried a single-source report that Iranian tankers had begun transiting Hormuz, and moved the reopening trigger to active. Independent operational data now contradicts that: **Argus reports "Hormuz tanker traffic unchanged after US-Iran deal,"** and AIS audits show only ~18 vessels — Iranian-domestic-flag only, foreign-flag absent, still ~95% below pre-war (source: Argus, Global Energy Flow AIS). Shipowners are waiting for tomorrow's signing and details before attempting transits; the strait remains physically closed. The US blockade-removal *order* stands and is real, but the physical reopening is **ordered, not occurring** — so we have reverted the trigger to "watching" pending confirmed foreign-flag transit. This is the honest correction: the deal's first physical deliverable has not yet materialised, and the war premium's last leg out of oil awaits actual ships moving.

### The Fed held but signalled a hike — the single biggest market force this week, and it has nothing to do with the war.
In the first decision under new chair Kevin Warsh, the Federal Reserve **held rates at 3.5-3.75% and its projections flipped to a +0.25% hike this year** — a turnaround from the cut penciled in three months ago — with cuts "off the table" while inflation runs at a three-year high (source: NPR, Federal Reserve statement). Markets repriced hard: the **S&P fell 1.2%, the Nasdaq 1.3%, the VIX jumped 12.4% to 18.44, and the dollar (DXY) broke back above 100** (source: yfinance). This is the "deal helps importers, not the Fed" split made concrete — the ~20% oil collapse did *not* buy a dovish pivot. Crucially for the investor: this drag is independent of the Iran deal and will not reverse when the MoU signs tomorrow.

### India held 24,000 — but it closed before the Fed, and foreign investors are still selling.
The Nifty rose for a fourth straight day, +0.4% to **24,085**, holding above the 24,000 resistance, with the Sensex +0.5% to 77,156, on the importer dividend (oil sub-$80, rupee firm at ₹94.5) and domestic buying (source: HDFC Sky, Business Standard). Two caveats define the setup. First, the Indian close **preceded the hawkish FOMC**, so the dollar's break above 100 and the global risk-off are unpriced headwinds for today's open. Second, the rally is domestically driven — **FIIs have sold ~₹47,000 crore in June**, with no turn, and a hawkish Fed makes the foreign-flow rotation into India harder, not easier. The macro tailwind is real; the conviction signal still isn't there.

### Gold held firm through a hawkish Fed — a quiet tell of structural demand.
On a day built to hurt gold — a hawkish Fed, the dollar above 100, the front-end yield up — bullion instead **rose 0.3% to $4,346** (source: yfinance). The structural central-bank and debasement bid is absorbing the rate headwind that would normally pressure it; gold has decoupled from the rate cycle. It is a small move, but holding on a textbook gold-negative day is itself the signal: the floor under gold is real.
