## I. What Happened

### Strait of Hormuz transit collapsed to 16 vessels Monday — the hardest operational finding of the crisis, and paper Brent has not confirmed it.
Al Jazeera reported only 16 ships transited the Strait of Hormuz on Monday against a pre-war baseline of 138; CNBC's transit tracker noted Sunday was the first zero-transit day of the crisis; The National (Abu Dhabi) described the situation as "near standstill" (CONFIRMED — MarineTraffic AIS, Al Jazeera, The National, CNBC, NBC News data graphics — five independent chains). The combined Iranian "blockade" announcement of April 18 and the US Navy's *Touska*-enforced counter-blockade have jointly suppressed commercial transit to roughly 12% of baseline, materially more severe than the "1-2% of baseline" framing carried in yesterday morning's brief and correcting it. Yet Brent settled at $94.88 Monday (+5.0%), still below Goldman's $96-98 interim band and well short of Morgan Stanley's $100 convergence trigger — the physical market is priced for severe disruption while the paper market is priced for a contained one-off. Three mechanisms explain the gap: floating storage in the Gulf remains elevated, Lloyd's AWRP moderated to ~1.0% rather than stepping up, and participants are pricing Wednesday's ceasefire decision as determinative. If the ceasefire lapses, the transit data becomes binding on paper and $100-105 becomes modal within 48 hours.

### Trump told Bloomberg a ceasefire extension is "highly unlikely" and the blockade "will stay until a deal is signed" — the Wednesday deadline is now a binary calendar event.
In a Bloomberg interview published Monday, President Trump dismissed a procedural rollover of the two-week ceasefire that expires Wednesday evening Washington time (roughly 05:30 IST Thursday) and reiterated that the US naval blockade of Iranian ports is non-negotiable until a framework is signed (CONFIRMED — Bloomberg, CNN live blog, CBS News, NPR, CNBC, Ynet, Haaretz — six independent chains). This closes the previously-live possibility that the ceasefire might roll over quietly. Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei responded publicly that US demands "will never lead to good results" and called the blockade "unlawful and criminal, amounting to war crime and crime against humanity"; Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf added that Trump "seeks to turn this negotiating table — in his own imagination — into a table of surrender" (Al Jazeera, IRNA, CBS News). Tehran has preconditioned any negotiation on blockade-lifting; Washington has preconditioned blockade-lifting on a signed deal. The positions are now structurally opposed, and the next 32 hours either produce a face-saving bridge — US Treasury modifying the blockade, a named Iranian delegation landing in Islamabad, or a Ghalibaf-Witkoff handshake — or the ceasefire lapses and the default resets to kinetic-quiet blockade.

### Vice President Vance, Steve Witkoff, and Jared Kushner arrived in Islamabad overnight; Iran publicly refused to send negotiators while Al Jazeera reported a delegation may arrive Tuesday.
The US delegation landed overnight April 20-21, with Pakistani mediators led by PM Sharif and Army Chief Munir establishing a "Red Zone" security perimeter (CONFIRMED — Express Tribune, ANI, Bloomberg, Times of Israel liveblog, Tribune India — five sources). On the Iranian side the register is deliberately fractured. Baghaei's ministry said no delegation will be sent; Ambassador Reza Amiri Moghadam offered the notably softer conditional that "as long as the naval blockade remains, faultlines remain" — language that implies blockade-lifting resolves the impasse rather than rendering it terminal (Al Jazeera). FM Araghchi separately took a call from Pakistan's FM Dar on Tuesday morning, agreeing to "remain closely engaged" (Pakistan Today); Al Jazeera, citing Iranian sources, reported a delegation possibly including Ghalibaf and Araghchi might arrive in Islamabad Tuesday (REPORTED, single-source). Kayhan — the hardliner flagship that attacked Araghchi's April 17 Hormuz tweet — published no fresh assault on him Tuesday, which under yesterday's brief's methodology signals tacit factional acceptance of the two-track. The practical read: Iran is publicly rejecting the format while channel-level diplomacy continues through Araghchi, with multiple registers aimed at different audiences. Yesterday's "factional collapse" reading was too binary and has been superseded by rough-consensus.

Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya Central Headquarters statement, carried by Tasnim Monday and treated Tuesday as front-page analytical framing rather than breaking news, holds that retaliation is deferred "due to the presence of family members of the ship's crew on board" until "safety of the families and crew members is fully ensured" — the deferral has been elevated from tactical delay to operational doctrine.
