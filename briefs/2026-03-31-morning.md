## I. What Happened

### Trump threatened to "completely obliterate" Iran's entire civilian infrastructure while his 82nd Airborne paratroopers arrived in the Middle East — the most dangerous combination of rhetoric and capability since the war began.

In a Truth Social post late Monday, Trump stated the US would "blow up and completely obliterate all Electric Generating Plants, Oil Wells and Kharg Island (and possibly all desalinization plants!)" if the Strait of Hormuz is not "immediately" reopened and a deal is not reached "shortly" (source: CNBC, Axios, PBS — all quoting the post directly). International law experts immediately flagged this as describing collective punishment prohibited under international humanitarian law: "You can't deliberately harm an entire civilian population to pressure its government" (source: PBS, citing multiple legal scholars). Simultaneously, Military Times confirmed that thousands of 82nd Airborne paratroopers have physically arrived in the Middle East, alongside the 31st MEU's 2,200 Marines on USS Tripoli — swelling the force to approximately 55,000 with a division-level headquarters, the kind of command structure used for operations larger than brigade-level raids (source: Military Times, Stars and Stripes). Iran's VP Aref responded directly to the Kharg Island seizure threat: "Trump can only decide about sending troops to Khark; but when it comes to bringing them back, the decision is no longer his. Because no one returns home from hell" (source: NBC News). The triple contradiction deepens: "great progress" in talks + "obliterate everything" if talks fail + ground forces positioned to execute — all from the same administration within 48 hours. Six days remain until the April 6 energy strike pause expires.

### India closed FY26 with the worst FPI outflows, rupee, and market performance in a generation — and FY27 opens into a $115 oil headwind with exhausted policy buffers.

Indian markets are closed today for Mahavir Jayanti, but the numbers from FY26's last trading day tell the story: Nifty crashed -2.14% to 22,331, Sensex fell -2.22% to 71,948, the rupee hit a record intraday low of 95.22 before closing at 95.07, and FPIs dumped a record ₹1.14 lakh crore ($14.8B) in March alone — the worst monthly foreign investor outflow ever recorded (source: NSDL, Business Standard). The India 10-year bond yield breached 7.03%, surging 37 basis points in March — the biggest monthly move since February 2017 (source: Business Recorder, RBI data). The RBI's NOP cap — its most aggressive regulatory intervention — collapsed within four hours as the rupee rally reversed entirely. GIFT Nifty is trading down 1.84%, signaling a bearish open when markets resume on April 1 (a settlement holiday with trading but no clearing). One bright spot: H1 FY27 government borrowing was announced at ₹8.21 lakh crore, lower than expected, keeping bond yields from spiking further — a fiscal discipline signal even under extreme pressure. India entered FY26 as the world's fastest-growing major economy; it exited with record outflows, a record-low rupee, and Nifty down 13.9% from peak. Bernstein has warned: Nifty below 20,000 and rupee beyond ₹110 if the conflict persists.

### South Korea's KOSPI broke below 5,100 in Tuesday trading — now the worst-performing major index in March at -18.3% — as the helium-semiconductor cascade approaches its critical exhaustion window.

KOSPI fell 3.4% to 5,099.97 in live Tuesday trading, with SK hynix leading the rout at -5.8% and Samsung dropping 3.5% (source: yfinance live data). The won hit ₩1,533.72 against the dollar, its weakest since March 2009. Foreign investors have now sold ₩51.4 trillion ($37.8B) since the war began — the largest outflow since the Global Financial Crisis — and Samsung's foreign ownership has dropped to 48.9%, a 12.5-year low (source: Korea International Trade Association). The damage is a triple cascade: helium shortage from Qatar's Ras Laffan destruction accounts for approximately 40% of the decline (TrendForce estimates 6-8 days until Asian chip fab inventories exhaust at full production rates), South Korea's 70% energy import dependence from the Gulf accounts for another 25%, and the resulting foreign capital flight accounts for the remaining 35% (source: TrendForce, Korea Plus). Samsung claims a 6-month helium buffer via its in-house HeRS recycling system; this conflicts with TrendForce's industry-wide data and remains the single most important data dispute in the semiconductor supply chain. If SK hynix announces production cuts before Samsung, it confirms the buffer claim is credible for Samsung but not industry-wide.

### Iran is transforming Hormuz from a military blockade into a geopolitical sorting mechanism — approving ships from seven countries while maintaining 95% traffic reduction.

Iran's FM Araghchi announced that ships from China, Russia, India, Iraq, and Pakistan may transit the Strait of Hormuz, with Malaysia and Thailand subsequently added (source: Al Jazeera, CNBC). Pakistan separately secured a deal for 20 Pakistani-flagged vessels at two per day, announced by Pakistan FM Dar (source: Al Jazeera, CNBC). The IRGC toll booth system continues: yuan-denominated payments of approximately $2 million per transit, with IRGC escort through Iranian territorial waters around Larak Island (source: USNI News, Lloyd's List Intelligence). The strategy is deliberate: reward neutrals to split the Western coalition while maintaining the blockade against the US, Israel, and their allies. Total transits remain at 142 for March 1-25 versus 2,652 pre-war baseline — a 95% reduction that the selective opening has not meaningfully eased (source: Lloyd's List Intelligence). Trump's "20 boats" claim from Sunday appears to conflate the Pakistan deal with broader transit data — Iran has not confirmed his characterization. The selective opening is positive for India's energy security (Indian tankers Shivalik and Nanda Devi previously transited via the Jaishankar-Araghchi channel), but it does not resolve the crisis — it monetizes and politicizes it.

### Israel struck 170 targets "throughout Tehran" in 24 hours, including weapons production sites, with an unnamed official saying there is "no intention to scale back" before talks.

The IDF announced it "has just completed a further extensive wave of strikes targeting infrastructure of the Iranian terror regime in the heart of Tehran," hitting 170 targets including a site used for assembling long-range anti-aircraft missiles (source: IDF statement via Al Jazeera, Reuters, Business Standard). Power infrastructure was targeted, causing a blackout that has since been restored (source: Iranian authorities via CBS News). An unnamed Israeli official told Reuters there is "no intention to scale back attacks against Iran before any possible talks" — a statement that directly contradicts the diplomatic narrative Trump is projecting. This divergence is intelligence: Israel is pursuing its own military timeline independent of US diplomatic posturing, and the gap between "great progress" (Trump) and "no scale-back" (Israel) suggests the two allies are not coordinating their messaging on the exit strategy.

Gold rose 1.5% to $4,592.80, its fourth consecutive up session (+2.7%, +0.6%, +2.4%, +1.5%) after falling 17% from its January all-time high of approximately $5,600 (source: yfinance). The regime shift test is approaching its decisive moment: the graph requires three consecutive sessions closing above $4,600 to confirm the shift from an "inflation event" regime (where oil-driven inflation pushes real yields higher, strengthening the dollar and crushing gold) to a "systemic crisis" regime (where gold and oil rise together while equities fall — the traditional safe-haven correlation). If today's US session pushes gold above $4,600, the three-session clock starts. JP Morgan targets $6,300 by year-end. The mechanism constraining gold remains the US 10-year yield at 4.34% — real yield competition that diverts safe-haven flows into dollars instead of gold. If the crisis shifts from inflation-risk to financial-system-risk (a bank with Gulf exposure in trouble, for instance), that constraint breaks.

### Houthi officials made their most explicit Bab al-Mandeb closure threat yet — but Day 32 brought zero confirmed Red Sea shipping attacks, and the double-chokepoint trigger remains unactivated.

Three separate Houthi sources escalated the Red Sea rhetoric: deputy information minister Mohammed Mansour told media that "closing the Bab al-Mandeb strait is among our options"; two senior officials told international media the group has "decided to restart missile and drone operations against maritime traffic"; and IRGC-linked Tasnim quoted an unnamed military source warning Iran could "open new fronts at the mouth of the Red Sea" if the US conducts ground operations on Iranian islands (source: Washington Times, CBS News, Jerusalem Post, France 24). Maersk has pre-emptively rerouted its ME11 and MECL services back to the Cape of Good Hope (source: gCaptain). Despite all of this, Day 32 of the conflict has produced zero confirmed Houthi attacks on Red Sea commercial shipping — the attacks remain Israel-targeting only (two strikes on March 28-29, both intercepted). The Houthi pattern since the war began has been consistent: escalating rhetoric followed by delayed, limited action. 28 days of "fingers on the trigger" preceded the first Israel attack. The market is pricing in the threat (Maersk's rerouting is real commercial cost) even without the action — which is itself the Houthi strategy, generating "value without expenditure" as the Soufan Center noted.
