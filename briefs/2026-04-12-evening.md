## What Changed Since Morning

### Iran preserved the diplomatic track with three institutional signals but established "no hurry" leverage — while the specific "final offer" document remains formally unanswered.

This morning's brief identified the IRNA-Tasnim convergence as the most important signal. Three messages have since clarified Iran's post-talks stance. Iran's government posted on X: "Negotiations will continue despite some remaining differences" — explicitly preserving the track. FM spokesperson Baghaei stated: "It is natural that we shouldn't have expected to reach agreement in just one session" — normalising the failure (source: Al Jazeera, NPR, CBS). Working against these, Iran signalled it is "in no hurry to resume negotiations, stating that unless the United States agrees to a 'reasonable' deal, there will be no change in the situation in the Strait of Hormuz" (source: Free Press Journal). Iran's detailed demands have also emerged: $6 billion in frozen assets, recognition of the right to charge Hormuz transit tolls, a Lebanon ceasefire, and nuclear rights recognition — a framework structurally incompatible with Vance's nuclear formulation (source: Washington Post, CNBC, Time). The IRGC's 18+ hours of silence from military commanders persists — no denunciation of talks, no call for escalation. Fars News (IRGC-linked) ran "the Americans needed talks only to restore their lost international image" — dismissive but NOT destructive, and critically NOT an attack on Ghalibaf for negotiating. Iran has NOT formally responded to the "final offer" document itself — the silence on the specific proposal preserves optionality while the "no hurry" framing establishes leverage for the remaining 10 ceasefire days.

### AIS evidence now favours the US completing the Hormuz transit — while Trump escalated to "out-blockade" rhetoric and a leading energy analyst projected commercial escort operations by late April.

The morning brief reported conflicting US-Iran accounts of Saturday's mine clearing transit. Two data points have shifted the balance. Fortune and WSJ report that the USS Michael Murphy activated its AIS transponder on the far side of the strait — an expert assessed this was "purposeful messaging about having sailed through." WSJ also obtained a radio recording of the IRGC issuing "this is the last warning" during the transit — which undermines Iran's claim the ships turned back, since a warning implies ongoing transit, not reversal (source: Fortune, WSJ). Iran's denial has sharpened: spokesperson Zolfaghari told state TV the CENTCOM claim is "strongly denied" and that "the initiative for the passage of any vessel lies with the armed forces of the Islamic Republic of Iran" (source: IRIB). Separately, Trump posted on Truth Social proposing to "out-blockade" Iran — RHETORIC until CENTCOM announces blockade operations, but the intent signal is significant (source: Jerusalem Post). Bob McNally of Rapidan Energy Group assessed the mine clearing is Phase 1: with underwater drones arriving in days, the US could reduce IRGC control to "manageable levels" enabling commercial escort operations by late April — a timeline that coincides with ceasefire expiry (source: Fortune). Falsifiable: if correct, VLCC rates and war risk insurance should begin declining within 10-14 days.

Updated data shows urea at $838/mt, up from the $693/mt cited in this morning's brief (note: the morning figure used FinancialContent/Barchart sourcing; the updated figure uses DTN/Argus assessments — the discrepancy likely reflects different assessment timing rather than a single-session move, as CF Industries +1.6% and Nutrien +1.3% on Friday were not consistent with a same-day 21% urea surge) — now +68% above pre-crisis baseline, making fertiliser the single most honest real-economy indicator of whether the Hormuz closure is resolving (source: DTN, Argus — three independent sources).
