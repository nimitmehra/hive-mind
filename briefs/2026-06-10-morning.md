## I. What Happened

### Israel crossed the Beirut red-line, Iran fired its first missile salvo at Israel since April — and then both sides stood down within 24 hours, after Trump forced Netanyahu to call off a second strike. [NEW — the 7 June brief's "red-line held" status reversed within hours of publication]
On 7 June, after Hezbollah fired rockets near Yiftach, Israel struck what Netanyahu and Katz jointly called a "Hezbollah command centre" in **Beirut's southern Dahiyeh suburb**, killing at least two — the first attack on the Lebanese capital since the truce was renewed (CONFIRMED — Al Jazeera, NPR, Axios). The IRGC declared Israel had crossed "all red lines," and from ~22:00 local fired ballistic missiles at Israel — the **first direct Iranian strike on Israeli territory since 8 April** (CONFIRMED salvo; ISW counted ~10 missiles intercepted, the GlobalSecurity tracker logged ~30 across the night, most plausibly later waves). **Iranian missiles penetrated Israeli defences, with civilian casualties reported across several communities, though the toll is unconfirmed** (REPORTED — sources span "all intercepted" to "multiple fatalities"; widely circulated specific incidents are March events and are not used here). On 8 June Israel struck back deep inside Iran — Tehran, Isfahan, Tabriz, Kermanshah and the Mahshahr petrochemical complex (which the IDF said makes "unique materials critical for Iran's ballistic-missile program") — and Tasnim made the rare admission that ~20,000 Iranians lost drinking water after reservoir strikes (REPORTED). Then the reversal: the IRGC announced a suspension ("Israel has learned a lesson"), Netanyahu acknowledged the halt while vowing to respond "with force" if attacked — and **Israeli press across the spectrum reported that Netanyahu had approved a larger second operation and called it off after Trump warned Israel "would be on its own"** (REPORTED — multi-outlet, anonymously sourced). Trump's own framing: "Each of them had their fun… We don't need another one." A 60-day ceasefire extension remains unsigned, awaiting changes Trump has demanded.

### Iran downed a US Apache over Hormuz, the US struck ~20 Iranian coastal sites in reply, and this morning Iran hit US bases in Bahrain, Kuwait and — for the first time — Jordan, claiming heavy damage the US, Jordan and Kuwait all deny. [NEW — a direct US-Iran kinetic exchange, separate from the Israel axis]
A US Army Apache patrolling the Strait went down overnight on 8 June; two officials told CBS an armed Iranian Shahed drone struck it, **though whether it was deliberately targeted is undetermined** (CONFIRMED downing and rescue — both crew unharmed, recovered in the first-ever combat water rescue by an unmanned surface vessel; the attribution to Iran originated with Trump's own statements and was subsequently corroborated). On 9 June CENTCOM launched "self-defense strikes" on ~20 Iranian air-defence, radar and ground-control sites near Hormuz — which a US official pointedly told CNN was "a warning shot" that "won't hinder war talks." Early this morning the IRGC retaliated against the **US Fifth Fleet HQ in Bahrain, the Ali Al Salem base in Kuwait, and — for the first time in this war — the Azraq base in Jordan.** The IRGC **claims** 21 targets struck, four destroyed including an F-35 hangar, plus a downed MQ-9; against that, **a US official reported no injuries or damage, Jordan's military said it intercepted all five missiles with no casualties, and Kuwait said it downed "hostile aerial targets"** (CONFIRMED launches and interceptions; CLAIMED damage, denied across the board). This is the third iteration of an established pattern — spectacular claims, intercepted reality, zero casualties — performative deterrence calibrated to demonstrate reach without killing Americans. The genuinely new element is **Jordan**: a non-Gulf Arab US ally, widening the target geography to the Levant. The Arab League condemned the "brutal" attacks; as after every prior round, there were no expulsions or downgrades.

### The market's regime call survived its hardest possible test: gold fell 2.1% and Brent barely moved through a state-on-state missile exchange, US strikes on Iran, and Iranian strikes on three US-allied capitals. [UPDATED — extends the 7 June "rates-not-war" thesis with its strongest data point yet]
At the 9 June US close — *during* the most kinetic week since April — **gold fell to $4,168.90 (−2.1% on the day, −11.7% on the month), its lowest since ~23 March and ~$430 *below* the $4,600 regime trigger it is moving away from**; Brent closed $91.08 (−0.4%), drawing only a +0.9% bid this morning on the three-country strike; and the **VIX sat at 20.93 — below its >22 trigger through everything** (CONFIRMED — market data). The paradox to hold is that gold is falling *because of* the Iran shock, not despite it: the shock pushes oil → inflation → real yields and the dollar up, and that dominates any safe-haven bid. What is actually driving the tape is a **Fed *hike* repricing** — market-implied odds of a December rate *hike* have jumped to ~70% from ~45% a week ago on the hot May jobs print and oil-fed inflation, lifting the dollar (DXY ~99.9) and pinning the 10Y at 4.52%. The corollary is the one the investor must carry: with **essentially no war premium left to unwind anywhere**, the asymmetry has flipped — the unpriced risk is no longer the conflict continuing, but an *uncalibrated* shock (US deaths, Hormuz mining, a Beirut-scale strike on Tehran) hitting a market positioned for choreography.

Why it is the signal and not the noise: this metal had the most defensible war premium on the board, and it de-priced *during* the US-Iran strikes. **But it is also the most reversible move on the board** — because unlike gold, the underlying shortage is real. A confirmed ALBA/EGA non-restart, a re-hit smelter, or Hormuz mining would reverse it violently. A new graph trigger now watches exactly that.
