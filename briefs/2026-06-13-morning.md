## I. What Happened

### Trump set a date — the US-Iran deal "will be signed Sunday" — but the "final text" is contested, the Supreme Leader's approval is third-hand, and the enrichment red line is still unresolved.
A day after announcing a "great settlement," Trump said the deal "will be signed on Sunday," possibly in Europe, and that he "understands" Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei has approved the memorandum of understanding (source: Fox, CBS). Negotiators, with Pakistani mediation, reportedly agreed a final *draft* text on 12 June, structured in two parts — an MoU covering the ceasefire, Hormuz and the blockade, followed later by a fuller peace deal. But the optimism rests on contested ground: Iranian officials call the reports "preliminary," and the deal remains unsigned. This is the ninth "days away" claim of the war — the first with a specific date attached, but also the first where the text two parties call "final" demonstrably is not. The signing date is Trump's stated intention; the agreement is not yet a fact.

### The two claims holding the deal up are the two weakest in the file: a Supreme Leader "approval" relayed third-hand, and a "final text" both sides are publishing differently.
Trump's claim that Khamenei approved the MoU is attributed to an intermediary, not direct confirmation, and Tehran has not corroborated it — a problem compounded by the fact that Mojtaba Khamenei, who took power after his father's 28 February assassination, has reportedly been in hiding since, communicating by courier with multi-day delays (source: wire reports). Meanwhile Iranian state media published a draft with materially different terms — US force withdrawals, reconstruction funding — that Trump said "did not reflect the agreed terms," and a sitting Iranian lawmaker, Meisam Zohurian, called the parliament-circulated text "irresponsible" for implying recognition of enrichment (source: CNBC, Iran International). Two governments announcing a "final, agreed text" while publishing different documents on the hardest issues is, by definition, an unfinished negotiation. Watch whether a single agreed text is actually published on Sunday — that, not the rhetoric, is the test.

### Even a signed deal would leave a structurally higher oil floor — Iran keeps a Hormuz "service fee," and the strait must be demined before flows normalize.
The reported terms reopen Hormuz "immediately without tolls" with pre-war volumes restored within ~30 days and the US blockade lifted — but Foreign Minister Araghchi was explicit that while international law bars "tolls," Iran will collect "service fees," and the Persian Gulf Strait Authority has enforced a ~$1-per-barrel charge since 5 May (source: The National). Trump, for his part, called a Hormuz fee "a beautiful thing." Analysts add that even a breakthrough won't normalise oil quickly: a subsequent deal phase is explicitly demining the waterway, and idled fields must restart and damaged facilities be repaired before crude flows fully return (source: CNBC). For the investor this caps the downside in oil — a "buy-the-news" collapse below the pre-war baseline is unlikely to hold — and means India's full import-cost relief arrives over weeks, not on Monday.

### The guns largely went quiet into the signing window — no major new US-Iran strikes — but Lebanon is the clause that could break the deal even if it signs.
After Trump cancelled the threatened third strike round, the ceasefire "largely held, with attacks infrequent and with restraint" through 12-13 June; no major new US strike on Iran or Iranian strike on the Gulf was confirmed (source: NBC, Al Jazeera). The reported deal writes in an end to fighting in Lebanon — an Iranian condition for lastingly ending the war — but Israel, which sits outside the negotiations and has repeatedly broken prior Lebanon ceasefires, has made no commitment to it. That makes the Lebanon clause the clearest near-term obstacle to the deal *holding* even if it is *signed*: a single Israeli strike in southern Lebanon could give Tehran's hardliners the pretext they are already seeking.

### Markets are closed — Friday's relief rally carries into a pivotal weekend, and Sunday's signing is Monday's binary.
With Indian and US markets shut on Saturday, Friday's de-escalation repricing stands as the operative tape: Brent down ~6% on the week to ~$87, the VIX collapsed to 17.68, gold up on the inflation-headwind unwind (not a haven bid), and the Nifty up 2.0% on the importer dividend. The market is positioned for roughly 80% odds of a signing; because the confirmation is largely priced, the asymmetry now favours the *downside* on a slip — a contested text or an enrichment blow-up reverses Friday's move harder than a clean signing extends it. The physical war-risk complex (VLCC rates, ~7-10% hull insurance) has *not* unwound, which is the market's own quiet vote that this is still a probability, not a fact.

Brent's downside on the news is therefore capped above the ~$73 pre-war baseline. **Falsifiable:** if Brent gaps below ~$80 and holds on a signing, the read is wrong; if it stalls in the mid-$80s, it holds.
