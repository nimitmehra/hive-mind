## I. What Happened

### Iran's civilian government issued its first conciliatory signal since the war began — triggering a global risk rally built entirely on rhetoric that tonight's IRGC corporate threat deadline could reverse within hours.

Iranian President Pezeshkian told EU Council President António Costa that Iran possesses "the necessary will" to end the conflict — the most conciliatory language from Iran's civilian government in 33 days of war (source: IRNA via Bloomberg, AFP). Markets reacted violently: S&P 500 surged +2.91% (best day since May), NASDAQ +3.83%, KOSPI +7.9%, VIX collapsed -17.5% to 25.25, and Brent crashed -11.3% from $118.35 to ~$105 — the largest single-session oil decline since March 23's strike-pause rally (source: Yahoo Finance, CNBC). Trump added fuel by stating the US could end the war in "two to three weeks," and Pakistan became the first mediator to publicly confirm on record that "US-Iran indirect talks are taking place" — a Rubio-Araghchi meeting "within days" (source: US News, Pakistan FM office). But the de-escalation rests on three fragile legs: Pezeshkian attached conditions including reparations, sovereignty over Hormuz, and ending all sanctions — each individually a dealbreaker; the IRGC simultaneously issued threats against 18 named US companies (Apple, Microsoft, Google, Nvidia, JPMorgan, Boeing, Tesla) with a deadline of tonight 20:00 Tehran time (~22:00 IST); and Iran's FM spokesperson continues to deny any negotiations with America (source: Fars News, Tasnim, The Hill, Tasnim). The March 23 strike-pause rally reversed completely within five days. Pattern favors reversal.

### The Hormuz decoupling paradigm deepened — CNN corroborated that Trump is willing to end the war without reopening the chokepoint, Iran's parliament is drafting toll legislation, and the Al-Salmi VLCC attack demonstrated Iran's reach into allied waters.

CNN independently confirmed the Wall Street Journal's March 31 bombshell: the Trump administration "believes it will have to end Iran war with oil chokepoint still closed" (source: CNN, WSJ, Bloomberg). This breaks the assumption that war resolution equals Hormuz reopening equals oil normalization. Iran's parliament is now drafting toll legislation to formalize selective Hormuz control — potentially making the IRGC's yuan-denominated escort system permanent regardless of any ceasefire (source: Bloomberg). Meanwhile, an Iranian drone struck the Kuwait-flagged VLCC Al-Salmi at Dubai's port anchorage zone on March 31, hitting a fully laden vessel carrying approximately 2 million barrels of Saudi and Kuwaiti oil bound for China — the most significant single-vessel attack of the war, and the first targeting neutral shipping in a Gulf ally's waters rather than at the Strait itself (source: Bloomberg, Gulf News, Al Jazeera, Maritime Executive). All 24 crew members were confirmed safe, no oil spill. Concurrent attacks hit a Greek container ship off Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura and a Thai cargo ship near Hormuz (3 crew missing). The US Strategic Petroleum Reserve, flowing at 1.0-1.5M bpd from the IEA's 400M barrel coordinated release, has dropped to 243 million barrels — the lowest since 1984 — and is failing to contain prices above $100 (source: DOE). The structural implication: even if the war ends tomorrow, Brent's floor may be $90-100, not $70. Today's -11.3% crash (from $118.35 to ~$105) is the largest single-session decline since March 23 — but it landed at $105, not below $100.

On the first trading day of FY27, Nifty gapped up to ~22,550 and is trading near 22,800 (+2.1%), joining the global de-escalation trade alongside S&P 500 (+2.9%) and KOSPI (+7.9%) (source: 5paisa, Yahoo Finance, Seoul Economic Daily). Eurostat's flash estimate for March showed Eurozone inflation surging from 1.9% to 2.5%, driven by an energy component that swung from -3.1% to +4.9% — an 8-percentage-point reversal in a single month (source: Eurostat, CNBC).
