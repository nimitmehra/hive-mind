## I. What Happened

### Iran turned the Hormuz blockade into a precision diplomatic weapon — allowing Indian, Chinese, and Japanese tankers through while blocking US allies.
FM Araghchi stated Hormuz is "open to vessels linked to countries not supporting US-Israeli aggression" (Press TV). Indian tankers Shivalik and Nanda Devi passed through safely after Jaishankar-Araghchi diplomatic channel was established (Wikipedia/India in the 2026 Iran war). This is not a humanitarian gesture — it is the most strategically sophisticated move of the war. By offering passage to India, China, Japan, and Pakistan while keeping it closed to Western ships, Iran is simultaneously maintaining some oil/gas revenue, ensuring the nations with the most leverage over Washington have less incentive to support the US war effort, and creating a wedge between the US and its Asian partners. If non-aligned nations can get oil through, Washington can no longer claim a "global energy crisis." It transforms the blockade from a blunt instrument into a coalition-splitting tool.

### Iran publicly denies all talks with Trump — but privately admitted receiving US messages through mediators, and they are "being reviewed."
While every Iranian state outlet (IRNA, Tasnim, Fars) called Trump's "productive conversations" claim fake, a CBS News exclusive reported a senior Iranian FM official said "we received points from the US through mediators and they are being reviewed." This is the day's most important diplomatic signal. Trilateral mediation by Turkey, Egypt, and Pakistan is confirmed by multiple independent sources — foreign ministers from all three have been holding separate talks with US envoy Steve Witkoff and Iran's FM Araghchi, with Islamabad proposed as a meeting venue (Pakistan Today, Axios, NBC News). Trump claimed 15 points of agreement and an April 9 target date, with Kushner and Witkoff leading the US side (NBC News, Bloomberg). The dispute is framing: Trump calls it "talks," Iran calls it "message relay." Both are partially true. This is the classic pattern of a regime that might negotiate but cannot be seen to negotiate.

### Oil rebounded 3.6% as yesterday's "talks" narrative collapsed — Iran fired fresh missiles at Tel Aviv while calling Trump a liar.
Brent climbed back to $103.56 after yesterday's overdone 8.4% crash, with WTI surging 4.8% to $92.39 (yfinance). The rebound came as Iran's Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf called Trump's claims "fakenews used to manipulate financial and oil markets" (Al Jazeera), the IRGC branded Trump a "deceitful American president" (NPR), and Iranian missiles struck multiple areas of Tel Aviv causing major building damage and at least 4 casualties (CNN). Chevron CEO Mike Wirth said oil effects are "not fully priced in" (Fortune). The VIX rose 4.9% to 27.43 — fear is returning. Yesterday's de-escalation optimism lasted less than 24 hours.

### India's real economy is now being hit: PMI slowed to lowest since October 2022, and Delhi is quietly approaching Beijing for emergency urea.
India's March PMI reading dropped to its lowest since October 2022, showing that the war's impact has moved beyond markets into the real economy (CNBC). The IPO market is frozen. FPIs sold ₹82,700 crore in March — the largest monthly outflow since October 2024. Business Today ran a headline calling the crisis "second only to Covid" in terms of market losses (₹48 lakh crore wiped since war began). Most revealing: India is quietly approaching China for emergency urea supply while publicly denying any shortage (BigStory Network). Domestically, three fertilizer plants have reduced output after LNG feedstock was cut 30% due to Gulf disruptions. Restaurants are shifting to firewood as LPG prices rise.

### Asian markets bounced from Monday's carnage but US markets slipped — the whipsaw continues.
KOSPI surged 2.7% (recovering from Monday's 6.5% crash), Hang Seng +2.8%, Nifty +1.8% to 22,912, Sensex +1.9% (yfinance). But US markets fell: S&P 500 -0.5% to 6,551, NASDAQ -0.5%, as yesterday's relief rally evaporated. European markets also weak: DAX -1.0%. The time-zone whipsaw reversed direction — Asia caught the bounce from de-escalation headlines, while US markets absorbed the reality that strikes continue and talks may not exist.

### Houthis: rhetoric escalated again, but 25 days in, still zero confirmed attacks.
Houthi politburo member al-Bukhaiti said "all options are possible" (March 20) and the group's Saba news agency said it "will not remain on the sidelines" (March 22) — but every maritime tracker (Skuld, UKMTO), military source (CENTCOM, IDF), and shipping insurer confirms: zero attacks since the war began (Maritime Executive, Foreign Policy, The National). Leader al-Houthi himself adopted an "unusually calm tone" and "refrained from pledging military action" (Yemen Monitor). Analysts cite depleted stockpiles, fear of Israeli strikes on Hodeidah, and US warnings via Oman (Soufan Center, Foreign Policy). Saudi Arabia is actively working to keep them out (WSJ via Jerusalem Post). Physical preparations are real but the threat is generating "value without expenditure" — the posture itself is the weapon (Foreign Policy). Worth monitoring, not yet worth acting on.

### The Signal You Might Miss
**Iran's selective Hormuz opening is the most strategically sophisticated move of the war.** By allowing Indian, Chinese, and Japanese tankers through while blocking US allies, Iran is doing three things simultaneously: (1) reducing economic pressure on itself by maintaining some oil/gas revenue, (2) ensuring the nations with the most leverage over Washington (India as swing state, China as creditor, Japan as treaty ally) have less incentive to support the US war effort, and (3) creating a wedge between the US and its Asian partners. India is already walking a tightrope — Jaishankar negotiated tanker passage with Araghchi while Modi's government has not condemned any party. If this selective opening holds, it fundamentally changes the pressure dynamics: Washington can't claim a "global energy crisis" if the world's two largest importers (China, India) are getting their oil through. This is in the graph now as an updated signal on Iran→strait-of-hormuz, but the strategic implications are 2nd-order effects that haven't been widely analyzed.

### Risk Landscape
**Intensifying:**
- Houthi threat is the single biggest latent risk — rhetoric escalating, preparations real, but zero attacks in 25 days. The threat itself is generating "value without expenditure." If they cross from threat to action, double chokepoint would be unprecedented.

**Potentially stabilizing (fragile):**

- Iran's selective Hormuz opening for non-aggressor nations reduces total global supply disruption even if the blockade continues.
- Asian market bounce suggests some of the panic selling is exhausted.

**What would need to happen for capital deployment:**
1. Hormuz reopening confirmed by shipping data — tanker transit count rising to 40+/day (currently 2-3/day), not just rhetoric
2. Houthis standing down — no Red Sea attacks within 2 weeks of today's announcement
3. Oil below $85 sustained for 2+ weeks (currently $103.56, nowhere close)
4. Rupee stabilizing below ₹90 (currently ₹93.80)
5. FPI selling pace declining to <₹2,000 crore/week (currently ₹20,000+ crore/week)
6. At least one of: Trump extends pause beyond March 28, or Iran publicly acknowledges negotiations

None of these conditions are close to being met. The March 28 deadline is the nearest catalyst — outcome determines whether this crisis inflects toward resolution or spirals further.
