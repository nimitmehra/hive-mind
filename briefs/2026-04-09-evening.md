## What Changed Since Morning

### Three Chinese VLCCs are testing the Strait of Hormuz — the first laden crude tankers to attempt exit since the ceasefire, and the real-time operational verdict on whether the deal has legs.

The Cospearl Lake (Cosco Shipping Corp), He Rong Hai, and Yuan Hua Hu moved east toward Hormuz at near-top speed on Thursday morning before halting near the strait. All three are broadcasting Chinese ownership on their AIS tracking systems — a move typically done during Iran-approved transits (source: Bloomberg ship-tracking, Rigzone, Insurance Journal). If Cospearl Lake completes passage, it would be the first laden crude tanker to exit the Persian Gulf since the ceasefire. Bloomberg data confirms only 3 ships exited on Day 1. The broader picture has worsened: 479+ vessels remain stranded in the Gulf — including 426 tankers, 34 LPG carriers, and 19 LNG ships per MarineTraffic; some estimates including all commercial vessel classes put the total above 800 (OilPrice.com, Gulf News). The IRGC toll system remains in force: vessels must coordinate with Iranian armed forces and receive permission. The ceasefire text formalises this. Whether the IRGC permits these Chinese tankers through is tomorrow's first binary before the Islamabad talks and CPI even begin.

### Russia and China vetoed Bahrain's UN Security Council resolution to keep Hormuz open — closing the multilateral military-escort pathway and strengthening Iran's hand at Islamabad.

Bahrain sponsored a UNSC resolution authorising defensive missions to keep Hormuz open to international shipping. Russia and China vetoed it (source: Soufan Center, Al Jazeera). The UAE's UN representative stated the strait "cannot become a bargaining chip for Iran." This closes the multilateral forced-reopening option: any Hormuz resolution will now be bilateral (US-Iran at Islamabad) or unilateral (Iran's IRGC toll system). Both Russia and China benefit from the status quo — Russia from higher oil prices funding its war effort, China from preferential yuan-denominated access through the toll system. The veto materially strengthens Iran's negotiating position heading into Friday: there is no Plan B if Islamabad fails.

### Islamabad delegations are confirmed — Vance leads the US side, Ghalibaf leads Iran's, and the enrichment red line is already drawn.

The White House confirmed VP JD Vance leads the US delegation with Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner. Iran sends Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Araghchi. Bloomberg and Axios report formal talks begin Saturday morning local time, not Friday as Pakistan initially announced — suggesting preparatory diplomatic activity Friday, substantive negotiations Saturday (source: Bloomberg, Axios, Tribune India, Al Jazeera). Three red lines are already visible: enrichment (White House Press Secretary Leavitt called Iran's 440 kg of 60% enriched uranium "a red line the President is not going to back away from"), Lebanon scope (the three-way contradiction persists — Iran and Pakistan say included, US and Israel say excluded), and Hormuz sovereignty (Iran wants a permanent toll regime, US wants unconditional reopening). Axios reports Vance offered Israel an arrangement to restrain Lebanon strikes during talks — an acknowledgement that Operation Eternal Darkness is the deal's most immediate threat.

### A diplomatic memo claims Mojtaba Khamenei is unconscious in Qom — intelligence shared with Gulf allies, but ALL reporting traces to ONE source.

A memo reportedly based on American and Israeli intelligence and shared with Gulf allies claims Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei (56) is "unconscious and receiving treatment" in Qom and "unable to be involved in any decision making" (source: Jerusalem Post, Times of Israel, Ynet, IBT Singapore, GB News — ALL tracing to the same single memo). No Iranian confirmation or denial. On April 8, a written ceasefire statement attributed to Mojtaba was read on state TV, but this is identical in format to all previous communications since his March 8 appointment — written documents read by others, ZERO video, audio, or verified physical appearance in 42 days. If true, this is the most consequential intelligence of the war: Iran's Supreme Leader is a figurehead while the IRGC and civilian government fight for control, explaining the IRNA-Tasnim divergence perfectly. If false, it is an information operation designed to undermine Iranian command credibility before Islamabad. Tag: CLAIMED — one source amplified by many outlets does not become multiple sources.

### Saudi East-West Pipeline damage was LIMITED — Yanbu loadings uninterrupted, the Hormuz bypass holds.

Bloomberg reported that the Saudi East-West pipeline suffered only limited damage from the drone strike on its pumping station. Crude flows continued. Loadings at Yanbu — the pipeline's Red Sea terminus and the critical alternative to Hormuz-dependent Gulf ports — were NOT interrupted (source: Bloomberg, Investing.com). This morning's brief covered the strike; this evening's data de-escalates its significance. The last major non-Hormuz export route from the Eastern Province is intact.

### Lloyd's Market Association clarified the real Hormuz bottleneck: it's safety, not insurance. Even free coverage wouldn't restart traffic.

Neil Roberts (LMA): "The reason ships are not moving is not through a lack of insurance; it is a question of the risk to crew and vessel safety being assessed by the ship masters and owners as too high." War risk premiums are now 1.5-3.0% of hull value (updated from the 0.35-0.45% range cited this morning), with the higher end for US/UK/Israeli-linked vessels. 88% of Lloyd's underwriters still have appetite to write hull war risks (source: Lloyd's List, LMA, Maritime Executive). The US launched a $20 billion reinsurance facility — but as Roberts's clarification makes plain, this addresses the wrong bottleneck. Ships aren't staying put because they can't get insured; they're staying put because their masters won't sail into what Bloomberg's AIS data confirms remains a largely blocked strait.
