## I. What Happened

### Iran fired its first retaliatory missile of the cycle — one shot at a US airbase, intercepted over Kuwait, zero casualties — and immediately kept negotiating. [NEW — CONFIRMED ACTION; the dominant event; reverses the May 27 non-retaliation thesis]
Early Thursday May 28, US forces shot down four Iranian one-way attack drones near the Strait of Hormuz and struck an IRGC ground-control station near Bandar Abbas that the Pentagon said was "reportedly preparing to launch a fifth" — the second US "defensive" strike in three days, framed as "measured, purely defensive, and intended to maintain the ceasefire" (CONFIRMED — CENTCOM via CNN, NBC, WaPo, PBS). In response, at roughly 04:50 local the IRGC said it had "targeted" the "American airbase, as the origin of the aggression"; CENTCOM confirmed Iran launched a ballistic missile **toward Kuwait, which Kuwaiti air defenses intercepted**, calling it an "egregious ceasefire violation" — **no damage or casualties were reported** (CONFIRMED — CENTCOM, Kuwaiti MoD, Euronews). The IRGC called it "a serious warning… any act of aggression will not go unanswered," holding a "more decisive" response in reserve — escalation-managing language, not all-out-war language. This both breaks and confirms yesterday's read: Iran *did* retaliate (breaking the "can absorb a strike without responding" thesis) but did so via a single, telegraphed, intercepted, zero-casualty missile at a base — the Ain al-Assad (2020) playbook — and reverted to the table. Tehran minimised the US strike in parallel (Tasnim: the US "fired at a barren area near Bandar Abbas," and the IRGC Navy fired a "warning shot at an American oil tanker" — CLAIMED, no corroboration). The structural fact every wire desk converges on (WaPo, PBS, Washington Times): across seven weeks the two have repeatedly traded strikes and accusations **without returning to full-scale war, and keep negotiating.** Treat this as a tit-for-tat inside a violated-but-holding ceasefire — not its end.

### The deal hardened into deadlock: a 60-day ceasefire-extension MOU reportedly awaits Trump's signature, but Trump ruled out sanctions relief and the nuclear file is now stuck. [UPDATED — material negative shift from "largely negotiated"]
Reporting on May 28 says the US and Iran have **reached an MOU to extend the ceasefire 60 days — but Trump has not approved it** (REPORTED — US News, single source, consistent with prior Axios/WaPo reporting); under it, Hormuz would be open "with no tolls," Iran would clear its mines, and the US would lift its blockade and issue "some sanctions waivers." Against that, Trump hardened publicly at the May 27 cabinet meeting — **"No sanctions, no money, no nothing"** — insisting Iran's ~1,000 lbs of highly enriched uranium "will be destroyed" and that he opposes Russia *or* China taking custody of it (CONFIRMED — PBS, CNBC). Separately, Iranian state TV published a draft framework — gradual Hormuz reopening within a month via Oman, US force withdrawal, blockade lifting — which the White House immediately dismissed as a **"complete fabrication"** ("Nobody should believe what Iranian state media is putting out") — the textbook one-side-framing pattern; treat Tehran's published terms as CLAIMED, not agreed (CLAIMED Iran / DENIED US — GlobalSecurity, CNN). Trump is also under pressure from Iran hawks in his own party not to sign a nuclear-light deal. Net: the ceasefire limb is incrementally more concrete (a document exists), while the nuclear and sanctions limbs hardened — and today's live exchange cuts against signature. Two hands of one coercive strategy, now with a sharper nuclear deadlock.
