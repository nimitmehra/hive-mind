## I. What Happened

### The deal held its first weekend — but the in-person Bürgenstock ceremony was cancelled, the first stumble in implementation.
A correction to yesterday's brief, which described the in-person ceremony as having run: the **Swiss Foreign Ministry confirmed the Bürgenstock signing event and implementation talks were cancelled** (source: Swiss FM; Iran International flagged it as the deal's "first setback"). Crucially, this does **not** unwind the agreement — the 14-point MoU was already signed electronically and its full text published (Pezeshkian on X, NBC, i24), which is what makes the deal real. What fell through was the ceremonial signing and the immediate start of implementation talks. So the deal is intact and public, but its execution stumbled at the first step. The substantive test now is whether the implementation talks are rescheduled and proceed — not the ceremony itself, which was always theatre layered on an already-signed text.

### The Lebanon ceasefire was renewed after a flare-up — holding, but fragile, with Israeli troops staying to the Litani.
The Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire that took effect Friday survived its first test: after a flare-up in southern Lebanon (reported four Israeli soldiers and dozens of Lebanese casualties around the truce window), **Israel and Hezbollah renewed the ceasefire** (source: CNN, Times of Israel). But it is explicitly fragile — Israeli officials said they will "respond to Hezbollah's violations and do whatever is necessary," and **IDF troops remain in southern Lebanon up to the Litani River**. It is a managed pause, not a withdrawal, and it leaves Iran's stated condition (no continued occupation) unmet. For now the deal's most fragile external dependency is holding; a serious breach remains the likeliest route to trouble.

### A message attributed to the Supreme Leader voicing reservations stirred Tehran — but it reads as positioning, not repudiation.
A message **attributed to Iran's Supreme Leader (Mojtaba Khamenei) suggesting reservations** about the US agreement sparked a fierce debate in Tehran (source: NPR, Fox): hardline figures read it as proof the leader was sidelined in the negotiation; government supporters framed it as a roadmap for the next phase. US officials quoted say that while some hardliners want to kill the deal, **most actually want it** but are messaging reservations to domestic audiences for leverage. No MoU term has been repudiated. The signal matters mainly because of the persistent proof-of-life ambiguity around Mojtaba — a "message attributed to" an unseen Supreme Leader is exactly the kind of lever hardliners could use to dilute implementation. Watch whether it hardens into anything official.

### Hormuz is reopening, but only as a trickle — about seven ships since the deal.
The physical reopening is proceeding slowly: only **~7 ships have transited the strait since the deal was announced** (source: MarineTraffic; an earlier Starboard count showed ~13 — different trackers, both a trickle). The MoU's free-passage window (no tolls, 60 days) is confirmed and tankers/LNG carriers are expected to get priority, but the backlog will take 10-15 days to clear, full normalisation weeks to months, and many shipping firms have already restructured 2026 schedules around the Cape of Good Hope. The reopening is real and confirmed — but throughput remains a small fraction of pre-war while insurance stays elevated and naval checks are slow.
