## I. What Happened

### Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz "closed" again — but it is rhetoric: 55 ships transited, and oil fell on the news.
Three days after the strait reopened under the signed deal, Iran's IRGC (Khatam al-Anbiya central headquarters) **declared Hormuz closed again on 20 June**, blaming continued Israeli operations in southern Lebanon for violating the MoU, accusing the US of "clear bad faith," and calling it the "first step" with more to follow (source: Stars and Stripes, Seatrade, CNN). It is a declaration, not a blockade: **CENTCOM reported that 55 merchant ships carrying more than 17 million barrels transited that same day, with no Iranian military movements indicative of enforcement — "Iran does not control the Strait of Hormuz"** (source: CENTCOM via Tribune, OpIndia). The market's verdict was unambiguous — **Brent fell 1.2% to $76.99 and WTI 2.3% to $73.13**; a genuine closure would have spiked oil $20+. This is Iran weaponising Hormuz verbally as leverage over the Lebanon fighting, not physically closing it. The distinction is the whole point: rhetoric, not action.

### The Supreme Leader endorsed the deal in writing — resolving the weekend's "reservations" ambiguity in its favour.
The weekend's worry — a message attributed to the Supreme Leader voicing reservations — resolved positively: **Mojtaba Khamenei's 18 June written statement endorsed the memorandum** (source: negotiations timeline; consistent with US-official reads). The hardliner "reservations" framing was spin on a statement that actually backed the deal, materially strengthening its Iranian-side durability and boxing in the IRGC's closure rhetoric. The caveat is unchanged — it is a written statement, not a video, so the proof-of-life ambiguity around Mojtaba persists; but the deal now has the Supreme Leader's explicit backing on record, even as the IRGC pulls the Hormuz lever. That civilian-endorsement-versus-IRGC-defiance split is the clearest internal divergence of the war.

### The implementation talks were postponed over Lebanon — the 60-day nuclear clock has not started.
The Switzerland technical/nuclear talks were **postponed**, with the White House pulling VP Vance's trip and Switzerland confirming the delay — "hundreds of journalists already waiting in Lucerne" (source: CBS, Fox). PBS tied it directly to the Lebanon fighting: the US and Iran "called off talks over fighting in Lebanon." So the signed, SL-endorsed MoU stands, but the 60-day nuclear track — the hardest part — has not begun, delayed by the same Lebanon dependency Iran cites for its Hormuz rhetoric. A reschedule is the thing to watch; the nuclear clock's start is slipping.

### The Lebanon ceasefire is holding but fragile — Israel lifted border restrictions, yet clashes persist.
The ceasefire held the weekend with mixed signals: **Israel's Home Front Command lifted all restrictions on communities near the Lebanon border** — a concrete de-escalation — and Hezbollah has not struck Israeli territory since roughly last Sunday (source: Times of Israel). But Netanyahu and Katz ordered the IDF to **stay in occupied Lebanese territory** and hold fire everywhere except **Ali al-Taher Hill**, where IDF-Hezbollah clashes continue, and the IDF chief called the Lebanon goal "clear and unchanged." It is a real de-escalation layered over a live-fire pocket and a continued buffer-zone presence — fragile, holding, and the fulcrum on which Iran's Hormuz leverage and the postponed talks both turn.

### India outperformed a falling world — the FII turn and cheap oil finally produced the decoupling the thesis predicted.
The most important development for the investor was not in the Gulf. On a day the **S&P fell 1.4% and the semiconductor ETF dropped 7%** in an AI-valuation, hawkish-Fed selloff, **the Nifty rose 0.4% to 24,103 and the Sensex 0.4%** — India green while the US tape was red (source: yfinance). The driver: **foreign investors turned net buyers last week, infusing ~₹3,386 crore — the first net-positive FII week of the war** after months of selling — alongside sub-$77 crude (source: Business Standard, Goodreturns). For four months India lagged a risk-on world; today it rose while US tech sold off. That is the importer-dividend decoupling the brief has argued for, finally appearing now that the war risk is resolved. The caveat: it is one week of flows, not yet the five-consecutive-session turn, and the hawkish Fed remains the swing risk.
