## I. What Happened

### Vance and Ghalibaf are in the same building in Islamabad — the highest-level US-Iran engagement since 1979 — but in separate rooms, with preconditions that expose the gap between what each side can accept.

Both delegations arrived in Islamabad overnight. VP JD Vance — the highest-ranking US official to enter negotiations with Iranians since the 1979 hostage crisis — leads the US team alongside Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner with a 30-member security detail. Iran's Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf leads with FM Araghchi, SNSC Secretary Ahmadian, and Central Bank Governor Hemmati (source: Al Jazeera liveblog, PressTV, PBS, Axios — all confirmed). The format is PROXIMITY TALKS: both teams in the Serena Hotel but in separate rooms, with Pakistani officials shuttling messages — they will not sit face-to-face. Pakistan requisitioned the entire hotel, declared a two-day public holiday, and established a 3-kilometre red zone. Ghalibaf publicly set two preconditions before negotiations begin: (1) a ceasefire in Lebanon, and (2) the release of Iran's blocked assets — preconditions Israel has already rejected by continuing Lebanon strikes and Trump rejected by flatly stating "there will be no enrichment of uranium" (source: PressTV, Kurdistan24, NBC News). Pakistan's stated goal is explicitly modest: enough common ground to keep talks going, not a comprehensive deal. The negotiating basis is Iran's 10-point proposal (demanding continued Hormuz control, enrichment rights, sanctions lifting, no-aggression commitment) against a US 15-point proposal. The structural question flagged by every major think tank: reports suggest the IRGC has seized executive functions and sidelined President Pezeshkian — if true, does Ghalibaf actually have authority to commit Iran, or will any agreement face an IRGC veto? (source: Iran International, NewsX — REPORTED, single source cluster).

### March CPI resolved at 3.3% — below the feared 3.4% — and the market absorbed it without flinching, treating six weeks of $100+ oil as transitory war-inflation that a ceasefire will fix.

The first CPI print reflecting the full war-era oil shock landed on April 10 at 3.3% YoY headline — below the feared 3.4% upper estimate but above the 3.1% baseline consensus, a hot headline driven entirely by energy (source: BLS, confirmed CNBC, CBS News). Energy surged 10.9% monthly — gasoline alone rose 21.2%, the largest monthly increase since 1967 — accounting for nearly three-quarters of the entire headline print. Core CPI at 2.6% YoY and 0.2% monthly was benign, both below forecast. The market's reaction was remarkable for what didn't happen: S&P 500 fell just 0.1% (expected -0.5% to -1.0%), the 10Y yield rose a meagre 2.7bps to 4.32%, and gold barely moved at -0.4% (source: CBOE, Fed H.15, yfinance). The market separated headline from core with surgical precision — treating 3.3% as an oil-driven supply shock, not demand-pull inflation — and concluded the ceasefire will bring oil down, making the surge transitory. Rate hike fears are "off the table"; CME FedWatch shows June cut at 35% (from 55%), April hold at 98.4%. VIX compressed further to 19.23 — the lowest since pre-war February. This is the most dangerous positioning of the crisis: the market is one failed Islamabad away from the "transitory" narrative collapsing, and there is zero insurance in place.

### Israel and Hezbollah escalated simultaneously on both sides of the Lebanon border — 13 Lebanese State Security killed at Nabatieh, Hezbollah struck Ashdod at 200km strategic depth — threatening to hand the IRGC the exit case it needs to reject Islamabad.

Israel's strikes on April 10 crossed a qualitative threshold: the Nabatieh strike killed 13 Lebanese State Security personnel at a government building — the largest attack on that city since the war began and the first confirmed targeting of Lebanese government forces rather than Hezbollah (source: Al Jazeera, HRW). Hezbollah matched escalation with escalation: 100+ rockets since the ceasefire, 70 on April 10 alone, with the most significant being a reported strike on Ashdod Naval Base in central Israel — approximately 200km from the Lebanese border — if confirmed, a qualitative capability leap from border harassment to strategic depth (source: ANI, Sunday Guardian, Times of Israel). Haaretz military correspondents report a systemic gap between political rhetoric and operational reality: IDF intelligence assesses Hezbollah maintains a "solid military hold in southern Lebanon," the IDF has halted 10km south of the Litani and is "not planning to advance deeper," and senior officers say the force is "stretched to breaking point" (source: Haaretz military desk). Trump instructed Netanyahu to keep operations "low key" to avoid derailing Islamabad — the continued escalation suggests this instruction was ignored. The simultaneity is the strategy: every Israeli bomb on Lebanon gives Iran's IRGC faction — already building the Tasnim exit-case framing — a concrete, televised justification to walk away from the table.

### Hormuz traffic is declining post-ceasefire, not recovering — and the IRGC's published mine map creates a permanent mechanism for controlled passage that outlasts any deal.

Yesterday's brief reported approximately 3% of normal Hormuz traffic. The day-by-day data tells a sharper story: Ceasefire Day 1 (April 8): 11 ships including 9 tankers — a brief optimistic surge. April 9: collapsed to 5 cargo ships and zero oil or gas tankers. April 10: 6-7 vessels total (source: Kpler via NYT, The Week India, Al Jazeera). The three Chinese VLCCs that halted at the strait entrance on April 9 have still not completed transit. ADNOC CEO Sultan Al Jaber delivered the most authoritative industry statement yet: "The Strait of Hormuz is not open. Access is being restricted, conditioned and controlled. That is not freedom of navigation. That is coercion" (source: CNBC). Oman formally rejected the IRGC toll proposal, citing UNCLOS international maritime agreements — but no other nation has formally accepted it either (source: Oman FM). The most structurally significant development: the IRGC published a navigational map acknowledging mines deployed in or near the strait — the first official Iranian admission (source: Daily Caller, Maritime Executive). The map directs vessels toward Iranian-controlled corridors, citing mine avoidance — a fait accompli where even if political leaders in Islamabad agree to "free passage," the physical mines in the water give the IRGC permanent operational justification for escorted passage. Windward AI estimates approximately 3,200 vessels stranded west of Hormuz — far higher than the 800+ Bloomberg estimate (source: Windward). The toll regime's tiered pricing is confirmed: allies $0.50-0.70/bbl, neutral $0.80-1.00, hostile $1.20-1.50 — payment via yuan, Bitcoin, or USDT, entirely outside SWIFT.

### Saudi Aramco's war damage is now quantified — 600,000 bpd of production capacity lost, SATORP refinery shut, and Arab Light's record $19.50 premium signals structural supply tightness regardless of ceasefire.

The first comprehensive damage assessment of Saudi energy infrastructure has emerged. Arab Light OSP for May set at a record $19.50/bbl premium over Oman/Dubai — the highest ever — reflecting physical scarcity (source: OilPrice.com). Goldman Sachs revised Q2 Brent to $90 from $99, but with a critical caveat: if Hormuz stays closed one more month, Brent stays above $100 throughout all of 2026 (source: Investing.com, OilPrice.com). JPMorgan's earlier $150 Brent warning if Hormuz remains shut into mid-May is now less than five weeks away. The structural damage persists regardless of ceasefire — Saudi cannot surge production to compensate for the Hormuz constraint. Meanwhile, the physical-futures gap persists at $24+ ($120+ physical vs $94 futures) — the widest since 2008.
