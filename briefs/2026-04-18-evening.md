## I. What Happened

### IRGC gunboats opened fire on a tanker and two merchant vessels in the Strait of Hormuz — the first confirmed kinetic action on commercial shipping since the April 8 ceasefire.
UK Maritime Trade Operations (UKMTO) and the UK Ministry of Defence confirmed on Saturday afternoon IST that two Iranian Revolutionary Guard fast boats fired on a tanker roughly 20 miles (32 km) northeast of Oman; two other merchant vessels were separately fired upon while attempting to cross (sources: Irish Times, AP via WSLS, Haaretz, Al Jazeera all citing UKMTO — CONFIRMED). No injuries were reported and the vessels were not disabled — these were warning shots, not a disabling attack — but the escalation-ladder step is substantive: Baghaei's Friday "reciprocal measures" conditional has been converted into an operational action within 24 hours, and the pattern "threat → conditional → warning-shot kinetic" is the classic Iranian signalling ladder observed in 2019 against Saudi Aramco and in 2020 after Soleimani. CENTCOM has issued no public response as of the evening IST, and the flags, operators, and cargo of the three vessels are not yet publicly disclosed. The test over the next 72 hours is whether this stays a single-event warning shot — consistent with preserving the Lebanon ceasefire umbrella — or whether additional kinetic events escalate before Monday's reported Islamabad round.

### Within hours of the kinetic, the IRGC Joint Military Command declared Hormuz "returned to its previous state" under strict armed-forces control — institutionally overturning Araghchi's Friday "completely open" within 24 hours.
Iran's joint military command (Khatam al-Anbiya) issued a formal statement on Saturday morning declaring that "control of the Strait of Hormuz has returned to its previous state … under strict management and control of the armed forces," vowing to "continue to block transit through the strait as long as the US blockade of Iranian ports continues" and accusing Washington of "piracy" (sources: Irish Times, ABC7, Tribune India, PBS — CONFIRMED). Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf reinforced the "designated route" and "Iranian authorisation" framework, giving civilian-side cover to the IRGC's operational codification. Iran's Ministry of Transport separately said it would not impose "traditional fees" on Hormuz transit (ANI, Tribune India — CONFIRMED) — a deliberate off-ramp signal that full re-closure is not the immediate trajectory. The IRGC–civilian divergence the morning brief flagged as a factional opening has now been operationally resolved in the IRGC's favour: Araghchi's Friday declaration has not been retracted, but it is institutionally dead letter. Haaretz ran a Saturday headline attributing a "Navy's Ready" signal to Mojtaba Khamenei, which — if verbatim — marks the first direct Supreme Leader endorsement of a hardline operational posture since his March elevation (REPORTED, single-outlet; primary Persian-language statement not independently located).

### Reliance Industries rejected two Iranian oil cargoes hours before the waiver expires — the largest private Indian refiner has pre-positioned for post-waiver compliance, inverting the morning brief's "racing to the deadline" framing.
Reliance Industries publicly rejected the Iranian-origin tankers *Derya* and *Lenore* on Saturday, stating the cargoes "did not meet with the company's compliance requirements" (sources: MarineLink, Business Standard, Goodreturns citing the Reliance statement — CONFIRMED). This contradicts the morning brief's framing of Indian Oil and Reliance running yuan-settled cargoes jointly to the Sunday midnight UTC deadline. The operator of the world's largest single-location refining complex at Jamnagar (~1.4M bpd) is explicitly walking away from any cargo that could create sanctions exposure on Monday morning — consistent with the March 26 Bloomberg denial and with a policy that tracks US Treasury posture closely rather than defying it. State refiners have behaved differently: Indian Oil's 2M-barrel cargo on the *Jaya* — the first Indian Iranian crude import in seven years — has landed, and BPCL/HPCL have issued no reversal. The net read for Monday's Nifty open is that India's physical shortfall from the waiver expiry is smaller than this morning's dossier implied, but so is India's leverage in any US oil-supply discussion ("we're already complying"). Monday is still binary; the downside is slightly narrower and the upside is more contingent.

### Kpler's operational data confirms only about two oil and gas tankers have transited Hormuz since the April 8 ceasefire — the "normalisation" narrative never matched the data.
CNBC's Saturday update of Kpler data — the authoritative operational source — found that "just two oil or gas tankers have transited the Strait of Hormuz since the ceasefire was announced" (source: Kpler via CNBC, reconfirmed April 18 — CONFIRMED). This is dramatically below the eight-commercial-vessels figure that anchored the morning dossier's Friday framing, and it is roughly 1–2% of the pre-war ~120-vessels-per-day baseline. The mix matters: the broader week figure is ~9 tankers (including Iraq's *Ocean Thunder* on April 5, Chinese VLCCs *Cospearl Lake* and *He Rong Hai* on April 11, and *Shalamar* on April 16), but oil/gas specifically is the subset that matters for India, Korea, and the Chinese teapots. The AWRP has stayed at ~1% of hull for five straight days — Lloyd's syndicates never priced a step-down when Araghchi spoke. Taken together, insurance and Kpler both told the truth all week; Friday's −7.6% Brent crash was paper-market theatre built on a political declaration that had no operational backing. Saturday's IRGC kinetic makes that explicit.

### Trump reintroduced a "drop bombs" threat and reaffirmed the blockade is "in full force" — and "prohibited" Israeli strikes on Lebanon in a framing the State Department immediately narrowed to offensive action only.
On Saturday Trump told reporters and posted on Truth Social that the US would "drop bombs again" if a deal is not agreed and that the port blockade "is going to remain … maybe I won't extend [the ceasefire], but the blockade is going to remain" (sources: Irish Times, ABC7, NBC — CONFIRMED). This is meaningfully cooler than Friday's "very close" / "this weekend" deal optimism and re-establishes a coercive floor on the rhetoric. Separately, Trump posted that Israel is "prohibited" from further strikes on Lebanon, which drew immediate State Department clarification that the prohibition applies only to offensive action, not self-defence — a framework functionally identical to the ceasefire's existing self-defence clause and to Netanyahu's 10-km buffer-zone reading (source: AP via KGW, CBC — CONFIRMED). The operational effect on Israel's strike freedom is zero; the messaging effect is to give Trump a US-facing optic without constraining the IDF. The Kounine drone strike on Saturday — one killed, three wounded including a Syrian citizen — took place inside this "prohibition" (CBC, Lebanese Health Ministry — CONFIRMED). Haaretz's Friday "Trump hijacked Israeli foreign policy" framing remains the sharpest establishment critique, and the Smotrich–Ben-Gvir coalition bloc has still not walked out on Day 2.

### Rubio called Saudi FM Faisal bin Farhan Saturday on keeping Hormuz open — Saudi Arabia is positioning to be the post-ceasefire convener; Gulf states have not publicly condemned Iran's kinetic.
Secretary of State Rubio and Saudi FM Faisal bin Farhan discussed the Strait of Hormuz and Lebanon ceasefire consolidation on Saturday, with Middle East Eye reporting the two sides discussed "keeping the Strait of Hormuz open" (sources: Tribune India, ANI, Middle East Eye — CONFIRMED). It is the third confirmed Saudi–US direct engagement this week and the visible shape of the Gulf response: Saudi Arabia publicly coordinating with Washington, the UAE conspicuously quiet (consistent with its "we don't publicly react to Iran actions" doctrine), Oman silent (the quiet de-escalation mediator), and Qatar's Al Jazeera playing neutral broker. No Gulf state has publicly condemned Iran's Saturday action — the absence is itself a signal that the region prefers a US–Iran bilateral resolution to a Gulf-led confrontation. Pakistan's Army Chief Munir visited Tehran April 15–16 meeting Pezeshkian, Araghchi, and the IRGC's Khatam Al-Anbiya HQ; this was shuttle diplomacy, not Round 2 itself, and as of Saturday night IST no US delegation has publicly travelled to Islamabad. The Iranian-sourced Monday April 21 date (CNN) remains REPORTED, single-thread, and uncorroborated by Witkoff, Vance, or Kushner travel manifests.

### Houthis have not mirrored Iran's action — Day 52 of silence through the 18 hours after the UKMTO kinetic report is the most important Houthi non-event of the crisis.
UKMTO JMIC Advisory 031 confirms zero Houthi attacks between March 1 and April 12, and Saturday produced no new attack despite Iran's kinetic escalation in Hormuz (source: UKMTO — CONFIRMED non-event). This is the moment the Soufan Center's Iran–Houthi coordination thesis is most testable: if the Houthis were going to mirror Iran kinetically, the 18–24 hours after an IRGC warning-shot action is the window. They did not. Deputy information minister Mohammed Mansour's "closing Bab al-Mandeb is among our options" remains rhetoric; the forecastle-commitment has not come. If Houthi quiet persists through Tuesday IST while Iran's gunboat activity continues, the coordination thesis weakens meaningfully — and one of the tail risks priced into Friday's Frontline +5.6% rally and into VLCC rates sitting at ~$130K/day dilutes in kind.
