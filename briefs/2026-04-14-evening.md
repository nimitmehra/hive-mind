## What Changed Since Morning

### Two corrections undermine the morning brief's two most alarming claims — the crisis is real, but the morning overstated the operational chokepoints.

**Correction 1: War risk insurance was NOT suspended.** This morning's brief stated "Major P&I clubs suspended war risk insurance for Gulf transits entirely — the operational bottleneck that persists regardless of military or diplomatic outcomes." Lloyd's List explicitly reported this afternoon: "No, P&I clubs have not 'cancelled war risk cover.'" The Lloyd's Market Association clarified that "safety concerns, not insurance availability" are driving reduced vessel traffic, and 88% of Lloyd's marine war market "continue to have appetite to underwrite" hull war risks (source: Lloyd's List, LMA, Seatrade Maritime). Insurance IS available — but at $10-14M per VLCC transit (2.5-5% of hull value), a de facto barrier for most commercial operators even though the market exists. The distinction matters for the recovery timeline: insurance capacity exists and could normalize within weeks of a diplomatic breakthrough, whereas actual suspension would have required months to rebuild. The morning's framing was too pessimistic.

**Correction 2: M/T Rich Starry completed its transit.** The morning brief reported Rich Starry "reversed course off Qeshm Island." The tanker initially turned back but made a second attempt in the evening and appears to have passed through the Strait by early Tuesday UTC, carrying ~250,000 barrels of methanol loaded at Hamriyah (source: Bloomberg, Nikkei Asia, Al Jazeera, Lloyd's List). Al Jazeera reports "at least three vessels, including two US-sanctioned tankers" entered the Gulf on Day 1. Combined with the Elpis transit, the blockade's character is clearer than the morning assessed: the US is deterring legitimate commercial shipping but NOT interdicting sanctioned/shadow-fleet vessels. Iran's illicit oil network continues while legitimate trade is strangled — a perverse outcome that weakens blockade credibility significantly.

### Rubio will personally host April 15 Israel-Lebanon talks — the most high-level direct meeting since 1993.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio will personally attend and host tomorrow's Israel-Lebanon talks at 1 PM ET, elevating the session from ambassador-level to SecState-level (source: Axios, State Department, Times of Israel, Jerusalem Post). Participants: Rubio, senior adviser Michael Needham, US Ambassador to Lebanon Michel Issa, Israeli Ambassador Leiter, Lebanese Ambassador Hamadeh Moawad. Agenda: ceasefire, Hezbollah disarmament, full peace agreement. The structural obstacles are unchanged — Hezbollah's Qassem rejected the talks as "futile" and declared Hezbollah "will not abide by any agreements" — but Rubio's personal involvement signals the administration views this as a priority, potentially to secure a face-saving win after the Islamabad failure. The Iran connection: Ghalibaf named a Lebanon ceasefire as one of Iran's preconditions at Islamabad, meaning progress here could unlock the broader negotiation.

### Brent crept back toward the $100 trigger; VIX compressed further — the complacency gap widened through the session.

Brent settled at $99.19, up from the morning's $97.98 — the deal-hope selloff partially reversed as the afternoon session progressed, putting the $100 trigger back within one session's range (source: Bloomberg). VIX dropped to 18.30 from 19.12 (-4.3%), its lowest close since the war began. The morning brief flagged the market-reality divergence; it got worse. DXY weakened further to 98.07 (from 98.32), supporting the rupee offshore to Rs93.03 (from Rs93.28). INR strengthening is mechanical — dollar-driven, not structural — with FIIs still at Rs 1.79 lakh crore in CY2026 outflows.
