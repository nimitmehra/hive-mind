## I. What Happened

### Houthis entered the war with a ballistic missile strike on Israel — the first confirmed military action in 28 days, ending the longest rhetorical standoff of the conflict.

Yemen's Houthis launched a ballistic missile at southern Israel on Saturday morning, triggering air raid sirens across Beersheba and the Negev desert — their first offensive action since the war began on February 28 (source: IDF confirmed interception, no casualties; Houthi military spokesman Yahya Saree claimed responsibility via Al-Masirah TV, stating they targeted "sensitive Israeli military sites"). CENTCOM struck Houthi positions in Yemen in response. After 28 consecutive days of escalating rhetoric, physical preparations, and zero military action — a posture analysts called "generating value without expenditure" — the Houthis have now crossed the threshold from threat to force. The critical distinction: this was a missile at Israel, not a Red Sea shipping attack. The double-chokepoint scenario (Hormuz + Red Sea both closed to commercial shipping) has not materialized, but it is now one step away rather than theoretical. Michael Knights (Washington Institute) assesses this as a "calibration shot" — testing the response before escalating to shipping attacks, likely spacing provocations 5-10 days apart.

### Israel assassinated the IRGC Navy chief who was personally overseeing the Hormuz blockade — the highest-ranking kill since the war's opening strikes.

Israeli forces killed IRGC Navy commander Rear Admiral Alireza Tangsiri in a precision airstrike at 3:00 AM local time on March 26 in Bandar Abbas — the port city from which Tangsiri was personally directing the Strait of Hormuz closure (source: IDF and CENTCOM confirmed; Netanyahu stated in a video: "We eliminated the commander who led the closure of Hormuz"). A US admiral issued a public message to remaining IRGC Navy personnel: "Abandon your posts or die" (source: Gulf News). Iran has not officially confirmed Tangsiri's death — a notable silence, as Tehran typically denies false claims within hours. This is the highest-ranking Iranian military figure killed since the February 28 strikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. The strategic question: does decapitating the Hormuz commander disrupt IRGC naval operations, or does it harden resolve? Karim Sadjadpour (Carnegie) argues the latter — "decapitation strikes create martyrs, not compliance."

### Iran rejected the US 15-point ceasefire plan and issued a counterproposal so maximalist it effectively closes the near-term diplomatic path.

Iran's Foreign Minister Araghchi publicly rejected the US ceasefire plan on March 25, calling it "extremely maximalist and unreasonable" and issuing a 5-point counterproposal: end all aggression, guarantee no future war, pay reparations, end hostilities including all resistance groups, and recognize Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz (sources: NPR, CNBC, PBS, Al Jazeera, The Hill). Most significantly, Araghchi stated on state TV: "We do not want a ceasefire. We want the war to end in a way that it does not repeat, on our own terms" — explicitly rejecting the concept of a pause in favor of a total capitulation framework. Pakistan's FM Ishaq Dar publicly confirmed that "US-Iran indirect talks are taking place through messages being relayed by Pakistan," providing the first third-party validation that a communication channel exists, even as both principals characterize it differently. The gap between positions — US wants nuclear rollback and Hormuz reopening; Iran demands sovereignty and reparations — is enormous. Emily Harding (CSIS) calls it "unbridgeable in 10 days."

### Trump extended the energy strike pause to April 6 — claiming "Iranian government request" that Iran denies — buying time but no visible progress.

Two days before the March 28 deadline, Trump pushed the energy infrastructure strike pause by 10 days, posting on Truth Social: "As per Iranian Government request, please let this statement serve to represent that I am pausing the period of Energy Plant destruction. Talks are ongoing and they are going very well" (sources: CNBC, Axios, NPR, Al Jazeera, Bloomberg). Iran immediately denied making any request or being in talks. The parliament speaker called it "fakenews used to manipulate oil markets." The contradiction continues: Trump claims progress, Iran claims zero contact. But Pakistan's confirmed message relay means SOME channel exists, even if both sides characterize it as something other than "talks." The extension is itself a signal — someone requested or agreed to it, and neither side wanted to be the one to let the deadline lapse.

### Iran formalized a yuan-denominated "toll booth" at the Strait of Hormuz — transforming the blockade from military closure to a monetized chokepoint.

The IRGC has created a structured toll system for vessels seeking passage through the Strait. Ships must submit full details (cargo, owners, destination, crew list) to IRGC intermediaries. Approved vessels receive a code and armed escort through Iranian territorial waters around Larak Island. At least two vessels have paid tolls of approximately $2 million each, settled in Chinese yuan (source: USNI News, Fortune citing Lloyd's data). Lloyd's List Intelligence tracked 26 ships using this system since March 13. Total transits March 1-25: 142 — versus 2,652 in the same period of 2025, a 95% reduction (source: Lloyd's List Intelligence). This is no longer just a military blockade — it is a formalized revenue-generating chokepoint under IRGC control, with yuan as the settlement currency, effectively displacing the dollar from the world's most critical oil transit route.

### India enacted emergency fuel duty cuts as the rupee hit 94.85 and FPIs pulled $11.5 billion in March alone.

India slashed petrol duties from ₹13 to ₹3 per liter and completely removed the ₹10/liter diesel duty on March 27 — the most aggressive fuel tax intervention since the 2022 Russia-Ukraine crisis (source: Al Jazeera). The rupee hit an intraday low of 94.85/USD (source: Business Standard), down over 4% since the war began. FPIs have withdrawn ₹88,180 crore ($11.5 billion) in March; 2026 outflows have crossed ₹1 trillion. Forex reserves dropped $7 billion to $709.75 billion. The RBI is intervening with a net short dollar position approaching $100 billion — effectively betting the crisis resolves before reserves run out. Bernstein warned that if the conflict lasts through 2026: double-digit inflation, growth slipping to 2-3%, rupee beyond 110, and Nifty below 20,000.

### The Signal You Might Miss
While media focused on the dramatic Houthi missile (dramatic, visual, easy to report) and the Tangsiri assassination (dramatic, personal), the more structurally significant development is the IRGC's formalization of the yuan-denominated toll booth at Hormuz. This transforms the crisis from a temporary military blockade into a **permanent toll-collecting regime** that Iran has financial incentive to maintain even after any ceasefire. If Iran keeps the toll system as part of its "sovereignty over Hormuz" demand — which is literally condition #5 of their counterproposal — the Strait doesn't return to pre-war normalcy even if fighting stops. The yuan settlement is a dollar-displacement signal that goes beyond this crisis. The graph doesn't yet have an edge for this mechanism separately from the military blockade, but it should — because the toll regime may outlast the war.
