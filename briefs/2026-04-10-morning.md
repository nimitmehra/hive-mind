## I. What Happened

### The ceasefire failed its first operational test — three Chinese VLCCs halted at the Strait of Hormuz, the IRGC toll system remains in force, and stranded vessel counts were revised sharply upward to 800-3,000.

The most important test of the ceasefire was not diplomatic — it was operational: could commercial ships actually transit the Strait of Hormuz? The answer, as of this morning, is no. Three Chinese VLCCs — Cospearl Lake (Cosco, ~2 million barrels of Iraqi crude), He Rong Hai (Saudi crude), and Yuan Hua Hu — sailed eastward at near-top speed on April 9 morning before halting at the strait's entrance. All were broadcasting Chinese ownership on AIS tracking — a protocol typically used during IRGC-approved transits — and NONE completed passage (source: Bloomberg AIS data, Rigzone, Taipei Times). Total traffic April 8-10: approximately 9 vessels across three days vs 100+/day pre-war, or roughly 3% of normal capacity (source: S&P Global, NBC News). More significantly, the stranded vessel count was revised sharply upward: Bloomberg reports 800+ vessels trapped in the Gulf, the IMO estimates 2,000, and Press TV/Global Security cites 3,000 with 94% traffic reduction — all far higher than the 479 previously reported from a subset tracker. An estimated 20,000+ civilian seafarers are stranded. NBC News: "Strait of Hormuz shipping traffic effectively at a standstill despite Iran ceasefire." CNBC: "Tanker traffic may take months to recover." The ceasefire text promises "safe passage within technical limitations" — but Iran's "technical limitations" interpretation (IRGC permit required per ship, toll fees, naval escort) is operationally indistinguishable from the pre-ceasefire regime.

### Crisis-beneficiary stocks are in freefall — CF Industries -5.4%, Frontline -3.7%, VIX crashed to 19.49 — while oil keeps rising and physical crude trades $24-44 above futures, creating the most dangerous split-personality market of the war.

The market is simultaneously saying two contradictory things. On one hand: CF Industries fell 5.4% (its war premium unwinding), Frontline tankers -3.7%, Cheniere LNG -3.4%, Nutrien -2.8%, and VIX collapsed 7.4% to 19.49 — its lowest since pre-war February, pricing in ceasefire success (source: yfinance confirmed). On the other hand: WTI crude rose to $98.06 (+0.2%), Brent to $96.23 (+0.3%), and physical Dated Brent trades above $120 while Dubai physical crude sits at $126-140, creating a $24-44 futures-physical gap that is the widest since 2008 (source: CNBC, S&P Global). The WTI-Brent spread has INVERTED for the first time since the war — WTI at $98.06 now trades $2 above Brent at $96.23, when in March Brent averaged $12 above WTI. This inversion is the market's clearest signal: it believes Hormuz normalises eventually (deflating Brent's global supply disruption premium) but the US inflation damage from six weeks of $100+ oil is already baked in (keeping WTI elevated). VIX at 19.49 ahead of today's double binary (CPI + Islamabad) is the most extreme volatility mispricing of the war — the market has no protective hedging for either event, meaning any negative surprise amplifies 2-3x.

### March CPI consensus tightened to 3.4% — the first war-inflation print releases tonight at 6 PM IST, and a hot number kills the June rate cut narrative in both Washington and Mumbai.

The US March Consumer Price Index releases today at 8:30 AM ET (6:00 PM IST), and the consensus has hardened to 3.4% year-over-year headline with a 0.9% monthly increase — the highest since April 2024 and the first print reflecting March's $100-119 Brent range (source: FactSet, Morningstar, Kiplinger). The Fed already raised its 2026 inflation forecast from 2.4% to 2.7% at the March 18 meeting — the largest single-session revision in recent cycles — and 7 of 19 FOMC participants now see zero cuts for 2026 (source: Yahoo Finance, Fed minutes). Glenmede estimates oil could add approximately 0.8% to inflation over the next year (source: Kiplinger). A hot print (above 3.4%) would kill the June rate cut narrative that the RBI's dovish guidance is built on — if the Fed can't cut, Governor Malhotra's "interest rates likely to remain low in the medium to long term" becomes wishful thinking regardless of domestic conditions.
