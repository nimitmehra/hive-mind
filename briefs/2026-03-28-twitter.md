I. What Happened

Houthis entered the war with a ballistic missile strike on Israel — ending 28 days of rhetoric-only posture.

Yemen's Houthis launched a ballistic missile at southern Israel on Saturday morning, triggering air raid sirens across Beersheba and the Negev. The IDF confirmed the missile was intercepted with no casualties. Houthi military spokesman Yahya Saree claimed responsibility via Al-Masirah TV, calling it a strike on "sensitive Israeli military sites." CENTCOM struck Houthi positions in Yemen in response.

After 28 consecutive days of escalating threats and zero action — a posture analysts called "generating value without expenditure" — the Houthis have crossed from threat to force.

The critical distinction: this was a missile at Israel, not a Red Sea shipping attack. The double-chokepoint scenario (Hormuz + Red Sea both closed) has not materialized, but it is now one step away. Michael Knights (Washington Institute) assesses this as a "calibration shot" — testing the response before escalating to shipping attacks, likely spaced 5-10 days apart.

Sources: IDF statement, Al Jazeera, Times of Israel, CNN, Jerusalem Post, Al-Masirah TV

---

Israel assassinated the IRGC Navy chief overseeing the Hormuz blockade — the highest-ranking kill since the war began.

Israeli forces killed IRGC Navy commander Rear Admiral Alireza Tangsiri in a precision airstrike at 3:00 AM on March 26 in Bandar Abbas — the port city from which he was personally directing the Strait of Hormuz closure. The IDF and CENTCOM both confirmed the kill. Netanyahu stated: "We eliminated the commander who led the closure of Hormuz."

A US admiral issued a public message to remaining IRGC Navy personnel: "Abandon your posts or die."

Iran has NOT officially confirmed Tangsiri's death — a notable silence, as Tehran typically denies false claims within hours. This is the highest-ranking Iranian military figure killed since the February 28 strikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei.

The strategic question: does decapitating the Hormuz commander disrupt IRGC naval operations, or harden resolve? Karim Sadjadpour (Carnegie) argues the latter — "decapitation strikes create martyrs, not compliance."

Sources: IDF, CENTCOM, Al Jazeera, Haaretz, Jerusalem Post, Euronews, Gulf News

---

Iran rejected the US 15-point ceasefire plan and issued a maximalist counterproposal.

Iran's FM Araghchi publicly rejected the US plan on March 25, calling it "extremely maximalist and unreasonable." He issued a 5-point counterproposal:

1. End all "aggression"
2. Guarantee the war will not recur
3. Pay war reparations
4. End hostilities on all fronts, including all resistance groups
5. Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz

Most significantly, Araghchi stated on state TV: "We do not want a ceasefire. We want the war to end in a way that it does not repeat, on our own terms."

Pakistan's FM Ishaq Dar publicly confirmed: "US-Iran indirect talks are taking place through messages being relayed by Pakistan" — the first third-party validation that a communication channel exists.

The gap between positions — US wants nuclear rollback + Hormuz reopening; Iran demands sovereignty + reparations — is enormous.

Sources: NPR, CNBC, PBS, Al Jazeera, The Hill, Pakistan FM statement

Trump extended the energy strike pause to April 6 — claiming Iran requested it. Iran denies.

Two days before the March 28 deadline, Trump extended the pause by 10 days, posting on Truth Social: "As per Iranian Government request... Talks are ongoing and they are going very well."

Iran immediately denied making any request. The parliament speaker called it "fakenews used to manipulate oil markets."

The contradiction continues: Trump claims progress, Iran claims zero contact. But Pakistan's confirmed message relay means SOME channel exists. The extension itself is a signal — someone requested or agreed to it, and neither side wanted to be the one to let the deadline lapse.

Sources: CNBC, Axios, NPR, Al Jazeera, Bloomberg

Iran formalized a yuan-denominated "toll booth" at the Strait of Hormuz.

The IRGC has created a structured toll system for vessels seeking passage. Ships must submit full details (cargo, owners, destination, crew list) to IRGC intermediaries. Approved vessels receive a code and armed escort through Iranian waters around Larak Island.

Key facts:
- At least 2 vessels paid ~$2 million each, settled in Chinese yuan
- 26 ships have used this system since March 13
- Total transits March 1-25: 142 vs 2,652 in same period 2025 — a 95% reduction
- Ships from China, India, Malaysia, Egypt, South Korea allowed through

This is no longer just a military blockade. It is a formalized, revenue-generating chokepoint under IRGC control, with yuan displacing the dollar on the world's most critical oil transit route.

Sources: USNI News, Fortune, Foreign Policy, Al Jazeera, NBC News, Lloyd's List Intelligence

India enacted emergency fuel duty cuts as the rupee hit 94.85 and FPIs pulled $11.5 billion in March.

India slashed petrol duties from 13 rupees to 3 rupees per liter and completely removed the 10-rupee diesel duty on March 27 — the most aggressive fuel intervention since 2022.

The damage in numbers:
- Rupee hit intraday low of 94.85/USD on March 27 (down 4%+ since war began)
- FPIs withdrew 88,180 crore rupees ($11.5 billion) in March; 2026 outflows crossed 1 trillion rupees
- Forex reserves dropped $7 billion to $709.75 billion
- RBI net short dollar position approaching $100 billion
- Bond yields at 2-year high

Bernstein warned: if the conflict lasts through 2026, India faces double-digit inflation, growth slipping to 2-3%, rupee beyond 110, and Nifty below 20,000.

Sources: Al Jazeera, Business Standard, RBI data
