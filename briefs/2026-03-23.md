## I. What Happened

### Trump blinked: 48-hour ultimatum on Iran's power grid expired without action, replaced by a 5-day "pause" and claims of talks that Iran flatly denies.
On Saturday, Trump threatened to "obliterate" Iran's power plants unless Tehran reopened the Strait of Hormuz within 48 hours — the most aggressive civilian infrastructure threat of the war (NBC News, Al Jazeera). By Monday morning, without explanation, he posted on Truth Social that the US had held "very good and productive conversations regarding a complete and total resolution of our hostilities" and announced a 5-day postponement of all strikes on Iranian energy sites (Bloomberg, Fox News, CNN). Iran's Foreign Ministry immediately denied any contact. Fars and Tasnim — both IRGC-linked agencies — confirmed "zero direct or indirect talks with Washington," calling Trump's claim "psychological warfare designed to project strength while retreating" (IRNA, Fars News, Anadolu Agency). The deadline passing without strikes is itself the signal: the US faces real constraints on further escalation, likely driven by oil above $110, surging Treasury yields, and bipartisan Congressional resistance to the $200B war budget.

### Asia markets were crushed by the weekend escalation threats — before Trump's Monday reversal reached them.
A brutal time-zone divergence: Asian markets opened Monday reacting to Trump's Saturday power plant threats, not his Monday morning de-escalation. South Korea's KOSPI crashed 6.5% — briefly triggering a circuit breaker — driven by a "triple shock" of surging oil, won weakness (1,517/USD), and evaporating rate cut hopes (Seoul Economic Daily, CNBC). Japan's Nikkei fell 3.5%, with chip stocks Renesas and Lasertec the worst performers as the helium shortage narrative hit semiconductor sentiment (Japan Times). Shanghai and Hang Seng both dropped 3.5%. India's Sensex fell 1,837 points (-2.5%) and Nifty hit 22,513 — the lowest since April 2025 — wiping ₹14 trillion in market cap in a single session (Business Standard, India TV). By contrast, US markets opened after Trump's de-escalation and rallied: S&P 500 +2.2%, NASDAQ +1.8% (24/7 Wall Street).

### PM Modi addressed parliament on the crisis, framing it as India's biggest economic challenge since the pandemic.
Modi told the Lok Sabha that crude oil, gas, and fertilizers reach India through the Strait of Hormuz where "vessel movement has become increasingly difficult," and that India imports nearly 60% of its LPG from the Gulf (DD News, India TV, PMO). He announced a Group of Ministers would coordinate a "whole-of-government approach" across energy, agriculture, fertilizers, shipping, and finance. Over 3 lakh Indians have now returned from the Gulf. The government is absorbing some of the oil price shock — petrol and diesel prices remain unchanged — but LPG has already risen ₹60/cylinder. The unspoken question: how long can the subsidy buffer hold if oil stays above $100?

### The helium-to-chips cascade is real: South Korea most exposed, with 65% of helium imports from Qatar now offline.
Fitch flagged South Korea and Taiwan's chip sectors as "most exposed" to the helium shortage — South Korea sourced 64.7% of its helium from Qatar's Ras Laffan, now offline (South China Morning Post, Fitch). SK Hynix has been forced to diversify suppliers on a "two-week clock" (Tom's Hardware). Helium spot prices have doubled since the conflict began. TSMC says "no significant impact at this time" but is monitoring (Fortune). The cascade chain: **Iran strikes Qatar → Ras Laffan offline → 33% global helium gone → semiconductor wafer cooling disrupted → chip production delays → KOSPI down 6.5%, Taiwan TAIEX down 2.4%, SMH ETF volatile.** This is a 4th-order war effect that no newspaper covers as a connected chain.

### Gold's counter-intuitive collapse continued: down 3.2% today, -12.5% in a month, worst rout since 2011.
Gold fell to $4,425, approaching its 2026 low — the opposite of what every traditional playbook predicts during a war (GoldSilver.com, Yahoo Finance). The mechanism: oil spike → inflation expectations → Fed forced hawkish → dollar strengthens → gold crushed. The DXY remains firm at 99.35. JP Morgan still targets $6,300 year-end, but the near-term trade is broken. This tells you something important: the market believes the Fed will prioritize inflation-fighting over everything else, even with a war raging. Bonds, not gold, are the fear trade.

**The time-zone divergence is a structural feature, not a one-off.** Asian markets consistently absorb the weekend's bad news on Monday morning, while US markets get to react to the Monday morning revision. This means Asian investors — including Indian — systematically bear more downside from this conflict's news cycle. If Trump's pattern of "threaten on weekends, walk back on Monday" continues, Asian markets will keep getting whipsawed while US markets capture the relief rallies. For an Indian equity investor, this creates an asymmetric timing disadvantage.
