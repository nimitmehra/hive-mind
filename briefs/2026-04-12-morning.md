## I. What Happened

### The Islamabad talks — the first US-Iran face-to-face since 1979 — ran 21 hours and ended with no deal, leaving a "final and best offer" as the only residue of the most ambitious diplomatic effort of the war.

VP Vance led a ~300-member US delegation (with Witkoff and Kushner) against Iran's ~70-member team headed by Speaker Ghalibaf and FM Araghchi. The format evolved during the session: initial proximity talks with Pakistan shuttling between rooms began Saturday afternoon, then upgraded to trilateral face-to-face meetings from roughly 5 PM — putting US and Iranian principals in the same room for the first time since 1979 (source: CBS News, CNBC, Al Jazeera, NPR — all confirmed). The core impasse was nuclear: Vance demanded "an affirmative commitment that Iran will not seek a nuclear weapon — not just now, not just two years from now, but for the long term." Iran refused this formulation, with spokesperson Baqaei framing success as contingent on "the acceptance of Iran's legitimate rights and interests." The Arms Control Association published pre-talks analysis arguing US negotiators were "ill-prepared for serious nuclear talks" — Witkoff lacked technical expertise and his characterisation of Iran's positions "likely informed Trump's assessment that talks were not progressing" (source: ACA). Vance departed saying "we leave here with a very simple proposal, a method of understanding that is our final and best offer." The two-week ceasefire (April 8-22) remains technically in effect despite the no-deal, and Pakistan FM Dar called the 21-hour session "intense and constructive" — but no second round is scheduled and the clock is running on 10 remaining ceasefire days.

### The US Navy entered the Strait of Hormuz for the first time since the war began — launching a mine clearing operation while Islamabad talks were still underway 2,000 miles away — in a unilateral move to break Iran's physical control of the strait.

CENTCOM confirmed that the guided-missile destroyers USS Frank E. Peterson and USS Michael Murphy transited the strait on Saturday as part of a mine clearance mission. Admiral Brad Cooper stated: "Today, we began the process of establishing a new passage, and we will share this safe pathway with the maritime industry soon to encourage the free flow of commerce" (source: CENTCOM, Naval News, Al Jazeera — confirmed). Underwater drones will join in coming days. The accounts of what happened next diverge sharply: CENTCOM and US officials (via WSJ, Axios) say both ships completed the full transit. Iran's state TV reported the IRGC issued a 30-minute warning threatening attack, and that the USS Michael Murphy "turned back." A regional intelligence official told Bloomberg both destroyers were "forced to turn back" after the IRGC launched a UAV in their direction. Iran's government called the crossing a ceasefire violation (source: Press TV, Fortune, Bloomberg — conflicting accounts). The truth matters: if the US successfully transited, it breaks the narrative of IRGC physical control; if the destroyers turned back, IRGC control is reinforced even against the US Navy. Regardless of the confrontation's outcome, the mine clearing mission represents a strategic shift from waiting for diplomacy to resolve Hormuz to unilaterally creating a commercial corridor — the most significant development at the strait since the IRGC toll system was established March 27.
