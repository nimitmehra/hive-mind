## I. What Happened

### The US-Iran exchange escalated into a second, larger round — 49 Tomahawks on Iran, a second Iranian wave on three Gulf/Levant states, and a "bigger" third round threatened for tonight — but the kinetics stayed calibrated for a third straight iteration.
After Iran's first-wave strikes covered in Tuesday's brief, CENTCOM struck Iran in three waves overnight 10-11 June — Trump put it at 49 Tomahawks, some landing within 40 miles of Tehran, and for the first time the target set widened into energy infrastructure with a hit on a South Pars petrochemical plant at Asalouyeh (CONFIRMED; CENTCOM, Haaretz live). Iran answered with a second wave of 12 ballistic missiles at US-hosting bases in Bahrain, Kuwait and Jordan — all intercepted, near-nil damage (Jordan reported 20 missiles downed, "no casualties or material damage"). The escalation is real in *tempo* — the US has moved from a one-off "warning shot" on 9 June to nightly multi-wave packages, with Defence Secretary Hegseth declaring at MacDill that the US would "negotiate with bombs" — but the *effect* remains choreographed: a third consecutive zero-fatality round, Iran still not striking Israel, and a third "bigger, more powerful" US round threatened for tonight that remains rhetoric until it lands. The widening into Iranian energy targets is the one genuinely new escalatory vector to watch.

### Three Indian sailors were killed by a US strike off Oman — the war's first Indian fatalities, at American hands — and Delhi's response marks the sharpest pivot in its US lane in weeks.
A US strike disabled the Palau-flagged MT Settebello off the Oman coast on 10 June; three Indian crew — deck cadet Aditya Sharma, fitter Shivanand Chaurasiya and chief engineer Patnala Suresh — were confirmed dead on 11 June, with 21 of 24 rescued (CONFIRMED; CBS, Zee News, BusinessToday). CENTCOM said the tanker was moving Iranian oil in breach of the blockade and "repeatedly failed to comply." India summoned the US Chargé d'Affaires and lodged a "strong protest," with the MEA's Randhir Jaiswal saying "these attacks must cease and end" — unusually imperative language for the India-US relationship. This sits inside a now-routinised enforcement campaign (9 strikes on non-compliant tankers, 135 redirected, 42 humanitarian passages since 13 April; two more tankers hit this week), but the deaths open a *non-market* channel of India exposure: domestic political pressure over Indian seafarers killed by US fire, three weeks before the Section 301 tariff comment deadline, with the Indian press swinging from market-mechanics to "India vs America."

### US May CPI printed 4.2% — a three-year high — locking in the "rates-not-war" engine, knocking the Dow down 953 points, and pushing the VIX to its first close above 22 of the entire war.
May CPI rose 0.5% month-on-month and 4.2% year-on-year, the fastest annual pace since April 2023, with energy (war-derived, via the Hormuz blockade) contributing roughly 60% of the monthly increase (CONFIRMED; BLS via Yahoo Finance, CBS). US equities sold off into the 10 June close — S&P −1.62% to 7,266.99, Dow −1.87% (−953 points), Nasdaq −1.98% — and the VIX closed 22.22, finally crossing the >22 line the briefs have tracked for weeks. The diagnostic detail is gold: it fell *again*, toward $4,109, on the same day the US and Iran traded strikes — the war-risk bid is dead, and this is now confirmed as an inflation event, not a risk-off. For the investor this is the regime fact: the crisis has migrated from the fast, reversible war-premium channel into the slow, sticky CPI/Fed channel. A December Fed *hike* is now priced as a live base-risk, and — critically — even an Iran deal tomorrow does not un-print May's 4.2%.

### Iran formally declared the "complete closure" of Hormuz — but this is a policy act, not a confirmed operational change, and CENTCOM publicly disputes it.
Iran's top joint military command announced the Strait "closed until further notice" for oil tankers and commercial vessels, warning all traffic "would be shot at" with no nationality exceptions — formally ending the selective, coordinated-passage regime in place since April, including the permitted Chinese transits (CONFIRMED announcement; Al Jazeera, Haaretz, NBC). The IRGC claimed it struck two transiting tankers, but there is no UKMTO or independent corroboration (CLAIMED), and CENTCOM countered that commercial ships continue to transit — a direct contradiction, with each side holding the incentive to shade it. The operative truth: the Strait was already running at roughly 5% of normal flow, so "complete closure" changes the *policy perimeter* more than the observed throughput. The market read it as choreography — Brent rose just 0.7% to about $93.78 through the declaration plus a petrochemical-plant strike, and European TTF gas moved half a percent. What is *not* priced is enforcement at scale: an operationally-confirmed strike on a neutral hull would gap Brent violently toward $110.

### Aluminium reversed back toward $3,600 as the closure formalised — validating Tuesday's call that its 9.4% drop was the most reversible move on the board.
The one commodity carrying a genuine *physical* war premium did exactly what the graph flagged: after the −2.9% LME close on 10 June (not the −6.6% data artifact — the third such aluminium misprint of the cycle), prices recovered toward $3,600 on 11 June as Iran formalised the closure (CONFIRMED, ±2pt contract caveat; AlCircle, TradingEconomics). The mechanism is the point — because the Gulf supply loss is *real* (EGA's flagship smelter facing up to a year to restart, ALBA partially suspended, Wood Mackenzie projecting up to 3.5m tonnes of lost 2026 output), closure-formalisation re-prices aluminium upward where gold, which has no real premium, cannot bid. It is the cleanest illustration in the complex of which "war premium" is choreography (gold, Brent, VIX) and which is structural (aluminium, war-risk insurance, VLCC rates).

### India's market resilience thinned but held — and the rupee re-approaching ₹96 has quietly un-ticked the one deployment box that was green yesterday.
The Nifty closed 23,161.60 (−0.23%) and the Sensex 73,832.55 (−0.20%) on 11 June — a shallow drift, not a break, through the second US-Iran exchange and the Hormuz declaration, with IT the main drag (Infosys −2.25%) on US-recession and CPI worry (CONFIRMED; Business Standard). But the deployment math worsened at the margin: FIIs sold ₹2,125cr on Wednesday (the net-positive-for-three-sessions condition remains unmet and is not close — May was a record ₹55,963cr outflow month), and the rupee weakened to about 95.79 from 95.25, eroding the sub-₹96 cushion that Tuesday's brief counted as the single green box. The deployment scorecard now reads one of three met (Brent under $95, barely) — the crisis is still arriving in India as global risk-off, not as rotation *into* it.

### Trump posted that the US "will be taking Kharg Island… and assume total control of their Oil and Gas Markets" — then walked it back within hours.
In an 11 June Truth Social post Trump wrote that the US would, "in the not too distant future," seize Kharg Island — which handles roughly 90% of Iran's crude exports — and take "total control of their Oil and Gas Markets, much like we have with Venezuela" (CONFIRMED statement; CNBC, The Hill, NBC). Hours later, on Fox, he was "not sure America has the stomach" for it, while confirming talks with Iran continue. This is the strongest *rhetorical* signal the graph's Kharg-seizure trigger family has received — the President naming the objective in writing — but the same-cycle walkback and the complete absence of any force-posture or amphibious buildup keep it firmly in the rhetoric column. The trigger's 30-day window closes 15 June; it is being watched daily through the weekend.

### Continuing threads.
**Lebanon:** the Tyre death toll from the 9 June Israeli strikes rose to 11, the IDF killed a gunman who infiltrated from Lebanon, and raids continued at a dozen-plus southern sites — the theatre grinds on *inside* the wider stand-down, with no new Hezbollah rocket fire found this window. **The Israel-Iran axis held:** no strikes in either direction since the 8 June stand-down, Trump confirmed Israel was not involved in the US strikes, and an Israeli official complained Washington demands restraint "noting Trump didn't hold back" himself — the decoupling is now openly chafing. **Houthis:** missiles at Israel during the 7-8 June window (intercepted) and a declared "complete and total ban on Israeli maritime navigation" — but still zero confirmed attacks on neutral shipping on Day 104, the oil-market wildcard that stays unfired. **Pakistan:** the 7 June letter to Tehran is now sourced to PM Sharif *and* Field Marshal Munir jointly, the military co-signature the only new detail; no Iranian readout.

### Wood Mackenzie (Charvi Trivedi) — commodities research, aluminium supply

### Macro / rates desks — the December-hike repricing
