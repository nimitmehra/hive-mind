## I. What Happened

### The physical oil market just revealed the de-escalation trade's blind spot — actual barrels of Dated Brent traded at $144 last week, a $49 premium over futures that makes every position built on "Brent below $100" suspect.

Dated Brent — the price paid for physical barrels loaded onto tankers — hit an all-time high above $144/barrel approximately April 7-11, according to Al Jazeera citing Platts and Argus assessments. With Brent futures at $95.21 on April 15, the physical-futures gap has exploded to approximately $49 — more than double the $22 estimated in Monday's brief and the widest divergence ever recorded in this conflict (sources: Al Jazeera citing Platts/Argus, CNBC — REPORTED, as Platts/Argus assessments are paywalled). Morgan Stanley described it as a "much more violent shock in physical Brent-linked barrels compared to the main financial contract." The gap reveals a three-layer mispricing: physical market at $120-144 (what refiners and tanker operators actually pay), futures at $95 (what Wall Street trades), and equity-implied Brent at roughly $85-90 (based on the energy sector selloff — XLE -2.0% while S&P hit a new post-war high at 6,967). These three layers cannot all be right. If the physical market is right, every position built on the futures narrative — S&P erasing all war losses, VIX at 18.36 (its lowest since the war began), Goldman's Q2 forecast — is mispriced by $49/barrel of unpriced risk. The $144 figure represents a peak from "last week" and current levels may have retreated, but even at $120 the gap dwarfs any previous divergence in this crisis. Physical and futures always reconverge — the question is which one moves.

### Rubio hosted the first direct Israel-Lebanon talks since 1993 and walked out with a joint statement — but Hezbollah's pre-emptive rejection defines the ceiling of what diplomacy can deliver without Iranian buy-in.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio personally hosted trilateral talks at the State Department on April 14-15, producing a joint statement in which all parties "agreed to launch direct negotiations at a mutually agreed time and venue" and stated the process could curb Iran's influence and "unlock significant reconstruction assistance and economic recovery for Lebanon" — the first direct Israel-Lebanon diplomatic engagement in 33 years (sources: US State Department joint statement, Axios, Washington Times, CBC News — CONFIRMED). Rubio framed expectations carefully: "This is a process, not a single event." Israel Hayom called it "historic" while Japan Times noted "slim odds of breakthrough." But before the talks began, Hezbollah's Naim Qassem declared them "futile" and stated Hezbollah "will not abide by any agreements" — the talks proceeded without Lebanon's most powerful armed group at the table (sources: Al Jazeera, Euronews — CONFIRMED). The gap between Lebanese state diplomacy and Hezbollah reality is the core constraint. The Iran connection gives the talks broader significance: Ghalibaf named a Lebanon ceasefire as one of Iran's preconditions at Islamabad, meaning progress here — even symbolic — could unlock the stalled US-Iran track, and the administration may be pursuing it precisely as a face-saving deliverable if the broader Iran negotiation fails.

### India's energy supply is now squeezed from three directions simultaneously — the Russian oil waiver expired four days ago, the Iran waiver expires in four days, and the Hormuz blockade blocks what remains.

CNBC reported what may be the most significant India-specific development of the week: the US sanctions waiver for Russian oil purchases expired on April 11 — before the Hormuz blockade even took effect — meaning India can no longer buy from its largest alternative crude source without risking secondary sanctions (sources: CNBC, Atlantic Council — CONFIRMED). The Iran sanctions waiver expires April 19, four days from now. Combined with the physical disruption of the blockade, India faces a triple squeeze on an import base of ~5.5 million barrels per day, approximately 85% of consumption, with strategic reserves at roughly 160 million barrels — only 30 days of buffer (sources: BusinessToday, Bloomberg, Kashmir Observer — CONFIRMED). India had begun receiving Iranian crude for the first time since 2019 (tankers Felicity at Sikka and Jaya at Paradip, ~4 million barrels total), and Reliance was seeking government approval for four more Iranian vessels — a strategy the approaching waiver expiry now threatens. Indian markets, reopening after Monday's Ambedkar Jayanti holiday, rallied into the squeeze: Nifty +1.5% to 24,194, crossing above its 200-day moving average for the first time since April 10, with the rupee strengthening 1.8% to Rs93.24 — the first session since ceasefire day where both Nifty and INR moved in the same direction. But FII outflows continued at Rs 1.79 lakh crore for CY2026, meaning the rally remains DII-funded and structurally fragile. S&P Global Ratings assessed India's "strong fundamentals" would cushion the shock but growth may slow up to 80 basis points.

### CENTCOM declared the blockade has "completely halted" Iranian sea trade in under 36 hours — shipping tracker data from the same period says otherwise, and the gap between the claim and reality is now the blockade's defining feature.

Admiral Brad Cooper, CENTCOM Commander, stated the blockade "completely halted economic trade going into and out of Iran by sea in less than 36 hours," with 10,000 personnel, 15+ warships, and dozens of aircraft deployed, and that six merchant vessels complied with direction to turn around (sources: CENTCOM official statement, ABC News — CONFIRMED claim made). Al Jazeera, citing Kpler shipping data, confirmed at least three vessels including two US-sanctioned tankers entered the Gulf on the blockade's first full day; Bloomberg corroborated through the same data source (sources: Al Jazeera, Bloomberg, Kpler — CONFIRMED transits). Iran's IRGC maintained maximum rhetoric through Tasnim — threats of "deadly whirlpools" and "all traffic under full control of armed forces" — but Day 2-3 has produced zero confirmed kinetic actions: no mining, no attacks on US vessels, no confrontation despite the language (sources: Tasnim, ISW — CONFIRMED non-action). The blockade's character is now clear: it deters legitimate commercial shipping while allowing sanctioned and shadow-fleet vessels through — a perverse outcome that strangles legal trade while Iran's illicit oil network continues, paradoxically undermining blockade credibility while delivering economic pain through deterrence rather than interception. The restraint on both sides — the US not interdicting the Elpis or Rich Starry, the IRGC not confronting the blockade force — suggests neither wants the first shot.

### Britain and France set April 18 for a 40-country conference on an independent Hormuz escort mission — the concept has moved from announcement to calendar event with a confirmed roster.

The UK-France multinational Hormuz initiative advanced materially from Monday's "conference in coming days" to a specific date: April 18, video conference hosted from Paris, with confirmed participants including France, the Netherlands, Germany, Italy, Canada, Australia, and the UAE — over 40 countries in total (sources: Macron statement, Republic World/ANI, Al Jazeera, Euronews — CONFIRMED). The US is explicitly excluded. Macron described the mission as "peaceful, multinational, strictly defensive" aimed at "restoring freedom of navigation." If the April 18 conference produces an actual naval escort commitment with operational capability, it could provide the insurance framework and military protection needed for commercial shipping to resume — a third pathway between the US blockade and Iranian selective control that the market has not priced. This matters because the physical oil market ($120-144 Dated Brent) is being set by insurance costs ($10-14M per VLCC transit) and safety concerns, not by military confrontation — a multinational escort that reduces insurance premiums directly addresses the binding constraint. Iran has not publicly responded to the initiative; the US was not invited (sources: CNBC, NPR — CONFIRMED for conference; operational naval escort remains at planning stage).

### The Iranian government's war damage has been estimated at $300 billion to $1 trillion, with the blockade accelerating the economic collapse at $435 million per day in lost oil revenue.

The Iranian government's damage assessment puts the economic cost of the war at $300 billion to $1 trillion as of approximately April 11, per reporting compiled by Richard Haass (formerly Council on Foreign Relations) and Britannica's conflict summary (sources: Haass Substack April 10, Britannica — REPORTED). Tehran consumer prices are up approximately 40% since war onset; the rial has fallen 8% on black markets. The blockade adds approximately $435 million per day in lost oil revenue, with 90% of Iran's economy dependent on sea trade per CENTCOM's assessment (sources: BusinessToday citing Robin Brooks/Brookings — REPORTED). Hyperinflation dynamics are emerging. FM Araghchi's X post captured Iran's framing: "In intensive talks at highest level in 47 years, Iran engaged with US in good faith to end war. But when just inches away from 'Islamabad MoU', we encountered maximalism, shifting goalposts, and blockade." The economic pressure is the leverage the blockade is designed to create — but whether it produces concessions or hardens resolve remains the unanswered question. The nuclear gap (5-year Iranian offer versus 20-year US demand on enrichment moratorium) and the Hormuz question (Iranian control versus US-demanded reopening) are the two substantive obstacles, and both sides' positions are now publicly known, constraining concessions (sources: Araghchi X post — CONFIRMED statement; damage estimates — REPORTED from government sources).
